For two thousand years every astronomer knew planets move in circles. Johannes Kepler, armed with Tycho Brahe's twenty years of naked-eye Mars observations and no telescope, spent five years at war with a discrepancy the width of a clock minute-hand seen from across a room — and refused to round it away. Chapter 1 · Antiquity – 1600 · The problem

Mars, the star that wanders backwards

Retrograde loops · epicycles upon epicycles · Tycho's twenty years of 2-arcminute data · Kepler hired, 1600

The wandering star

Five "stars" refuse to stay put. Night after night they drift eastward through the constellations — until, every couple of years, Mars slows, stops, runs backwards for a few weeks, and then resumes course, tracing a loop against the fixed stars. The Greeks named these objects planētēs — wanderers — and the backwards stretch is retrograde motion. Any theory of the heavens stood or fell on explaining that loop.

Wheels upon wheels

Ptolemy's answer, good for fourteen centuries: if Earth sits still at the centre, then Mars must ride a small circle (an epicycle) whose centre rides a big circle (the deferent). Cycles within cycles reproduce the loops — at the price of machinery nobody could explain physically. In a Sun-centred system the loops need no machinery at all: Earth simply overtakes Mars on the inside track, and Mars appears to slide backwards exactly like a slower car seen from a faster one. The moment of overtaking — Sun, Earth, Mars in a line — is called opposition, and it will carry this whole story.

Tycho's twenty years

Theories were cheap; what was new was data. On his island observatory, Tycho Brahe spent two decades measuring planetary positions with giant naked-eye instruments to an accuracy of about 2 arcminutes — several times better than anyone before, and at the limit of the unaided human eye. Mars, with its big retrograde swings and visibly irregular pace, was both the best-observed and the most theory-resistant object in his ledgers.

Kepler's war on Mars

In 1600 Tycho hired the young Johannes Kepler and assigned him the Mars problem; Tycho died the next year and Kepler inherited the data. He expected the job to take eight days. It took more than five years — he called it "my war with Mars" — and the campaign log became Astronomia Nova (1609), one of the few great books of science that records the author's wrong turns as faithfully as the result. This companion follows that log, with the original woodcuts along the way.

Why "2 arcminutes" is the number that matters

An arcminute is 1/60 of a degree; the full Moon spans about 30 of them. Tycho's positions were good to roughly 2 — about the width of a coin seen from a kilometre away, achieved with sighting instruments and bare eyes. Hold that number: the entire revolution in this story happens because Kepler's best circular model missed two observations by 8 arcminutes, four times Tycho's error budget — a miss invisible to every astronomer before Tycho, and intolerable after him. Precision is what turned a philosophical preference into a falsifiable claim.

Practice problems

1. Earth orbits the Sun in $T_E=365.25$ days and Mars in $T_M=687$ days. The synodic period $S$ (the time between successive oppositions, when Earth laps Mars) satisfies $\dfrac{1}{S}=\dfrac{1}{T_E}-\dfrac{1}{T_M}$. Find $S$.

Show solution
  1. Subtract the two rates. $\dfrac{1}{S}=\dfrac{1}{365.25}-\dfrac{1}{687}=2.7378\times10^{-3}-1.4556\times10^{-3}=1.2822\times10^{-3}$ per day.
  2. Invert. $S=\dfrac{1}{1.2822\times10^{-3}}=779.9$ days.
Answer: $S\approx780$ days ($\approx2.14$ yr) — Mars goes into retrograde, and reaches opposition, once every $\sim780$ days, exactly the rhythm Tycho logged.

2. A retrograde loop happens once per synodic period. Tycho watched Mars for $20$ years. Using $S=779.9$ days, how many Mars retrograde episodes fell in that span?

Show solution
  1. Total days observed. $20\times365.25=7305$ days.
  2. Divide by the synodic period. $\dfrac{7305}{779.9}=9.37$.
Answer: about $9$ retrograde loops — roughly one every two years, which is why Mars was the richest planet in Tycho's twenty-year ledger.

3. In Mars's own clock, one orbit is $687$ days. Over $10$ Earth years ($3652.5$ days), how many complete laps of its orbit does Mars make?

Show solution
  1. Divide elapsed time by the Mars year. $\dfrac{3652.5}{687}=5.32$ Mars-years.
  2. Take the whole-number part. $\lfloor5.32\rfloor=5$ complete laps (plus a third of a sixth).
Answer: $5$ complete orbits — the slow outer planet falls steadily behind faster Earth, which is the very source of the apparent backward loops.
Chapter 2 · The method · Untying the observer

You can't map Mars until you know where you are

Every measurement is taken from a moving, imperfectly-known platform — and Kepler's first move was to make that not matter

Everything moves

Every one of Tycho's Mars positions is a direction as seen from Earth — a moving platform whose own orbit was only approximately known. The same problem Vera Rubin's pipeline solves with computed corrections (her Chapter 5) confronted Kepler with no computation to lean on: to place Mars in space you must know where Earth was at that instant, and nobody did, exactly.

The error you inherit

The danger is not that the error is large but that it is entangled: any mistake in Earth's assumed position leaks straight into Mars's computed one, and you cannot tell which body owns the error. Kepler's insight was strategic rather than computational: decouple the two measurements — find observing moments at which Earth's exact position becomes irrelevant.

Opposition: the alignment

Those moments are oppositions: Sun, Earth and Mars on one straight line (Earth in the middle), once every 780 days on average. On that line, the direction from Earth to Mars and the direction from the Sun to Mars are the same direction — the angle is shared, so Earth's position along the line drops out of the longitude entirely. Tycho's ledgers contained about a dozen such alignments across twenty years.

Observe from the Sun

So at opposition, Kepler could pretend the measurement was taken from the Sun — a fixed platform — at no cost in accuracy. One choice of observing geometry replaced an impossible correction. Everything that follows in this story is built exclusively from opposition data for exactly this reason, and the move foreshadows the modern habit: when you can't subtract your own motion, arrange to observe when it doesn't matter.

∴ Why opposition kills the Earth-position error

One picture, three steps — the cleanest trick in the whole campaign.

  1. The general case is hopeless. Off-alignment, the direction Earth→Mars and the direction Sun→Mars differ by a parallax angle that depends on exactly where Earth is. Wrong Earth ⇒ wrong Mars, with no way to apportion blame.
  2. On the alignment, the directions merge. If Sun, Earth and Mars are collinear, the lines Sun→Mars and Earth→Mars are the same line. Any point on it sees Mars in the same ecliptic longitude. Earth's position along the line — known or not — contributes nothing.
  3. Detecting the alignment needs no theory: at opposition Mars stands opposite the Sun in the sky (rising at sunset), sits at the midpoint of its retrograde loop, and is at its brightest. Tycho logged these moments directly; no model of either orbit is required to know you are in one.
The yield: a set of Mars longitudes as seen from the Sun — heliocentric data taken from a planet that never stops moving. Chapter 3 opens that dataset and immediately finds a scandal in it.

The coordinate system: ecliptic longitude and the zodiac

Tycho's positions are ecliptic longitudes: angles measured along the ecliptic, the Sun's apparent yearly path across the sky, which is also the plane of Earth's orbit. In Kepler's notation the circle is divided into the twelve 30° signs of the zodiac — Aries, Taurus, Gemini, Cancer, Leo, Virgo, Libra, Scorpius, Sagittarius, Capricornus, Aquarius, Pisces — so a position reads like "25°43′ into Virgo". The worked numbers (115.717°, 206.717°, …) are these same entries converted to decimal degrees measured from a single zero point. One subtlety worth knowing: a longitude is a direction, not a location — which is exactly why the triangulation of Chapter 5 will be needed to turn directions into places.

Practice problems

1. Oppositions repeat once per synodic period, $S=779.9$ days. Across Tycho's $20$-year campaign, how many Sun–Earth–Mars alignments could in principle have been logged?

Show solution
  1. Days in the campaign. $20\times365.25=7305$ days.
  2. Divide by the synodic period. $\dfrac{7305}{779.9}=9.37$.
Answer: about $9$ oppositions — consistent with the "roughly a dozen" alignments Kepler found in the ledgers, the only moments at which Earth's position drops out.

2. The opposition trick works for any outer planet. Jupiter's orbital period is $T_J=4332.6$ days. Using $\dfrac{1}{S}=\dfrac{1}{365.25}-\dfrac{1}{T_J}$, how often does Jupiter come to opposition?

Show solution
  1. Subtract the rates. $\dfrac{1}{S}=\dfrac{1}{365.25}-\dfrac{1}{4332.6}=2.7378\times10^{-3}-2.308\times10^{-4}=2.507\times10^{-3}$ per day.
  2. Invert. $S=\dfrac{1}{2.507\times10^{-3}}=398.9$ days.
Answer: $S\approx399$ days — only about a month longer than a year, because slow-moving Jupiter is nearly overtaken on each of Earth's laps.

3. Show that between two oppositions Earth gains exactly one full lap on Mars. Over one synodic period $S=779.9$ days, how many degrees does Earth sweep ($360°/365.25$ d) and how many does Mars sweep ($360°/687$ d)? Take the difference.

Show solution
  1. Earth's angle. $\dfrac{360°}{365.25}\times779.9=768.7°$.
  2. Mars's angle. $\dfrac{360°}{687}\times779.9=408.7°$.
  3. Subtract. $768.7°-408.7°=360.0°$.
Answer: exactly $360°$ — Earth laps Mars once per synodic period, which is precisely why the Sun, Earth and Mars realign and the retrograde loop repeats.
Chapter 3 · 1587 – 1595 · The discovery in the table

Four oppositions and a clock that won't keep time

Tycho's opposition table · longitudes 115.717° · 206.717° · 282.267° · 347.528° · the arithmetic that exposed Mars's variable speed

Reading Tycho's table

Kepler opened the campaign with the opposition ledger — date, time, and ecliptic longitude for each Sun–Earth–Mars alignment. He worked with the oppositions of 6 March 1587, 8 June 1591, 25 August 1593, and 31 October 1595, whose longitudes convert to 115.717°, 206.717°, 282.267°, and 347.528°. Four dates, four directions from the Sun: not yet an orbit, but enough for a devastating consistency check.

Mars's year was already known

One number about Mars was solid long before Kepler: its orbital period, 687 Earth days, read directly from the rhythm of its returns. If Mars also moved at constant speed — as every model from Aristotle to Copernicus assumed in some form — then its heliocentric longitude is a clock: elapsed days ÷ 687 gives the number of laps, laps × 360° gives the angle it must have swept. Predicting one opposition from another becomes pure arithmetic.

The 687-day check

Run it: from 6 March 1587 to 8 June 1591 is 1,555 days = 2.26 Mars-years = 814.9° swept. Add that to 115.717°, wrap by 360°, and a uniform Mars should stand near 210.6°. Tycho measured 206.717° — roughly 4° short. Other pairs miss by even more, in both directions (the lab below runs every pair). Tycho's error budget was 2 arcminutes; these misses are over a hundred times that. The data are fine. The assumption is broken.

Mars runs fast and slow

Conclusion: Mars has a variable speed in its orbit. It sweeps through some parts of its path quicker than others — which no single circle traversed uniformly can produce. Either the circle is traversed non-uniformly, or it isn't centred where everyone thought, or it isn't a circle. Kepler would eventually need all three. Chapter 4 starts with the patch Ptolemy had already invented for this disease.

∴ The uniform-clock test, worked exactly as in the notes

No geometry yet — just a clock, a calendar, and modular arithmetic.

  1. Count the days between two oppositions: 6 March 1587 → 8 June 1591 is $1{,}555$ days.
  2. Convert to Mars-years: $1{,}555 / 687 = 2.263$ — Mars has completed two full laps plus 0.263 of a third.
  3. Convert to angle, assuming uniform speed: $2.263 \times 360° = 814.9°$ swept since the first opposition.
  4. Predict and compare: $115.717° + 814.9° = 930.6°$; subtract two full turns (720°) to land on the circle: 210.6° predicted. Observed: 206.717°. The clock is wrong by about 4 degrees — two orders of magnitude beyond Tycho's measurement error.
What the sign pattern means: some pairs come out ahead of schedule, others behind — Mars gains time in one part of its orbit and loses it in another. A planet on a uniform circular clock can do neither. (Run all six pairs in the lab; the spread is −4° to +19°.)

About those day counts

A worked example quotes 1,556 days and a 2.6° miss; counting on the modern (proleptic Gregorian) calendar gives 1,555 days and a 3.9° miss. Sixteenth-century dates, calendar reforms (Gregorian adoption, 1582, mid-dataset!), and rounding of the converted longitudes all shuffle the third digit. Practice problems

1. Kepler's uniform-clock test: from $6$ March $1587$ to $8$ June $1591$ is $1555$ days. With a Mars year of $687$ days and uniform speed, the swept angle is $\dfrac{1555}{687}\times360°$, added to the start longitude $115.717°$. Predict the second longitude and compare with the observed $206.717°$.

Show solution
  1. Mars-years elapsed. $\dfrac{1555}{687}=2.263$ laps.
  2. Angle swept. $2.263\times360°=814.9°$.
  3. Add and wrap. $115.717°+814.9°=930.6°$; subtract $720°$ to get $210.6°$ predicted.
  4. Compare. $210.6°-206.717°=3.9°$ miss.
Answer: predicted $210.6°$ vs observed $206.7°$ — a $\sim4°$ miss, two orders of magnitude beyond Tycho's $2'$ precision, proving Mars does not run at constant speed.

2. A uniform Mars would sweep $360°$ in $687$ days. How many days should it take to sweep just $90°$ of its orbit at that constant rate?

Show solution
  1. Set up the proportion. $t=\dfrac{90°}{360°}\times687$ days.
  2. Evaluate. $t=0.25\times687=171.75$ days.
Answer: $171.75$ days — the constant-speed prediction Kepler tested; the real Mars covers some quadrants faster and others slower, which is what breaks the clock.

3. Mars completes one orbit in $687$ days. Earth's year is $365.25$ days. By what factor is Mars's average angular speed (degrees per day) slower than Earth's?

Show solution
  1. Angular speeds. Earth: $360°/365.25=0.9856°$/day. Mars: $360°/687=0.5240°$/day.
  2. Take the ratio. $\dfrac{0.9856}{0.5240}=1.88$.
Answer: Earth sweeps about $1.88\times$ faster — the inner planet's quicker march is what lets it overtake Mars and drag the sight line into retrograde.
Chapter 4 · 1600 – 1604 · The patch that almost worked

Ptolemy's equant, Kepler's bisection — and eight fatal arcminutes

Uniform motion about an off-centre point · the law of sines weighs the Sun–Mars distance · the "vicarious hypothesis" fails by 8′

Ptolemy's patch

Mars's uneven pace was ancient news, and Ptolemy had built a fix for it: the equant, a phantom point displaced from the circle's centre, about which the planet's angular motion is uniform. Seen from the equant, Mars ticks off equal angles in equal times; seen from anywhere else, it visibly speeds up when far from the equant and slows when near. One bolted-on point bought fourteen centuries of adequate predictions — at the price of abandoning, quietly, the principle of uniform circular motion it claimed to preserve.

The equant — uniform ticking, non-uniform speed

Mars moves so that the bearing from the equant (green) sweeps at a perfectly constant rate — watch the trail dots: equal time steps, unequal spacing. Slide the equant offset: at zero the motion is plain uniform circular; push it out and the planet visibly rushes through the near side and lingers on the far side, mimicking what Tycho's table demands. This single tunable point is what kept the circle alive for fourteen centuries.

Kepler bisects

Working heliocentrically, Kepler rebuilt the scheme around the Sun: Mars rides a circle whose centre sits between the Sun and the equant. Ptolemy had assumed those two offsets equal (the "bisected eccentricity", $e_{Sun} = e_{Equant}$) for the other planets; Kepler made it a hypothesis to be tested for Mars, fitting the geometry to four oppositions. The result — his "vicarious hypothesis" — predicted opposition longitudes to within ~2′, inside Tycho's error budget. For a moment, the war looked won.

Weighing distances with sines

But longitudes alone don't fix a model — distances do. Tycho's ledgers also held Mars's ecliptic latitudes, the small tilt of Mars above or below the ecliptic plane. At opposition that tilt builds a long thin triangle between Sun, Earth and Mars, and the law of sines turns its two measurable angles into the Sun–Mars distance, in units of the Earth–Sun distance. Doing this on opposite sides of the orbit gave $d_{SM} \approx 1.66$ on one side and $1.38$ on the other: radius $r \approx 1.51$, with the Sun displaced 0.08–0.10 au from the centre. The Sun is genuinely off-centre.

Eight minutes of arc

Feeding the measured distances back into the model snapped the trap shut: a circle that matched the longitudes missed the distances, and a circle that matched the distances missed off-opposition longitudes by up to 8 arcminutes — four times Tycho's 2′. Lesser astronomers would have shrugged; pre-Tycho data couldn't even have seen it. Kepler threw the model away, writing that those eight minutes "pointed the road to a complete reformation of astronomy". The circle itself was now the suspect.

How Tycho actually measured a planet's place

Every angle in Tycho's ledgers — the opposition longitudes of Chapter 3, the latitudes $\theta$ and $\alpha$ used just below — began as a naked-eye sighting against the fixed stars. Tycho almost never measured a planet's coordinate directly; he measured its angular distance from stars he had already catalogued, and let geometry supply the rest. That indirection is why his positions were good to one or two arcminutes a full lifetime before the telescope existed.

∴ A naked-eye position, step by step

No single instrument hands you a longitude and a latitude. Each is reconstructed from simpler, more trustworthy measurements.

  1. Distances to reference stars. With a finely divided sextant — two sight-vanes hinged at a graduated arc — the observer reads Mars's angular separation from each of two catalogued stars. Every measured distance confines Mars to a circle on the sky; two such circles cross at a single point, fixing its place. Measuring separations rather than absolute coordinates cancels most errors of levelling and alignment.
  2. The meridian anchor. The reference frame itself came from the great mural quadrant. As a star crossed the meridian (due south), its altitude gave the declination — given the known latitude of the observatory — while the moment of crossing, timed against a clock, gave the right ascension.
  3. A calibrated sky. Behind every planet measurement sat Tycho's catalogue of roughly 1,000 stars, their places tied through repeated solar observations to the equinox, the zero-point of ecliptic longitude. The planets were measured against a sky that had itself been pinned down first.
  4. Squeezing out the error. Tycho built the first serious refraction tables — correcting for the atmosphere bending light near the horizon — and averaged repeated nights. Together these lifted naked-eye accuracy to ~1–2′, several times sharper than any predecessor, and sharp enough for eight arcminutes to matter.
  5. Rotate to the ecliptic. A position recorded in equatorial terms (right ascension and declination) is swung into ecliptic coordinates — longitude $\lambda$ measured along the ecliptic, latitude $\beta$ measured across it — by spherical trigonometry through the obliquity $\varepsilon\approx23.5°$.
The payoff: the $\lambda$ and $\beta$ that drop out are exactly what the rest of this chapter consumes. The geocentric latitude $\theta=4.536°$ in the triangle just below is one such measured $\beta$; reduced to the Sun's vantage it becomes $\alpha=1.833°$.

The sextant — reading the angle between two stars

Tycho's sextant is a 60° arc with two sight-arms hinged at a pivot. One arm is locked on a reference star; slide the other until it lines up with Mars. The opening angle between the arms, read straight off the graduated arc, is the angular distance between the two objects — the raw number that feeds the triangulation below.

From the eyepiece to λ and β

Tycho recorded most observations as right ascension and declination — the natural output of a meridian instrument. Converting to the ecliptic longitude and latitude that Kepler needed is a fixed rotation of the celestial sphere through the obliquity $\varepsilon\approx23.44°$: for the latitude, $\sin\beta=\sin\delta\,\cos\varepsilon-\cos\delta\,\sin\varepsilon\,\sin(\mathrm{RA})$, with a companion formula for $\lambda$. Run the other way, the same trigonometry let Tycho fold each night's stars back onto his catalogue.

∴ The distance measurement, with the actual numbers

A triangle so thin it looks like a line — and yet it weighs an orbit. (Right-triangle trig and the law of sines are built from scratch in the spectroscopy companion's trig supplement.)

  1. The triangle: at an opposition, Sun, Earth and Mars are aligned in ecliptic longitude — but Mars rides slightly above the plane in latitude. In other words, from above they are aligned, but from the side, Mars is tilted. Looking from Earth, Mars stands at latitude $\theta = 4.536°$; reduced to the Sun's viewpoint the same tilt is $\alpha = 1.833°$. Sun, Earth, Mars form a triangle with those two known angles.
  2. The third angle is free: the angle at Earth, interior to the triangle, is $\beta = 180° - \theta = 175.464°$; the angle at Mars follows as $\gamma = 180° - \alpha - \beta = 2.703°$.
  3. Law of sines: sides are proportional to the sines of their opposite angles, so $\dfrac{d_{SM}}{\sin\beta} = \dfrac{d_{SE}}{\sin\gamma}$, giving $d_{SM} = d_{SE}\,\dfrac{\sin 175.464°}{\sin 2.703°} \approx 1.66$ astronomical units (Tycho's $d_{SE}$ ranged 0.975–1.000, hence the bracket 1.6315–1.6735).
  4. Do it again on the far side of the orbit (the second opposition: $\gamma = 4.40°$, $\beta = 173.95°$): $d_{SM} \approx 1.37$–$1.39$. Average the two sides: radius $r_{Mars} = \tfrac{1.6315 + 1.3900}{2} = 1.51075$ au — and the Sun sits $d_{SM} - r \approx 0.08$–$0.0994$ au away from the centre.
Why this mattered: these are absolute distances, obtained with no orbital model at all — pure triangle. Any proposed orbit now had two independent juries to satisfy: the longitudes and the distances. The vicarious hypothesis satisfied one and perjured itself before the other, by 8′.

Why Kepler trusted 8′ enough to demolish his own best model

The vicarious hypothesis was his — months of work, and the most accurate predictor of Mars longitudes ever constructed to that date. The discipline in discarding it came from knowing the instrument: Tycho's positions carried ~2′ of error, repeatedly verified, and the model's residual was 8′ — a 4σ-style violation, in modern terms, that no honest rounding could absorb. Compare the spirit of Rubin's flat curves (her Chapter 7): in both stories the revolution hinges not on a bold idea but on a measured discrepancy, an error budget, and the refusal to split the difference. Kepler's own verdict: "Since the divine benevolence has vouchsafed us Tycho Brahe, a most diligent observer, from whose observations the 8′ error in this Ptolemaic computation is shown, it is fitting that we with thankful mind both acknowledge and honour this benefit of God… For us, who by divine kindness were given an accurate observer such as Tycho Brahe, for us it is to the good that by these eight minutes the road to a complete reformation of astronomy has been opened."

Practice problems

1. At opposition Sun, Earth and Mars form a long thin triangle. The reduced angle at the Sun is $\alpha=1.833°$ and the angle at Earth is $\beta=180°-\theta=175.464°$, so the angle at Mars is $\gamma=180°-\alpha-\beta$. With the Earth–Sun distance $d_{SE}=1.000$ au, use the law of sines $\dfrac{d_{SM}}{\sin\beta}=\dfrac{d_{SE}}{\sin\gamma}$ to find the Sun–Mars distance.

Show solution
  1. Third angle. $\gamma=180°-1.833°-175.464°=2.703°$.
  2. Law of sines. $d_{SM}=d_{SE}\,\dfrac{\sin\beta}{\sin\gamma}=1.000\times\dfrac{\sin175.464°}{\sin2.703°}$.
  3. Evaluate. $=\dfrac{0.07905}{0.04716}=1.676$ au.
Answer: $d_{SM}\approx1.68$ au — an absolute distance from two angles and a baseline, with no orbital model assumed: the second jury that convicted the centred circle.

2. Repeat the measurement on the far side of the orbit, where $\gamma=4.40°$, $\beta=173.95°$, and Tycho's Earth–Sun distance was $d_{SE}=0.975$ au. Find $d_{SM}$ there.

  2. Sun offset. $d_{far}-r=1.6315-1.5108=0.121$ au; equivalently the eccentricity $e=\dfrac{0.121}{1.5108}=0.080$.

The patch is dead. Now draw the orbit itself.

No more fitting models to angles. Kepler's next move was to construct Mars's true positions in space, point by point, with no orbit assumed at all — using nothing but geometry and a 687-day clock.

Chapter 5 · The construction · Mars as its own surveyor's mark

The 687-day trick: triangulating an orbit with a clock

Same Mars, two Earths · two sight lines cross at one point in space · the construction behind keplerOrbitCalc.ipynb

Mars as its own clock

A longitude is a direction, not a place (Chapter 2's parting warning) — one sight line cannot say how far Mars is. Surveyors solve this by looking at the same landmark from two ends of a baseline. Kepler's flash of method: the 687-day period makes Mars its own landmark. Pick two observations exactly one Mars-year apart, and Mars is at the same point of its orbit in both — while Earth, whose year doesn't divide 687, is somewhere completely different each time.

Two Earths, one Mars

So each pair of Tycho entries separated by a multiple of 687 days hands Kepler a triangle: two known Earth positions (the baseline) and one unknown Mars position at the apex. Draw the sight line from each Earth in its recorded direction; the lines cross at the one point in space consistent with both observations. That crossing is Mars — located absolutely, with no assumption about the shape of its orbit whatsoever.

Drawing the sight lines

The notebook's dataset (triangulation.csv) holds five such pairs from Tycho's ledgers, 1585–1595: for each entry, Earth's heliocentric longitude (placing it on its orbit) and Mars's observed geocentric direction. Two lines per pair, one intersection each — five fixes on Mars's true orbit, spread around the zodiac. The lab below draws every pair, exactly as the notebook computes them.

Five points, no model

It is worth pausing on how radical this is: every previous astronomer fitted a presumed curve to angles. Kepler instead reconstructed raw positions in space and only then asked what curve they lie on — letting the data dictate the orbit rather than the orbit interpret the data. He has been called the first data scientist on these grounds, and the next two chapters are precisely a model-fitting contest on these five points. (He pulled the same trick in reverse, using Mars as the fixed anchor to redraw Earth's orbit first.)

∴ Where the sight lines cross — the notebook's formula, derived

The whole computation is two straight lines and one shared point; this is the algebra inside findMars().

  1. Place the Earths. Taking Earth's orbit as a unit circle around the Sun (the notebook's simplification), an Earth at heliocentric longitude $\theta_E$ sits at $(x_E, y_E) = (\cos\theta_E,\ \sin\theta_E)$.
  2. Write each sight line. From Earth, Mars was seen in direction $m$ (its geocentric longitude). A line through $(x_E, y_E)$ with slope $\tan m$ is $y - y_E = \tan m\,(x - x_E)$.
  3. Intersect the pair. Set the two lines equal and solve for $x$: $$x_M = \frac{y_2 - y_1 + x_1\tan m_1 - x_2\tan m_2}{\tan m_1 - \tan m_2},$$ and symmetrically for $y_M$ using cotangents — exactly the two expressions in the notebook code below.
  4. Sanity-check the geometry: the construction fails only if $\tan m_1 = \tan m_2$ — parallel sight lines, meaning Earth happened to lie at the same bearing from Mars both times. Tycho's pairs are well-conditioned; the worst crossing angles in the dataset are still tens of degrees.
Output: five Cartesian positions of Mars in the ecliptic plane, in astronomical units — listed in the lab readout, and matching the notebook's printout to the fourth decimal. These five dots are the raw material for everything that remains.

The construction in code — verbatim from keplerOrbitCalc.ipynb:

def findMars(earthLocation1, marsAngle1, earthLocation2, marsAngle2):
    x1, y1 = earthLocation1          # Earth at the first date  (unit circle)
    x2, y2 = earthLocation2          # Earth one Mars-year later
    ma1, ma2 = marsAngle1, marsAngle2   # observed directions to Mars

    xMars = ((y2 - y1 + (x1 * math.tan(ma1)) - (x2 * math.tan(ma2)))
            / (math.tan(ma1) - math.tan(ma2)))

    yMars = ((x2 - x1 + (y1 / math.tan(ma1)) - (y2 / math.tan(ma2)))
            / ((1 / math.tan(ma1)) - (1 / math.tan(ma2))))

    return [xMars, yMars]

The third dimension: Tycho's latitudes and the orbital plane

The five points above live in the ecliptic plane — they are projections, shadows of Mars's true positions. Mars's recorded latitudes (its small tilt above and below the ecliptic, the same angles that weighed distances in Chapter 4) recover the missing dimension. The notebook converts each geocentric latitude to a heliocentric one, places the points on the celestial sphere, and fits a plane through them by gradient descent — finding Mars's orbital plane tilted by about 1.85° to the ecliptic and, crucially, passing through the Sun: the modern value of Mars's inclination, recovered from 1590s naked-eye data. Kepler established the same two facts (a fixed plane, through the Sun, at constant inclination) early in the campaign, demolishing the older theory that Mars's latitudes needed oscillating planes. "Lifting" the five points onto that plane (z = -ax - by) is what the next chapter's fits operate on.

Chapter 6 · 1604 · The shape refuses to be round

Fit the circle. Watch it miss.

A best-fit circle through the five points errs by 17% · the orbit pinches inward by 0.00429 au · Kepler's "egg" wrong turn

Fit the circle

Five model-free positions in hand, the obvious first hypothesis is the one every astronomer since antiquity would reach for: a circle. The notebook does precisely what Kepler did — find the circle that best accommodates the five points (average their distances from the Sun's side of the plane: radius 1.578 au) and measure the leftover error. Honest fitting means honest residuals: the misfit is 16.9% — vast against data this good.

It misses systematically

And it misses with a shape: the points pull inside the circle on the flanks and touch it at the ends — the orbit is slightly flattened, pinched along one axis. Kepler quantified the pinch directly: at quadrature the true path sits inside the reference circle by a maximum gap he computed as 0.00429 (in units of the orbit radius) — about half a percent. A tiny number, but Tycho-grade data resolves it without effort.

The egg mistake

Astronomia Nova faithfully records the next wrong turn: convinced the path was non-circular, Kepler first tried an ovoid — egg-shaped, fatter at one end — reasoning physically that Mars should linger more at one extreme. Over a year of brutal computation went into the egg before it, too, failed against the data. The honest record of this detour is why the book reads like a lab notebook rather than a victory speech.

A gap of 0.00429

What survived the egg's collapse were two stubborn numbers: the orbit lies inside the circle by up to 0.00429, and the Sun sits off-centre by about 0.09 (Chapter 4). Kepler now had the orbit's amount of squash and its off-centre Sun, but not yet its law. The connection between the two numbers would arrive, as he admitted, "as if by accident" — the next chapter's secant.

∴ Fitting a circle to the five points — the notebook's arithmetic

The simplest least-squares fit there is, and the residual that ends an era.

  1. Lift the points onto the orbital plane found in Chapter 5 ($z = -ax - by$), so distances are true distances, not shadows.
  2. Best-fit circle about the Sun: with the Sun as centre, the optimal radius is just the mean of the five Sun-distances: $r = \tfrac{1}{5}\sum_i \lVert P_i \rVert = 1.5779$ au.
  3. Score it: sum the squared deviations, $\sum_i (r - \lVert P_i \rVert)^2 = 0.0713$, i.e. a scatter of $\sqrt{0.0713}/1.578 \approx$ 16.9% of the radius. Tycho's data justify errors a hundred times smaller.
  4. Diagnose the failure mode: the residuals are not random — alternate points sit systematically nearer the Sun. The circle isn't noisy; it is the wrong family of curves. (Allowing the circle's centre to slide off the Sun helps the longitudes but re-breaks the distances — the same trap as Chapter 4, now in Cartesian form.)
The methodological point here: fit the simplest model, quantify the loss, inspect the structure of the residuals, and only then upgrade the model class. Kepler ran a model-selection loop, three centuries before the vocabulary existed.

Half a percent — why the gap was invisible for two millennia

Mars's orbit deviates from a circle by at most ~0.4% of its radius (the 0.00429), even though its eccentricity — the off-centredness of the Sun — is a much larger 9%. That asymmetry is the historical trap: an off-centre circle mops up almost all of the observational signature of an ellipse, because an ellipse with eccentricity $e$ differs from its best circle only at order $e^2$ ($0.09^2 \approx 0.008$ — the right scale). Pre-telescopic data before Tycho carried ~10′ errors, comfortably blind to half a percent; Tycho's 2′ could just resolve it. Kepler's ellipse was, quite literally, the first discovery to live entirely inside the second decimal place.

Chapter 7 · 1605 · "I awoke as if from sleep"

The lucky secant: 1.00429, twice

The angle 5°18′ at quadrature · sec(5°18′) = 1.00429 = AC/MC · the distance law, the ellipse, and a 16× better fit

Two orphaned numbers

Out of the egg's wreckage, Kepler held two hard-won measurements with no visible connection: the orbit pinches inside the reference circle by a maximum of 0.00429 (Chapter 6), and from Mars at quadrature — the point M square-on to the apsidal line — the angle between the orbit's centre C and the Sun S spans 5°18′ (a direct consequence of the Sun's 0.09 off-centredness, Chapter 4). He had been staring at both for months.

The accident

Then, as he tells it, "by accident": idly computing the secant of 5°18′ — the reciprocal of its cosine — he got 1.00429. The same digits as the gap ratio AC/MC = 1.00429. A number he had measured from Tycho's data was reappearing out of a trigonometric table. "I felt as if I had been awakened from sleep," he wrote — the two orphans were one fact wearing two disguises.

What the coincidence forces

The geometry is a right triangle: at quadrature, C-M-S has its right angle at C, so $\sec(\angle CMS) = MS/MC$. Setting the two 1.00429s equal gives $MS = AC$ — the Sun–Mars distance at quadrature exactly equals the circle's radius (the semi-major axis). That is a signature property of one curve and one curve only: an ellipse with the Sun at a focus. The pinch and the off-centre Sun weren't two anomalies; they were the two halves of an ellipse's definition.

The ellipse, at last

From the recovered distance law, Kepler rebuilt the orbit as a true ellipse and it passed every jury at once — longitudes, distances, latitudes, inside Tycho's 2′. The modern replay in keplerOrbitCalc.ipynb reaches the same verdict numerically: a gradient-descent ellipse fit to the five triangulated points lands with 1.0% error against the circle's 16.9% — sixteen times better, with eccentricity ≈ 0.10 (Mars: 0.093). Astronomia Nova (1609) published it as what we now call the first law, alongside the equal-areas second law he had derived on the way.

∴ From a table of secants to the first law — the chain, completed

Each step is small; the conclusion moved the Sun to a focus.

  1. Measured (Ch. 6): at quadrature the circle overshoots the true orbit by the ratio $AC/MC = 1.00429$, where $AC$ is the circle's radius and $MC$ Mars's actual distance from the centre.
  2. Measured (Ch. 4): from M, the centre-to-Sun offset subtends $\angle CMS = 5°18'$. (Check it: the offset is $\approx 0.0926$ of the radius, and $\tan^{-1} 0.0926 = 5.3°$ — the two prior measurements were already consistent.)
  3. Geometry: S lies on the apsidal line through C, and M at quadrature is square to that line — so triangle CMS has its right angle at C, giving $\cos(\angle CMS) = MC/MS$, i.e. $MS = MC\,\sec(\angle CMS) = MC \times 1.00429$.
  4. The collision: step 1 says $AC = MC \times 1.00429$ too. Therefore $\boldsymbol{MS = AC}$: Mars's distance from the Sun at quadrature equals the semi-major axis $a$.
  5. Recognition: for an ellipse with a focus at S, the point at the end of the minor axis sits at distance exactly $a$ from each focus (the two focal distances, summing to $2a$ by definition, are equal there). Kepler's measured orbit had been an ellipse with the Sun at a focus all along — the off-centre Sun supplies the eccentricity, the 0.00429 pinch is the $e^2/2$ flattening.
First law: planets move on ellipses with the Sun at one focus. The "coincidence" was the curve introducing itself; what made it audible was carrying every measured digit honestly through five years of failed models.

The modern verdict — the notebook's gradient-descent ellipse fit, condensed:

# loss: an ellipse with foci at the Sun (origin) and (xf, yf) and major axis 2a
# demands  dist(P, Sun) + dist(P, F2) = 2a  for every orbit point P
def evaluateDistance(xM, yM, xf, yf, majorAxis):
    return sum((math.hypot(x, y) + math.hypot(x - xf, y - yf) - majorAxis)**2
               for x, y in zip(xM, yM))

The second law was already in his pocket

Two years before the ellipse, Kepler had extracted the area law from the same distance measurements: the line from Sun to planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times — the planet moving fast near perihelion and slow near aphelion in exactly compensating proportion. It was his replacement for the equant: where Ptolemy faked variable speed with a phantom point, the area law ties the speed physically to the distance from the Sun (Kepler suspected a force from the rotating Sun — wrong mechanism, right intuition; angular momentum conservation, as Newton would show). In Astronomia Nova the two laws appear together: the ellipse says where Mars goes, the areas say when.

Chapter 8 · 1609 → · What the ellipse founded

Three laws, one gravity, and the habits of a data scientist

Harmonice Mundi 1619 · Newton's Principia 1687 · the same clockwork Vera Rubin found broken at galactic scale

Three laws

The campaign's results, as posterity numbered them: (1) orbits are ellipses with the Sun at a focus (1609); (2) the Sun–planet line sweeps equal areas in equal times (1609, derived by 1602); (3) the square of the period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis, $P^2 \propto a^3$ — found in 1618, published in Harmonice Mundi (1619), and binding every planet into one system with a single rule.

Newton's why

Kepler's laws described; they did not explain. In 1687 Newton's Principia showed all three are theorems of one assumption — a universal attraction falling off as $1/r^2$. Ellipses with a focal Sun, equal areas (conservation of angular momentum), and $P^2 \propto a^3$ each drop out of the same gravity. The fit became a force; the force became predictions Kepler never dreamed of, from tides to new planets.

To the galaxies

And the same clockwork became astronomy's scale. The Vera Rubin companion opens exactly where this one ends: Newton's $v = \sqrt{GM/r}$ — Kepler's third law in dynamical clothing — predicts how anything must orbit anything. When Rubin found galaxy rotation curves refusing the Keplerian decline, the choice was the same one Kepler faced with his 8 arcminutes: distrust the data, or follow the discrepancy. Both times, the discrepancy won; the second time, it revealed the dark universe.

The first data scientist

The "first data scientist" framing earns its title. Kepler decoupled confounded variables (oppositions), validated his clock against held-out observations (the 687-day check), reconstructed model-free ground truth (triangulation), fit by explicit loss minimization, read the structure of his residuals, upgraded model class only when forced (circle → egg → ellipse), and published the failures beside the result. Every habit has a modern name; he had them in 1605, with a quill.

The third law — find the exponent yourself

All planets known to Kepler (plus the three found since), plotted as orbital period against semi-major axis on log scales. Slide the exponent $n$ in $P \propto a^n$: the line pivots, and the misfit readout scores it against all eight planets at once. At the correct exponent it becomes $v \propto 1/\sqrt{a}$ — the falling curve through every planet known as the Keplerian decline, the very shape Vera Rubin would later go looking for in galaxies.

The Keplerian decline — the shape Rubin went looking for

The third law has a second face, and it matters enough to deserve its name. A planet's average speed is circumference over period, $v = 2\pi a / P$; substitute the third law $P = a^{3/2}$ and the period cancels into $v = 2\pi a / a^{3/2} \propto \boldsymbol{1/\sqrt{a}}$ — the farther out, the slower, by exactly a square root. That falling curve (the lower panel above, with every planet on it) is the Keplerian decline: Mercury races at 47 km/s, Earth cruises at 29.8, Neptune crawls at 5.4. Newton explained why — it is the speed signature of orbiting a single central mass — which is precisely why, three and a half centuries later, it became the expected rotation curve for the outskirts of every spiral galaxy. The Vera Rubin companion opens by deriving this same curve from gravity, and turns on the fact that galaxies refuse to follow it. Notice the symmetry of the two stories: Kepler's data broke the perfect circle; Rubin's data broke the Keplerian decline. Same discipline, same kind of discrepancy — and each time, a bigger universe behind it.

  2. Take the square root. $T=\sqrt{140.6}=11.86$ yr.
Answer: $T\approx11.9$ yr — Jupiter's true sidereal period, the law that binds every planet into a single $T^2\propto a^3$ system.

2. The full law is $M[M_\odot]=\dfrac{a^3[\text{AU}]}{T^2[\text{yr}]}$. A hot-Jupiter exoplanet orbits its star at $a=0.05$ AU with period $T=4.0$ days. Weigh the star (convert $T$ to years first; $1$ yr $=365.25$ d).

Show solution
  1. Period in years. $T=\dfrac{4.0}{365.25}=0.010951$ yr.
  2. Cube and square. $a^3=0.05^3=1.25\times10^{-4}$; $T^2=(0.010951)^2=1.1993\times10^{-4}$.
  3. Divide. $M=\dfrac{1.25\times10^{-4}}{1.1993\times10^{-4}}=1.04$.
Answer: $M\approx1.04\,M_\odot$ — a Sun-like host star, weighed from nothing but the planet's orbit, exactly how exoplanet hosts are massed today.

3. Newton recast the law as a circular orbital speed $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$. With $G=6.674\times10^{-11}$, the Sun's mass $M=1.989\times10^{30}$ kg and Earth's orbit $r=1.496\times10^{11}$ m, find Earth's orbital speed.

Show solution
  1. Numerator. $GM=6.674\times10^{-11}\times1.989\times10^{30}=1.327\times10^{20}$.
  2. Divide by $r$. $\dfrac{1.327\times10^{20}}{1.496\times10^{11}}=8.872\times10^{8}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2$.
  3. Square root. $v=\sqrt{8.872\times10^{8}}=2.979\times10^{4}$ m/s.
Answer: $v\approx29.8$ km/s — Earth's actual orbital speed, and the formula $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$ whose galactic failure later announced dark matter.
