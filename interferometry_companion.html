How telescopes combine light
to see the impossibly small

A single mirror already works by interference — countless light rays adding up in step at one focus. Interferometry takes that idea outdoors: link separate telescopes, even continents apart, and make their light add up too. The reward is resolution no single dish could ever reach — sharp enough to photograph a black hole's shadow or pull a planet out of its star's glare. This companion builds the idea from one wave, to two telescopes, to an Earth-sized array.

The foundation · Two waves add

It all starts with interference

Thomas Young's two slits · why even an ordinary mirror is an interferometer

What was known

Light is a wave — an electric field rising and falling in time. Where two light waves overlap they do not simply brighten; they interfere. Crest meeting crest adds up (constructive interference); crest meeting trough cancels (destructive interference). Thomas Young showed this in 1801: two pinholes throw a pattern of bright and dark fringes on a screen.

The idea

An ordinary telescope mirror already exploits this. Light striking every part of the mirror is bounced to a single focus; only because all those rays arrive in phase do they add constructively into one sharp point. A mirror is, quietly, a perfect interferometer — millions of rays summed in step. So why not pull the pieces apart and combine the light from separate telescopes?

The test

Treat two telescopes like Young's two slits. The light from a distant star reaches each one, and when their beams are combined the result is a fringe pattern whose spacing depends on the separation — the baseline — between them. The crucial quantity is the phase difference: how far one wave is shifted relative to the other when they meet.

What it founded

This single fact — that two coherent waves add according to their phase — is the seed of all interferometry. Everything that follows (Earth-sized radio arrays, optical delay lines, nulling out a star) is just clever ways of controlling that phase so the waves add the way we want.

Add two light waves

Here are two identical waves arriving at a combiner. Slide the phase difference between them and watch the sum. In step (0°) they reinforce — a bright fringe; half a wave out (180°) they cancel completely — a dark fringe. This is the entire engine of interferometry: the combined brightness encodes the phase difference between the beams.

∴ The reasoning: why brightness reads out phase

Two equal waves combine into one wave whose amplitude depends only on their phase difference $\phi$.

  1. Add the fields: $A\sin\omega t + A\sin(\omega t+\phi)$. The trigonometric sum gives a single wave of amplitude $2A\cos(\phi/2)$.
  2. At $\phi=0$ the amplitude is $2A$ — twice as tall, so four times the power. At $\phi=180°$ it is zero: the beams annihilate. This is constructive and destructive interference.
  3. What a detector measures is power $\propto\cos^2(\phi/2)$. So measuring brightness is measuring the phase difference — the central trick that lets an interferometer recover information no single beam carries.
Conclusion: the combined intensity is a coded message about the relative phase of the two beams. Control or read that phase and you control the instrument.

Practice problems

1. Two equal beams combine with a phase difference of $\phi=90°$. By what factor is the combined power reduced compared with the in-phase case?

Show solution
  1. Power law. Combined power $\propto\cos^2(\phi/2)$. At $\phi=0$, $\cos^2 0=1$.
  2. At 90°. $\cos^2(45°)=(0.707)^2=0.5$.
Answer: the power is halved — at quarter-wave offset the fringe is exactly midway between bright and dark.

2. A path-length difference of half a wavelength between the two beams produces what phase difference, and what does the detector see?

Show solution
  1. Path to phase. One full wavelength of extra path = $360°$ of phase. So $\lambda/2$ of path = $180°$.
  2. Result. $\cos^2(90°)=0$ — complete cancellation.
Answer: $180°$, a dark fringe. This is exactly why path lengths must be controlled to a fraction of a wavelength.
The payoff · Why we bother

Resolution: the bigger the baseline, the finer the detail

From a single dish to an Earth-sized array — the law $\theta\approx\lambda/B$

What was known

Every telescope is blurred by diffraction. A single aperture of diameter $D$ can resolve two points no closer than about $\theta\approx1.22\,\lambda/D$ — the diffraction limit. Longer wavelengths blur more; bigger apertures sharpen. This is why radio dishes, working at metre to millimetre waves, are huge yet still see a coarse sky.

The idea

Interferometry replaces the single diameter $D$ with the baseline $B$ — the separation between two telescopes. The pair resolves detail as fine as $\theta\approx\lambda/B$, as if it were a telescope as wide as the gap between them, even though almost all the glass in between is missing. Spread telescopes across a continent and $B$ becomes thousands of kilometres.

The test

The numbers are startling. The Event Horizon Telescope links radio dishes worldwide into a baseline nearly the size of the Earth and observes at 1.3 mm; that gives $\theta\sim20$ microarcseconds — sharp enough to resolve the glowing ring around a black hole's event horizon. The VLTI reaches a few milliarcseconds in the near-infrared with 100-metre baselines.

What it founded

Resolution is the whole reason to combine light. A single 8-metre mirror resolves about 15 milliarcseconds in visible light; an array of the same mirrors 130 metres apart resolves a hundred times finer — measuring stellar diameters, imaging dust around young stars, and tracking stars orbiting our Galaxy's central black hole.

∴ The reasoning: why separation can replace a solid mirror

Resolution comes from the edges of the aperture, not the glass in the middle.

  1. Diffraction sets the finest angle a telescope can split: $\theta\approx\lambda/D$, fixed by how far apart its widest points are.
  2. Two small telescopes a distance $B$ apart sample the wave at two points $B$ across — exactly the widest points of an imaginary aperture of size $B$. For each detail at scale $\theta\sim\lambda/B$, they record one fringe.
  3. The price: a single baseline measures only one spatial scale and direction at a time. To build a real image you need many baselines — many telescope pairs, and the Earth's rotation to sweep them (next chapters).
Conclusion: an interferometer trades a filled mirror for empty space plus cleverness — it gets the resolution of a giant aperture from just its rim.
The core skill · Sliding waves into step

The phasing puzzle: a wave, a delay, and a slide

Why interferometry is, at heart, lining up two records of the same wave

What was known

A wavefront from a distant star is tilted relative to two telescopes on the ground, so the light reaches one before the other. That head-start is the geometric delay — extra path the far telescope's signal must cover. Until it is removed, the two beams are out of step and will not interfere cleanly.

The idea

Picture each telescope's output as a signal versus time — a wave whose amplitude wobbles. The two records look almost identical, but one is shifted in time relative to the other. The job is to slide one back and forth until it lines up with the other. That sliding is phasing (or delay compensation), and the right amount of slide is the geometric delay.

The test

How do you know when they line up? You correlate them — multiply the two signals together and add up the result. When they are aligned, crests sit on crests and the correlation peaks; the interference fringes appear. Misaligned, the product averages to noise. The peak of the correlation curve tells you the exact delay.

What it founded

This is the universal puzzle of interferometry, whether you slide the signal inside a computer (radio) or slide the actual light beam (optical). Solve it for every pair of telescopes, every moment, and you have measured the wave's phase across a huge aperture — the raw material of a high-resolution image.

Solve the puzzle: slide until the fringes appear

The two telescopes recorded the same starlight, but the second arrived with a built-in geometric delay. Drag the compensating delay to slide the second signal back into step. The correlation meter peaks — and the fringes lock in — only when your delay matches the true geometric delay. That single number is what every interferometer must find, for every baseline, all the time.

Why this is the same job for radio and optical

The puzzle never changes: two records of one wave, offset in time, to be slid into register. What differs is how you slide. In radio the signal is digitized, so the slide is arithmetic — shift the numbers in a computer. In optical the wave oscillates far too fast to digitize, so the slide is physical — lengthen or shorten the actual light path with a moving mirror (a "trombone" delay line). Same puzzle, different hands. Both also depend on knowing the time at each telescope extremely precisely, because a delay is a difference of arrival times.

Two ways to combine the light · numbers vs. photons

Radio waves are slow enough to capture as a stream of numbers and combine inside a computer. Optical waves are a hundred-thousand times faster — too fast for any electronics — so the light itself must be physically brought together. Same puzzle, two utterly different machines.

Radio · Record the field, correlate in a computer

VLBI and the Event Horizon Telescope

Atomic clocks, hard drives, and the Earth's own rotation building an image

What was known

Radio frequencies — megahertz to a few hundred gigahertz — are slow enough that electronics can sample the electric field directly. Each radio telescope becomes a faithful record of the field going up and down over time, stamped with the exact moment of each sample by a hydrogen-maser atomic clock.

The idea

If every dish records its field with a precise timestamp, the telescopes need never be physically connected. Ship the recordings to one place — a correlator — and let a computer do the phasing: shift each station's data by its geometric delay so the phases align, then correlate every pair. This is Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI), and it lets the baseline span the whole planet.

The test

The Event Horizon Telescope did exactly this at 1.3 mm, linking dishes from Hawaii to Spain to the South Pole. Each station's atmosphere is different — different altitude, water vapour, and distance to the source — so the propagation time to each is different and must be modelled and corrected. The computer slides each field record until they all line up.

What it founded

One baseline gives one measurement. But as the Earth rotates, each pair of telescopes views the source from a continuously changing angle, sweeping out new baselines and filling in the missing aperture — aperture synthesis. Combine a night's worth and you reconstruct an image: the glowing ring around M87's and our own Galaxy's central black hole.

Build a picture from the Earth's spin

Add telescopes and let the night run. Each pair of dishes traces a curved track across the uv-plane — the map of which baselines (which spatial scales and directions) you've measured — as the Earth rotates. The more dishes and the longer you observe, the more completely the plane fills, and the sharper and more faithful the reconstructed image becomes. This is aperture synthesis: trading a filled mirror for time and geometry.

∴ The reasoning: why rotation is free aperture

A static pair of dishes measures one baseline. A rotating Earth carries them through a whole family of baselines for nothing.

  1. Resolution needs many baselines — many separations and orientations — to sample every detail of the source. Building that many fixed dishes is impossible.
  2. But as the Earth turns, each pair's projected separation (as seen from the source) continuously changes, tracing a curve through the uv-plane. Hours of observing convert two dishes into hundreds of effective baselines.
  3. A Fourier transform of the filled uv-plane is the image. Gaps in coverage become artefacts; that's why the EHT needs many stations and a long track, plus careful modelling of each site's atmospheric propagation time.
Conclusion: VLBI turns precise timekeeping and the planet's own rotation into an aperture the size of the Earth — no glass required between the dishes.
Radio · The first interferometer was a cliff

How do astronomers identify the source of a radio burst?

Ruby Payne-Scott, the sea-cliff interferometer, and locating the Sun's storms

The problem

A burst of radio — from the Sun, a flare star, a fast radio burst — arrives at a single dish loud but placeless. With resolution $\theta\approx\lambda/D$ and metre-scale wavelengths, one antenna smears the source across whole degrees of sky. Knowing a burst happened is easy; knowing where is the hard part.

The idea

Interfere two antennas. The very same $\theta\approx\lambda/B$ that sharpens an image also localises a source: as it drifts across the sky the combined signal beats through fringes, and the fringe timing pins its position to a fraction of the single-dish beam. Localising a point source and imaging an extended one are the same trick read two ways.

The test · a cliff and the sea

In 1945–46 Ruby Payne-Scott (CSIRO, Sydney) built an interferometer from one antenna on the 85-m Dover Heights cliff. It received the Sun twice — directly, and reflected off the ocean — so the direct and sea-bounced rays interfered: a sea-cliff (Lloyd's-mirror) interferometer. The rising Sun swept the fringes past the dish, and the pattern fixed each burst's height and tied it to a sunspot group.

What it founded

Payne-Scott — one of the first radio astronomers anywhere — classified the Sun's bursts and co-invented the swept-lobe interferometer to follow a moving source live. Her cliff was a direct ancestor of every interferometric array in this companion, from the VLA to the EHT.

From fringe to position

Two antennas a baseline $B$ apart see a source's wavefront arrive with a path difference $B\sin\theta$. Each time that difference grows by one wavelength the combined signal passes through a full fringe. Counting fringes as the source moves measures $\theta$ to a precision $\sim\lambda/B$ — and for Payne-Scott's vertical "baseline" (the antenna and its mirror image below the sea surface), the rising Sun did the scanning for free.

What the burst's drift then tells you

Once located, a solar burst's drift in frequency reveals its origin: the corona thins with height, ionised gas radiates at its plasma frequency, so a disturbance climbing outward emits at an ever-lower frequency. A fast drift is an electron beam from a flare (a Type III burst); a slow drift is a coronal-mass-ejection shock (Type II). The physics of these solar storms — and the backyard "Itty Bitty Telescope" that hears the Sun — is laid out in the radio astronomy companion.

Optical · The light is too fast to record

Optical interferometry and the trombone delay line

The VLTI · bringing real beams together to within a wavelength

What was known

Visible light oscillates at roughly $5\times10^{14}$ cycles a second — a hundred thousand times faster than radio. No electronics can sample a field that quick, so the radio trick of recording the wave and shifting it in a computer is simply impossible at optical wavelengths.

The idea

If you cannot record the wave, you must combine the actual light. Pipe the beams from separate telescopes through tunnels to a central beam combiner and let the photons interfere directly — an analogue combination, done with mirrors and air, not arithmetic. The European Southern Observatory's VLTI does this with its 8.2-metre and 1.8-metre telescopes.

The test

But the beams still arrive out of step by the geometric delay, and that delay changes second by second as the Earth turns. The fix is a moving mirror on a track — a delay line, like a trombone slide — that lengthens or shortens one beam's path by exactly the right amount to re-equalise the two paths in real time.

What it founded

The paths must match to within the coherence length — for broadband light, only a few wavelengths — or the fringes wash out. Holding that match to a fraction of a micron, against a moving Earth and a churning atmosphere, is what lets optical interferometers measure stellar diameters and watch stars whip around the Galactic centre's black hole.

Tune the trombone until the fringe packet lights up

The two optical beams interfere only where their path lengths match to within the coherence length. Slide the delay line (the trombone) and a burst of fringes — the fringe packet — appears right at zero path difference, then fades. Narrow-band light has a long coherence length and a fat, forgiving packet; broadband "white" light gives a razor-thin one you must hit precisely.

Why optical interferometry is so much harder than radio

In radio the wave is recorded, so the delay can be searched at leisure in software, and the tolerance is a comfortable fraction of a long wavelength. In optical the wave is gone the instant it is combined: you must put the delay line in the right place physically, now, to within a fraction of a micron — and keep adjusting as the Earth rotates and the atmosphere shoves the fringes around. That is why optical arrays use long vacuum tunnels of moving mirrors and fast fringe-tracking, while radio arrays use disks and a computer.

Fighting the sky · Phasing on the fly

Adaptive optics and phasing a giant mirror

A deformable mirror reshaping the wavefront — and the ELT's 798 segments

What was known

The atmosphere is not still. Turbulent cells of warm and cool air bend starlight by slightly different amounts across the aperture, so the once-flat wavefront arrives crumpled. The light no longer adds in phase at the focus, and the star image boils into a blur of speckles — this is astronomical seeing.

The idea

If the wavefront is crumpled, un-crumple it. Measure its distortion many hundreds of times a second and bounce the light off a deformable mirror whose surface is pushed and pulled by thousands of tiny actuators to apply the opposite shape. The errors cancel, the wavefront flattens, and the light snaps back into phase. This is adaptive optics.

The test

Each beam carries its own atmospheric path, so correction must be done per telescope and continuously — essentially a fast, local version of the same phasing puzzle. The quality of the fix is measured by the Strehl ratio: how much of the light has been gathered back into a sharp diffraction-limited core versus left in a fuzzy halo.

What it founded

The same logic scales up to the mirror itself. The Extremely Large Telescope is not one mirror but 798 hexagonal segments that must be co-phased to a common surface within nanometres — and its built-in deformable mirror corrects the atmosphere in real time. Giant segmented telescopes are, in effect, interferometers that must continually phase their own pieces.

Two combiner designs · Where the light meets

Fizeau vs Michelson combination

Combine in the image plane, or in the pupil — ELT & Colossus vs VLTI & the EHT

What was known

Once you have several beams, there are two fundamentally different places to make them interfere. You can let them come to a common focus and overlap in the image plane — the way an ordinary camera forms a picture — or you can overlap them in the pupil plane, mixing the beams themselves before any image forms.

The idea

The first is the Fizeau scheme: the sub-apertures keep their true geometric layout and share one focus, so the result is a real, wide-field image — exactly how a single giant mirror works. A multi-mirror or segmented telescope like the ELT, exoplanet imagers like the ExoLife Finder (ELF) and its pathfinder SELF, or a proposed array like Colossus, are Fizeau interferometers: they add the electric fields right in the detector.

The test

The second is the Michelson scheme (pupil-plane combination), used by the VLTI: separate telescopes pipe their beams to a combiner that mixes pairs and records the fringe for each baseline. It handles very long, sparse baselines that no common focus could, at the cost of a tiny field of view. The radio correlator of VLBI is the digital version of the same idea.

What it founded

Either way the physics is the one we started with: add the fields and, in software or with mirrors, slide the records until they align. Fizeau gives you an image directly; Michelson and the correlator give you per-baseline fringe measurements you later assemble — through aperture synthesis — into an image.

Two ways to make beams interfere

On the left, a Fizeau combiner: the sub-apertures share one focus and form a true image — the design of segmented giants like the ELT and of exoplanet imagers like the ExoLife Finder (ELF) and its pathfinder SELF. On the right, a Michelson combiner: beams are carried to a mixer that records the fringe for each pair — the design of the VLTI, and (digitally) of the VLBI correlator.

The common thread, one more time

Fizeau and Michelson differ only in where the beams overlap, not in what happens: coherent fields are summed, and a delay is tuned so they add in phase. The Fizeau image plane sums them optically and instantly across a field; the Michelson combiner — and the digital correlator — sums them baseline by baseline. The choice is a trade between field of view and how long and sparse a baseline you can reach.

A Fizeau telescope built to image life: SELF & ELF

The clearest modern Fizeau design is Jeff Kuhn's ExoLife Finder (ELF) — a ~50-metre-scale telescope made not of one mirror but of many independent thin mirrors arranged in a sparse ring and combined at a common focus. Its 3.5-metre pathfinder, SELF (Small-ELF), with 15 sub-apertures of 0.5 m each, is under construction at the Teide Observatory in Tenerife (first light targeted for 2026). The mirrors are made extraordinarily light by curvature polishing — laser-stressing float glass into shape, as thin as 15 mm — and held in tensegrity frames of compression "bones" and tension "tendons," driving the mass-per-area down from ~24 t/m² (the 1949 Hale telescope) toward under 3 t/m². Crucially, each sub-aperture is co-phased point by point: a small adaptive secondary mirror tweaks its piston, tip and tilt, while machine-learning wavefront sensing and photonic lanterns read the alignment from the focal-plane speckle. Hold the phasing to ~0.01 radian (~60 nm) and a high-order coronagraph can dig a dark hole in the star's glare down to ~10⁻⁸ contrast — deep enough to image a planet like ε Eridani b and search its infrared light for biosignatures. See Kuhn et al., “Creating ground-based telescopes for imaging interferometry: SELF and ELF,” Light: Advanced Manufacturing (2025).

The payoff · erasing a star to see its planet

Phasing doesn't only let you add light up. Run it backwards and you can make light cancel exactly where you choose — switching off a blinding star so the faint world beside it can finally be seen.

Do something impossible · Subtract a star

Nulling interferometry: hiding a star to reveal a planet

The coronagraph idea · Bracewell nulling · using polarization to pull a planet out of the glare

What was known

A star can outshine its planet by a billion to one. Even with perfect resolution, the planet drowns in the star's glare. But interferometry gives a new handle: because we control the phase of the combined beams, we can choose to make the starlight interfere destructively — to cancel it.

The idea

Add an exact half-wave (180°) phase shift to one arm of the interferometer. Light arriving straight on-axis — from the star — now meets its twin perfectly out of step and vanishes: a deep central null. This is nulling interferometry (Bracewell's idea), the interferometric cousin of the coronagraph that blocks a star to expose what's beside it.

The test

A planet sits at a slightly different angle on the sky, so its light enters with a slightly different geometric path — a different phase. It does not fall in the null. Tune the baseline so the planet's angle lands on a bright fringe while the star sits in the dark, and the planet's photons survive the cancellation that erased the star.

What it founded

There's a second handle too: a planet shines by reflected starlight, which is partly polarized, while the star's own light is not. Filtering for that polarized signal isolates the planet's photons still further. Together, nulling and polarimetry are how interferometry hunts for worlds — and, one day, the chemical fingerprints of life in their light.

Null the star, keep the planet

A 180° phase shift puts a deep null on-axis, where the star sits — its transmission drops toward zero. The planet, at a small angle off-axis, lands somewhere on the fringe pattern. Tune the baseline so the planet falls on a bright fringe while the star stays buried in the null, and watch the planet's throughput climb even as the star is erased.

∴ The reasoning: why a planet escapes the null

The null is exactly on-axis; everything off-axis sees a different phase.

  1. With a 180° shift applied, two beams cancel for light arriving straight on (zero path difference) — the star, at the centre, is suppressed to a deep null.
  2. Light from an angle $\theta$ arrives with an extra geometric path $B\theta$, i.e. an extra phase $2\pi B\theta/\lambda$. That phase pulls it out of the null and onto the fringe pattern $T(\theta)=\sin^2(\pi B\theta/\lambda)$.
  3. Choose $B$ so the planet's $\theta$ lands on a transmission peak ($B\theta\approx\lambda/2$) while the star stays at $T=0$. The star is gone; the planet comes through. Its polarized reflected light gives a further way to confirm it.
Conclusion: the same phase control that builds a fringe can be aimed to destroy one exactly on a star — turning interferometry into a planet-finder.
