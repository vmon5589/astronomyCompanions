The stars at the edge
were moving far too fast

In the 1970s Vera Rubin pointed a spectrograph at the outskirts of spiral galaxies and asked a quiet question: how fast do the outer stars orbit? Newton said they should crawl, like Neptune crawls around the Sun. They didn't. Every galaxy she measured spun fast all the way to the edge of the light — and beyond. Either most of each galaxy's mass is invisible, or gravity itself is wrong. This companion retraces her path, and builds every tool she needed: orbits, Doppler shifts, tilted-disk geometry, the moving Earth, and differential rotation.

Chapter 1 · 1928 – 1965 · Choosing an unfashionable problem

Vera Rubin and the question nobody was fighting over

Vassar '48 · Cornell '51 · Georgetown '54 (under George Gamow) · Carnegie DTM from 1965, with Kent Ford's image-tube spectrograph

The path

Vera Rubin (1928–2016) was the only astronomy graduate in Vassar's class of 1948. Princeton never sent her the graduate catalogue she requested — its astronomy program would not admit women until 1975 — so she went to Cornell, and at 22 presented her master's work to the American Astronomical Society: tentative evidence that galaxies share extra, sideways bulk motions on top of the universal expansion. The reception was hostile. Her Georgetown PhD (1954, supervised by George Gamow) showed that galaxies are not scattered randomly but clump — also coolly received, and vindicated decades later as large-scale structure became a field of its own.

The strategy

Twice burned by controversial topics, Rubin made a deliberate choice that turned out to be the most consequential of her career: she picked a problem so plain that, in her words, nobody would bother her about it — how do spiral galaxies rotate? Astronomers "knew" the answer in outline. Measuring it carefully, point by point along a galaxy's disk, was considered worthy, careful, and dull. It was perfect.

The instrument

In 1965 she joined the Carnegie Institution's Department of Terrestrial Magnetism (DTM), partnering with Kent Ford, who had built something rare: an image-tube spectrograph. A photocathode amplified the incoming light electronically before it reached the photographic plate, cutting exposure times roughly tenfold. Spectra of the faint outer reaches of a galaxy's disk — previously a multi-night ordeal — became a few hours' work. The same year, Rubin became the first woman authorized to observe at Palomar Observatory.

Her hypothesis

The working assumption was simple and almost universally shared: light traces mass. A spiral galaxy's light is concentrated in its central bulge, so its mass should be too — and then the outer stars should behave exactly like the outer planets of the Solar System: the farther out, the slower, falling off as $v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$ (the Keplerian decline Chapter 2 derives). Andromeda would be the Solar System writ large. That is the hypothesis Rubin and Ford set out, methodically and without fanfare, to confirm.

∴ The hypothesis, stated precisely

"The outer stars should slow down" follows from two assumptions and one theorem — each of which the data could, in principle, break.

  1. Photograph the galaxy: the surface brightness of a spiral falls off steeply from the centre; by a few disk scale-lengths out, the starlight has essentially run out. Most of the light is central.
  2. Assume light traces mass: if a solar-mass of stars emits roughly a solar luminosity everywhere (a constant mass-to-light ratio), the mass map is just the light map rescaled. Most of the mass is then central too.
  3. Apply Newton: a star orbiting outside almost all of the mass feels the galaxy as a single central point (the shell theorem, Chapter 2) — precisely a planet's situation. Its speed must follow $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$.
  4. The prediction: beyond the bright inner regions, measured orbital speed should fall with radius — Mercury fast, Neptune slow. Plotted as $v$ against $r$ — a rotation curve — the outer half should sag like a falling sigh.
What's at stake: step 2 is the load-bearing assumption. If the measured curve refuses to fall, then either mass does not follow light — there is matter that doesn't shine — or step 3, Newtonian gravity itself, fails at galactic scales. Chapter 7 is the verdict; Chapters 2–6 build the tools needed to render it.

What it actually takes to "just measure the rotation"

Nothing in a telescope reads out velocity directly. To turn photons into a rotation curve you need the whole chain this companion builds: a physical yardstick connecting speed to mass (orbits, Chapter 2); a speedometer that works at two million light-years (Doppler shifts, Chapter 3); the geometry to undo the tilt of a disk seen at an angle (projection, Chapter 4); the bookkeeping to subtract your own spinning, orbiting observatory (the moving observer, Chapter 5); and the realisation that a galaxy does not turn like a wheel (differential rotation, Chapter 6). Every term in the final equation has its own chapter.

"In a spiral galaxy, the ratio of dark-to-light matter is about a factor of ten. That's probably a good number for the ratio of our ignorance to knowledge."

— Vera Rubin, looking back on what the quiet problem revealed

Chapter 2 · The physics · Newton's clockwork

What gravity predicts: the farther out, the slower

Kepler's harmonies · Newton's inverse square · the Solar System as the test case every galaxy was expected to copy

What Newton says

Since Newton, the speed of anything in a circular orbit has been a solved problem. Gravity pulls with force $F = GMm/r^2$ — doubling the distance quarters the pull — and a body moving in a circle requires a centre-pointing (centripetal) acceleration of $v^2/r$ to keep bending its path. Set the two equal and the orbital speed pops out: $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$.

Read backwards, it's a scale

That square root is a measurement device. Read it backwards: if you can measure how fast something orbits at radius $r$, you can weigh everything interior to its orbit, $M(<r) = v^2 r / G$ — without visiting, without waiting, without seeing the mass itself. A rotation curve is a set of scales two million light-years long.

The Solar System obeys

It does, beautifully. Essentially all the mass sits in the Sun, so $M(<r)$ is the same for every planet and $v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$: Mercury races at 47 km/s, Earth cruises at 29.8, Neptune crawls at 5.4. This falling shape — the Keplerian decline — is what the phrase "rotation curve of the Solar System" looks like, and what every spiral galaxy was expected to imitate outside its bright bulge.

What she expected of galaxies

Outside a spiral's bright bulge the starlight — and so, presumably, the mass — has mostly run out, leaving the outer stars in the planets' situation: orbiting a central hoard. Rubin's expectation was this chapter's curve transplanted to galactic scale: a Keplerian decline beyond the luminous core. Keep the reverse reading in your pocket, though — a flat measured curve would force $M(<r) = v^2r/G$ to grow in lockstep with radius — because Chapter 7 will need it.

∴ Deriving $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$ — and reading it backwards

One balance of forces, then one algebraic flip.

  1. Circular motion needs a centre-pointing pull. A body moving at constant speed $v$ on a circle of radius $r$ is constantly changing direction; turning the velocity vector at the right rate requires acceleration $a = v^2/r$ aimed at the centre. (Faster speed or tighter circle ⇒ harder turn — both appear in the formula.)
  2. Gravity supplies it. The only force available is $F = GMm/r^2$, so by Newton's second law: $\dfrac{GMm}{r^2} = \dfrac{m v^2}{r}$.
  3. The star's own mass cancels — $m$ divides out, which is why a pebble and a star at the same radius orbit at the same speed. Solve: $$v(r) = \sqrt{\frac{G\,M(<r)}{r}}.$$
  4. Flip it: $M(<r) = \dfrac{v^2\,r}{G}$. Speed and radius — two measurable numbers — weigh everything inside the orbit. If $M$ is all at the centre (Solar System), $v$ falls as $1/\sqrt{r}$. If $v$ stays flat, then $M(<r)$ must grow $\propto r$. Hold that thought until Chapter 7.
Takeaway: a rotation curve is not a picture of motion — it is a weighing of mass, radius by radius. The shape of $v(r)$ is a direct readout of how matter is distributed, visible or not.

The whole chapter in NumPy — the Solar System curve, and the scales run backwards:

import numpy as np

G = 4.301e-6        # gravitational constant in astronomer units: kpc (km/s)^2 / Msun

# --- Solar System: all mass central, so v falls as 1/sqrt(r) ---
r_au  = np.linspace(0.3, 31, 400)
v     = 29.78 / np.sqrt(r_au)          # km/s  (Earth: r=1 AU -> 29.78 km/s)

# --- the scales, run backwards: weigh a galaxy from its curve ---
v_meas, r_meas = 220.0, 8.2            # km/s at kpc  (the Sun, in the Milky Way)
M_enc = v_meas**2 * r_meas / G         # -> 9.2e10 solar masses inside the Sun's orbit

Why only the mass inside the orbit counts — the shell theorem

Step 4 above quietly wrote $M(<r)$, the enclosed mass, not the total. That is Newton's shell theorem: a spherically symmetric shell of matter pulls on anything outside it exactly as if the shell were a point at its centre — and pulls on anything inside it not at all (the near side's stronger tug is exactly cancelled by the far side's greater amount). So a star at radius $r$ feels only the matter interior to $r$, gathered to a point. That is what lets a rotation curve weigh a galaxy shell by shell, like an onion — and why mass outside the last measured point stays invisible to this method. (A flattened disk breaks the symmetry slightly; the correction is tens of percent, not factors — far too small to rescue the hypothesis in Chapter 7.)

Chapter 3 · The tool · A speedometer made of light

How you clock a star you will never visit

Doppler 1842 · Huggins 1868 · the Hα line at 656.28 nm — the workhorse of every Rubin & Ford spectrum

The ruler

Atoms emit and absorb light at exact, repeatable wavelengths — hydrogen's red Hα line sits at 656.28 nm, here, in Andromeda, everywhere (the fingerprint story is the heart of the spectroscopy companion). An exact, known wavelength is a ruler: if the line shows up somewhere else in the spectrum, something moved it.

The speedometer

Motion along the line of sight stretches or squeezes the wave. A source receding at speed $v$ lengthens every wavelength by the fraction $\Delta\lambda/\lambda = v/c$ (redshift); an approaching source shortens it (blueshift). Invert it and light becomes a speedometer: $v = c\,\Delta\lambda/\lambda$. A shift you can measure on a photographic plate yields a velocity in km/s.

Her targets: HII regions

Rubin and Ford didn't target individual stars — too faint. They targeted HII regions: glowing knots of hydrogen gas lit up by hot young stars, strung along the spiral arms like streetlamps, each blazing in narrow emission lines (Hα and its neighbours). Put the spectrograph slit across the galaxy and each HII region stamps a sharp, easily-measured line onto the plate — at its Doppler shift.

Two readings in one spectrum

One spectrum, two readings. The galaxy's lines as a whole sit displaced by its systemic velocity — the motion of the entire galaxy toward or away from us (Andromeda: ≈300 km/s toward, one of the few blueshifted galaxies). On top of that, lines from one side of the disk sit slightly blue of systemic, the other side slightly red: that differential is the rotation. The rest of this companion is about reading it correctly.

∴ Why $\Delta\lambda/\lambda = v/c$ — crests left behind

The Doppler formula is bookkeeping about where wave crests get emitted.

  1. A resting source emits one crest every period $T$, each from the same spot; crests travel out spaced $\lambda = cT$ apart.
  2. A source receding at $v$ emits each crest from a point $vT$ farther away than the last. Successive crests are therefore born $vT$ farther apart: spacing $\lambda' = cT + vT$.
  3. The fractional stretch is $\dfrac{\lambda'-\lambda}{\lambda} = \dfrac{vT}{cT} = \dfrac{v}{c}$. Approaching sources give the same with a minus sign — crests bunch up.
  4. Invert for the speedometer: $v = c\,\dfrac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda_0}$, valid for $v\ll c$ — comfortably true at galactic speeds (220 km/s is 0.07% of $c$).
Sensitivity check: a typical rotation speed of 220 km/s shifts Hα by $\Delta\lambda = 656.28\,\text{nm}\times(220/299{,}793) \approx 0.48$ nm — about five hundred line-widths of a good spectrograph's precision. Easily measurable in the 1970s; the bottleneck was always light, which is what Ford's image tube fixed.

The speedometer in three lines:

c       = 299_792.458          # km/s
lam0    = 656.28               # nm, hydrogen-alpha at rest

lam_obs = lam0 * (1 + v/c)     # where the line lands for radial velocity v
v       = c * (lam_obs - lam0) / lam0    # ...and the measurement, inverted

The fine print: only line-of-sight motion shifts the line

A source moving across your line of sight — however fast — neither stretches nor squeezes the crests travelling toward you: to first order in $v/c$, transverse motion produces no Doppler shift at all. The spectrograph measures only the component of velocity pointed along the line from telescope to source, called the radial velocity. This is not a footnote; it is the whole reason Chapter 4 exists. A rotating galaxy presents different fractions of its rotation along your line of sight depending on its tilt and on where in the disk you look — and unless you undo that projection, you systematically under-read every speed.

Show solution
  1. Solve for the shift. $\Delta\lambda=\lambda_0\,\dfrac{v}{c}$.
  2. Substitute. $\Delta\lambda=656.28\times\dfrac{220}{299{,}792}=656.28\times7.34\times10^{-4}$.
  3. Evaluate. $\Delta\lambda=0.482$ nm, so the line lands at $656.76$ nm.
Answer: $\Delta\lambda\approx+0.48$ nm (observed at $656.76$ nm) — tiny next to the wavelength itself, but hundreds of times a good spectrograph's precision. This single shift is the raw material of every rotation curve.

  2. Apply the formula. $v=2.998\times10^{5}\times\dfrac{-0.66}{656.28}$.
  3. Evaluate. $v=-301$ km/s.

  2. Evaluate. $v=137$ km/s.
Answer: about $137$ km/s of recession relative to centre — the differential between the two sides of the disk is the rotation Rubin set out to chart (before correcting for tilt in Chapter 4).
Chapter 4 · The geometry · Reading a spinning disk seen at an angle

Inclination, projection, and the velocity field

Why a face-on galaxy hides its rotation completely — and how $v_{obs} = v_{sys} + v_c \sin i \cos\theta$ undoes the tilt

A disk seen at an angle

A spiral galaxy is a thin circular disk, but the sky almost never shows it face-on. Seen at an inclination $i$ (0° = face-on, 90° = edge-on), the circle projects into an ellipse: the axis along the tilt keeps its full length (the major axis), the perpendicular one shrinks by $\cos i$. The apparent shape is therefore a protractor: measure the axis ratio $b/a$ on a photograph and the tilt follows, $\cos i = b/a$.

The double projection

Chapter 3's fine print now bites: the spectrograph reads only the line-of-sight component of motion. A star's circular velocity $v_c$ is a vector lying in the disk plane; project it twice — once for the disk's tilt, once for where the star sits around the disk — and what survives is $$v_{obs} = v_{sys} + v_c(R)\,\sin i\,\cos\theta,$$ with $\theta$ the star's angle around the disk measured from the major axis.

The spider pattern

That formula makes a sharp, checkable prediction about the velocity field — the map of $v_{obs}$ across the whole face of the galaxy. Maximum approach on one end of the major axis, maximum recession on the other, and exactly systemic everywhere along the minor axis ($\cos\theta = 0$: there the orbits run fully across the line of sight). The resulting butterfly pattern, the spider diagram, is the signature of a circularly rotating disk — and real galaxies wear it.

Her method at the telescope

Rubin's method drops out directly: lay the slit along the major axis, where $\cos\theta = \pm1$ and the rotation signal is largest, measure $v_{obs}(R)$, subtract $v_{sys}$, divide by $\sin i$ — and the true curve $v_c(R)$ is recovered. It also dictates target selection: face-on galaxies are useless ($\sin i \to 0$ kills the signal), edge-on ones hide their outskirts behind their own dust. The Sc spirals of her great 1970s campaigns were chosen at comfortable middle inclinations.

∴ Projecting the rotation — where $\sin i \cos\theta$ comes from

Two projections, applied one after the other, each a plain right-triangle cosine (the trig is built from scratch in the spectroscopy companion's trig supplement).

  1. Set the stage. Put the galaxy's centre at the origin; let the disk be tilted by $i$ about the major axis. A star at radius $R$, at azimuth $\theta$ around the disk (measured in the disk plane, from the major axis), moves on its circle at speed $v_c(R)$, its velocity tangent to the circle.
  2. Resolve the velocity in the disk plane. The tangential velocity splits into a component parallel to the major axis and one parallel to the minor axis. The component that will matter — the one parallel to the major axis — has size $v_c\cos\theta$ (at $\theta=0$, on the major axis, the orbit runs straight along it; a quarter-turn later, $\theta=90°$, it runs fully sideways).
  3. Tip the disk toward you. Tilting by $i$ leans that in-plane component partly into the line of sight: it acquires a line-of-sight projection $\sin i$. (Face-on, $i=0$: the whole orbit stays in the plane of the sky, $\sin i = 0$, nothing survives. Edge-on, $i=90°$: the major-axis component points squarely at you.) The minor-axis component, for its part, never gains a line-of-sight part — it stays in the sky plane at any tilt.
  4. Add the galaxy's own motion. The whole disk rides on the galaxy's systemic velocity $v_{sys}$ (Chapter 3). Total: $v_{obs} = v_{sys} + v_c(R)\sin i\cos\theta$.
Recovering the truth: on the major axis $\cos\theta=\pm1$, so $v_c(R) = \dfrac{v_{obs}(R) - v_{sys}}{\sin i}$. One photograph (for $i$), one slit spectrum (for $v_{obs}$), and the genuine rotation curve falls out. Every velocity in Chapter 7's flat curves has been through this formula.

The projection, and its inverse, in NumPy:

import numpy as np

# forward: what the spectrograph sees at disk position (R, theta)
v_obs = v_sys + v_c(R) * np.sin(i) * np.cos(theta)

# inclination from the photographed axis ratio b/a:
i = np.arccos(b / a)                  # circle tilted by i -> ellipse, minor = D*cos(i)

# inverse: Rubin's reduction, along the major axis (cos theta = +/-1)
v_true = (v_obs - v_sys) / np.sin(i)

Reading the tilt off a photograph — and its limits

A circle of diameter $D$ tilted by $i$ projects to an ellipse with major axis $D$ and minor axis $D\cos i$ — the familiar foreshortening of a coin turned in your fingers. So one measurement on an image, $b/a$, hands you $i = \arccos(b/a)$ with no spectroscopy at all. Two caveats astronomers carry: real disks have thickness, so even a perfectly edge-on galaxy shows $b/a \approx 0.1$–0.2 rather than zero; and warped or non-circular disks can masquerade as a different tilt. Near face-on the method is also treacherous for velocities: at $i=10°$, $\sin i = 0.17$, so dividing by it multiplies every velocity error by six. Rotation-curve work lives at $i \approx 40°$–75°.

Practice problems

1. A disk galaxy rotates at a true speed $v_c=210$ km/s and is seen at inclination $i=65°$. Using the major-axis projection $v_{obs}=v_c\sin i$ (where $\cos\theta=1$), what peak velocity does the spectrograph record?

Show solution
  1. Project onto the line of sight. $v_{obs}=v_c\sin i=210\sin 65°$.
  2. Evaluate. $\sin 65°=0.906$, so $v_{obs}=210\times0.906=190$ km/s.
Answer: about $190$ km/s — the tilt throws away $\sin 65°$ of the motion, so the raw reading under-reports the true speed until you divide it back out.

2. On a photograph a spiral's image is an ellipse with axis ratio $b/a=0.45$. Using $\cos i=b/a$, find its inclination — no spectroscopy needed.

Show solution
  1. Invert the foreshortening relation. $i=\arccos(b/a)=\arccos(0.45)$.
  2. Evaluate. $i=63.3°$.
Answer: $i\approx63°$ — a comfortable middle inclination, exactly the regime Rubin chose for her Sc spirals (not too face-on to kill the signal, not too edge-on to hide behind dust).

3. A slit laid along the major axis of a galaxy at $i=50°$ reads $v_{obs}=180$ km/s (systemic already subtracted). Recover the true rotation speed via $v_c=v_{obs}/\sin i$.

Show solution
  1. Divide out the tilt. $v_c=\dfrac{v_{obs}}{\sin i}=\dfrac{180}{\sin 50°}$.
  2. Evaluate. $\sin 50°=0.766$, so $v_c=\dfrac{180}{0.766}=235$ km/s.
Answer: about $235$ km/s — the de-projection that turns a raw plate reading into a point on the true rotation curve. Every velocity in Chapter 7's flat curves passed through this step.
Chapter 5 · The observer · A spinning seat on an orbiting planet

Subtracting ourselves: the moving observatory

Earth's spin (0.46 km/s) · Earth's orbit (29.8 km/s) · the Sun's galactic orbit (~220 km/s) — every spectrum is taken from a moving platform

The spinning platform

The spectrograph never stands still. It rides a planet spinning at up to 0.46 km/s, which orbits the Sun at 29.8 km/s, while the Sun itself sails around the Galaxy at roughly 220 km/s. Radial velocities add along the line of sight, so the raw Doppler reading of any object is the object's motion plus the projection of all of ours. Astronomers since the 19th century have subtracted these terms as routinely as taring a balance.

The budget

Each motion of ours is known precisely — orbital mechanics tells us Earth's velocity vector at any instant to high accuracy. So compute its component toward the target and subtract: the result is the velocity as seen from the Solar System's centre of mass, the barycentric correction. What cannot be computed away (the Sun's own galactic orbit) is handled differently — see the mathnote below, one of the slyest points in this whole subject.

January vs July

Observe the same galaxy in January and again in July: the raw velocity differs by up to ±29.8 km/s as Earth swings around its orbit — a pure annual sine wave stamped on every uncorrected measurement, scaled by how close the target lies to the plane of Earth's orbit. The daily spin adds a further ±0.46 km/s wobble. Against a 220 km/s rotation signal, an uncorrected 30 km/s is a 14% error — fatal to the science.

What survives the subtraction

Modern pipelines compute the barycentric correction to better than a metre per second — it is the enabling arithmetic of exoplanet hunting, where the signal is a few m/s. For Rubin's work the requirement was gentler but absolute: every plate reduced to the barycentre, every galaxy's systemic velocity then subtracted, leaving only the internal rotation. What survives that double subtraction is the curve.

∴ The full velocity budget of one measured line

Write down everything that moves between a hydrogen atom in Andromeda and your detector, largest first.

  1. The Sun's galactic orbit, ~220 km/s. Carries the whole Solar System; constant on any human timescale. (Handled by absorption into $v_{sys}$ — see below.)
  2. The galaxy's systemic velocity, tens to hundreds of km/s. The target's own drift through space, $v_{sys}$ — measured, not assumed, as the mean of both sides of the disk (Chapter 4: it is the velocity on the minor axis).
  3. Earth's orbital motion, ±29.8 km/s with a one-year period. Its projection on the line of sight is $29.8\cos\beta\,\sin(2\pi t/\text{yr} + \varphi)$ for a target at angle $\beta$ out of Earth's orbital plane — computable to high precision for any date, and subtracted.
  4. Earth's rotation, ±0.46 km/s with a one-day period (scaled by the observatory's latitude and the target's position). Also computed and subtracted.
  5. What remains: $v_{rot}\sin i\cos\theta$ — the galaxy's internal rotation, projected per Chapter 4. The signal is what's left when every known motion has been pulled off the pile.
The discipline: subtract what you can compute (Earth's spin and orbit), measure-and-subtract what you can't (systemic velocity), and the residual is the physics. Get any term wrong and it forges a phantom rotation.

The barycentric correction over a year

The curve is the line-of-sight component of Earth's orbital velocity toward one target across a full year — the correction every spectrum of that target needs. Drag the target away from Earth's orbital plane (the ecliptic) and the amplitude shrinks by $\cos\beta$: a target straight above the orbit's pole ($\beta = 90°$) sees Earth moving only sideways, and the annual term vanishes. The marker is the date of your observation; the diurnal (daily spin) term is drawn magnified in the thin band below.

The sly point: why our 220 km/s galactic ride doesn't ruin the curve

The Sun's orbit around the Milky Way is by far our largest motion — yet it never appears in the corrections above. Why doesn't it wreck the measurement? Because over the years of an observing campaign it is constant: it shifts every line of a given target by the same fixed amount, indistinguishable from the target's own drift. It therefore gets absorbed into the measured systemic velocity and subtracted along with it. A constant offset cannot forge a rotation signal — only motions that change during the campaign (Earth's day and year) can do that, and those are exactly the ones computed and removed. The same logic in reverse is Chapter 6's subject: for stars inside our own Galaxy, the Sun's orbital motion does not cancel, and its fingerprints across the sky are how the Milky Way's rotation was discovered from within.

Show solution

  2. Evaluate. $\cos 20°=0.940$, so amplitude $=29.79\times0.940=28.0$ km/s.

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. error $=\dfrac{25}{220}\times100\%$.
  2. Evaluate. $=11.4\%$.
Answer: about $11\%$ — an enormous, science-killing error that a one-line computed subtraction removes. This is why every plate is reduced to the barycentre.

3. The annual correction follows $v=A\sin(2\pi t/\text{yr})$ with amplitude $A=28$ km/s. What is the correction at a phase of $45°$ through the orbital cycle?

  2. Multiply by the amplitude. $v=28\times0.707=19.8$ km/s.
Answer: about $19.8$ km/s — a sizeable offset on this date. Because it changes through the year (unlike the Sun's constant 220 km/s galactic ride), it must be computed and subtracted for each observation.
Chapter 6 · The motion · A galaxy is not a wheel

Differential rotation — every radius keeps its own time

Solid bodies vs orbiting swarms · Oort's 1927 proof that the Milky Way shears · why spiral arms can't be made of the same stars for long

Not a wheel

A wheel, a vinyl record, a dinner plate: solid-body rotation. Every part shares one angular speed $\Omega$ (degrees per minute), so the outer rim moves faster in km/s exactly in proportion to its radius, $v = \Omega R$, and the whole object turns as a piece. It is tempting to picture a galaxy this way — a hundred billion stars bolted to a turning disk. Nothing could be more wrong: there are no bolts. Nothing connects star to star.

Every radius its own clock

Each star is its own independent satellite, on its own orbit, with its own period set only by the mass interior to it (Chapter 2). The angular speed becomes a function of radius, $\Omega(R) = v(R)/R$ — and unless $v$ rises strictly in proportion to $R$, inner stars complete their laps sooner and overtake the outer ones. That shearing motion is differential rotation. The Sun laps the Galaxy in about 230 million years; stars at half our radius lap it in roughly half the time.

Seen from the inside

How do you detect your own galaxy's rotation while riding it? Chapter 5's logic flips: for stars inside the Milky Way, the Sun's 220 km/s does not cancel out — nearby stars at different galactic longitudes $\ell$ share it to different degrees. Differential rotation leaves a precise fingerprint: a radial-velocity pattern $v_r = A\,d\,\sin 2\ell$ that runs through two full cycles around the sky. Jan Oort found exactly this double sine in 1927 — proof from the inside that the Milky Way rotates, and rotates differentially. A solid-body galaxy would show no pattern at all.

The winding problem

Differential rotation has a famous casualty: paint a straight line of stars from the centre to the edge and within a few rotations it winds into a tightening spiral — and after a few more, into an indistinct whorl. Real spiral arms survive for many rotations, so they cannot be material structures; they are density waves, patterns the stars drift through like cars through a traffic jam. And note the punchline for our story: even the flat rotation curves of Chapter 7 are strongly differential — $v$ constant still means $\Omega = v/R$ falling — so none of this rescues the Keplerian expectation.

∴ Three ways a disk can spin — and how to tell them apart

Define $\Omega(R) = v(R)/R$, the angular speed at radius $R$. The shape of $v(R)$ fixes everything.

  1. Solid body ($v \propto R$): $\Omega$ is the same everywhere. No shear, no winding, no Oort pattern. Real galaxies do behave this way near their centres, where the rising part of the rotation curve is roughly linear.
  2. Flat curve ($v = $ const): $\Omega \propto 1/R$. A star at half your radius has twice your angular speed — strong shear, even though every star moves at the same km/s. This is the regime Rubin's galaxies turned out to live in.
  3. Keplerian ($v \propto 1/\sqrt{R}$): $\Omega \propto R^{-3/2}$ — the strongest shear of the three. The Solar System: Mercury laps Neptune hundreds of times per Neptune-year.
  4. The discriminator: the local shear is what Oort's coefficient measures, $A = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{v}{R} - \tfrac{dv}{dR}\right)$ evaluated at the Sun. Solid body gives $A = 0$ (the pattern vanishes); a flat curve gives $A = v/2R \approx 13.5$ km/s per kpc — close to what is actually measured in the solar neighbourhood.
Takeaway: "the galaxy rotates" is not one statement but a whole function $v(R)$ — and the local sky, read carefully enough, betrays its derivative. Oort measured the Milky Way's shear from a few thousand nearby stars decades before anyone could see our galaxy from outside.

Why twice the longitude — the sin 2ℓ pattern, unpacked

Look from the Sun toward galactic longitude $\ell$ at a nearby star. Both of you orbit; what the spectrograph reads is the difference of the two velocities, projected on the line between you. Work through the geometry and two effects multiply: how much the star's orbital speed differs from yours (set by how much farther in or out it sits, $\propto\cos$-like in $\ell$) and how much of that difference points along your line of sight ($\propto\sin$-like in $\ell$). A product of a sine and a cosine is a sine of twice the angle — hence $v_r = A\,d\,\sin 2\ell$: zero toward the galactic centre ($\ell=0$), zero straight ahead along the orbit ($\ell=90°$), maximum approach and recession at the diagonals ($\ell = 45°, 135°, \dots$), the whole pattern repeating twice per circuit of the sky. Its amplitude grows with the star's distance $d$ — shear accumulates with separation — which is also why the effect hid until proper-motion and velocity surveys reached beyond the nearest stars.

Practice problems

1. The Milky Way's curve is flat at $v=220$ km/s. At the Sun's radius $R=8.2$ kpc, find the angular speed $\Omega=v/R$ and the orbital period $P=2\pi R/v$. (Use $1\text{ kpc}/(\text{km/s})=977.8$ Myr.)

Show solution
  1. Angular speed. $\Omega=\dfrac{v}{R}=\dfrac{220}{8.2}=26.8$ km/s per kpc.
  2. Period. $P=\dfrac{2\pi R}{v}=\dfrac{2\pi(8.2)}{220}=0.234$ kpc/(km/s).
  3. Convert to Myr. $P=0.234\times977.8=229$ Myr.
Answer: $\Omega\approx26.8$ km/s/kpc, $P\approx230$ Myr — the Sun's "galactic year." A flat curve still means $\Omega$ falls outward, so the Galaxy shears.

2. For a flat rotation curve, the Oort shear constant is $A=\tfrac12\!\left(\dfrac{v}{R}-\dfrac{dv}{dR}\right)$ with $dv/dR=0$, i.e. $A=v/(2R)$. Compute $A$ at the Sun ($v=220$ km/s, $R=8.2$ kpc).

Show solution
  1. Flat curve sets $dv/dR=0$. So $A=\dfrac{v}{2R}$.
  2. Substitute. $A=\dfrac{220}{2(8.2)}=\dfrac{220}{16.4}=13.4$ km/s per kpc.
Answer: $A\approx13.4$ km/s/kpc — comfortably close to the measured solar-neighbourhood value (~15), confirming the local curve is flat, not Keplerian (which would give a much larger $A$).

3. Oort's all-sky fingerprint is $v_r=A\,d\,\sin 2\ell$. With $A=13.4$ km/s/kpc, what radial velocity does a star at distance $d=2$ kpc show toward galactic longitude $\ell=45°$?

Show solution
  1. Evaluate the double-angle term. $\sin(2\times45°)=\sin 90°=1$.
  2. Multiply. $v_r=13.4\times2\times1=26.8$ km/s.
Answer: about $26.8$ km/s — the maximum of the $\sin 2\ell$ double wave, occurring at the diagonals ($\ell=45°,135°,\dots$). It is exactly this pattern Oort found in 1927, proving the Milky Way rotates differentially from the inside.

The toolkit is complete. Now the galaxies answer.

Orbits to predict, Doppler to measure, geometry to de-project, corrections to clean, differential rotation to interpret. Everything from here is what the data actually said — and what it forced astronomy to conclude.

Chapter 7 · 1970 – 1980 · The measurement

The curves refused to fall

Rubin & Ford 1970 (Andromeda, 67 HII regions) · Rubin, Ford & Thonnard 1978–1980 (21 Sc spirals) · the radio astronomers who pushed even farther

The expectation

Recall the stakes from Chapter 1: light traces mass, the bulge holds the light, so beyond the bright inner disk the curve must turn over and fall as $v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$. Sketches of "expected galaxy rotation" in textbooks of the day show exactly that — a rise through the bulge, a peak, and a long Keplerian sigh. Nobody considered the measurement urgent, because nobody doubted the answer.

1970: Andromeda

Rubin and Ford's first great target was M31, Andromeda — close enough to resolve individual HII regions. Sixty-seven of them, each yielding a Doppler velocity, traced the curve from the core out to roughly 24 kpc. It rose, flattened — and stayed flat to the last measured point. No turnover. The 1970 paper reported the curve without trumpeting an interpretation; the data simply ran out before the decline ever began.

1978–80: twenty-one galaxies

One galaxy might be a fluke. Through the late 1970s Rubin, Ford and Norbert Thonnard measured Sc spirals of every size — small, large, dim, luminous — and in 1980 published the systematic result: every single one of the 21 rotation curves was flat to the edge of the detectable light. Meanwhile radio astronomers (Morton Roberts on M31; Albert Bosma's 1978 thesis; later van Albada on NGC 3198) used the 21 cm line of cold hydrogen gas, which extends two to three times beyond the starlight — and the curves stayed flat out there too. The decline never came.

The arithmetic won't bend

Now run Chapter 2 backwards, as promised: $M(<r) = v^2 r/G$ with $v$ constant means the enclosed mass grows in direct proportion to radius — steadily, indefinitely, in regions where the starlight has fallen to nothing. Each new shell of each galaxy weighs the same as the last and shines ever less. The mass-to-light ratio climbs without limit. Either galaxies are wrapped in enormous halos of matter that emits no light, or Newtonian gravity fails at galactic scales. There is no third reading of a flat line.

∴ From a flat line to missing mass, in four forced steps

Nothing in this argument is subtle, which is precisely what made it so devastating.

  1. Measured: beyond a few kpc, $v(r) \approx$ constant — across 21 optical curves and every extended 21 cm curve. (After the corrections of Chapters 3–5: Doppler velocities, de-projected by $\sin i$, referenced to the barycentre, systemic velocity removed.)
  2. Newton: $M(<r) = v^2 r / G$. With $v$ fixed, $M(<r) \propto r$. At 30 kpc a typical Sc galaxy encloses several hundred billion solar masses — and is still gaining.
  3. The light: a spiral's surface brightness falls roughly exponentially with radius; integrate it and the total luminosity converges — the starlight adds up to a finite, centrally concentrated total. Light stops; mass doesn't.
  4. The collision: divide step 2 by step 3. The mass-to-light ratio of each successive shell rises from a few (normal stars) to tens and beyond (nothing visible at all). Matter that gravitates but does not shine outweighs everything we see, roughly ten to one.
The fork: accept the halo of unseen matter, or amend gravity itself (Chapter 8 weighs both). Note what is not in doubt: the measurement. Rubin's curves have been re-measured for fifty years by every technique invented since, and they are flat.

What a flat curve says about the halo's density — ρ ∝ 1/r²

The halo's run of density follows from the curve in two lines. Flat curve ⇒ $M(<r) \propto r$ ⇒ each spherical shell adds the same mass: $\dfrac{dM}{dr} = $ constant. But a shell's mass is its area times thickness times density, $dM = 4\pi r^2 \rho(r)\,dr$. For $4\pi r^2 \rho$ to stay constant while $r^2$ grows, the density must fall as $\boldsymbol{\rho(r) \propto 1/r^2}$ — the profile of an "isothermal sphere", the same mathematics as a self-gravitating ball of gas with constant temperature. So the dark halo isn't shapeless: the flat curves measure its density law, even though no photon has ever come from it. (Modern simulations refine this to profiles like NFW — $1/r$ at the centre, steeper than $1/r^2$ far out — but $1/r^2$ rules in the flat-curve region.)

Chapter 8 · The verdict · Most of the universe doesn't shine

Dark matter — from heresy to backbone of cosmology

Zwicky 1933 · Ostriker & Peebles 1973 · Rubin's curves 1970–80 · lensing, clusters & the CMB · the observatory that bears her name

Zwicky's ghost

Rubin's result had a forgotten ancestor. In 1933 Fritz Zwicky timed galaxies whirling inside the Coma cluster and found them moving far too fast for the visible mass to hold the cluster together — by his (over-rough) estimate a factor of hundreds, in modern terms tens. He named the missing ingredient dunkle Materie — dark matter — and was ignored for forty years: one eccentric's anomaly in one cluster. What changed in the 1970s was not the idea but the evidence: Rubin's flat curves were clean, repeated, systematic, and in everyone's favourite objects — ordinary spiral galaxies.

The halo

Theory had quietly arrived at the same place. In 1973 Ostriker and Peebles showed that a bare spinning disk of stars is violently unstable — left alone it buckles into a bar within a rotation or two. Embedding the disk in a massive, roughly spherical halo stabilizes it. Add Chapter 7's density law and the picture assembles itself: every spiral sits inside a dark halo with $\rho \propto 1/r^2$, holding the rotation curve flat and the disk intact, outweighing the stars roughly ten to one and extending several times beyond the visible edge. The luminous galaxy is froth on a dark sea.

The independent juries

Extraordinary claims got their independent trials, and dark matter kept winning. Gravitational lensing: the bending of background light weighs clusters with no orbital assumptions — same missing mass. Hot X-ray gas: its pressure-temperature balance weighs clusters a third way — same answer. The Bullet Cluster (2006): two clusters collided, the gas (most of the normal matter) slammed and stalled in the middle while the lensing mass sailed cleanly through with the galaxies — mass and ordinary matter visibly in different places. And the cosmic microwave background's acoustic peaks independently price the cosmic recipe: about five times more dark matter than everything atomic.

Her legacy

The standard cosmology that emerged — ΛCDM — is roughly 5% atoms, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy: Rubin's quiet measurement sits at the foundation of the claim that we have identified the ingredients of one part in twenty of the universe. She received the National Medal of Science in 1993 and died on 25 December 2016, famously without a Nobel Prize. The Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile — which saw first light in 2025 and will map billions of galaxies, in part through the weak lensing distortions dark matter imprints — is the first major US observatory named for a woman. Its core mission is, fittingly, a survey of the dark.

∴ Process of elimination: what the dark matter is not

"Unseen" first suggested mundane answers. One by one, the mundane answers failed independent tests.

  1. Cold gas? Hydrogen betrays itself — cold gas in the 21 cm line, warm gas in absorption against background quasars, hot gas in X-rays. Inventoried, all of it together falls far short of the halo masses.
  2. Faint stars, dead stars, black holes (MACHOs)? Compact halo objects passing in front of background stars cause tell-tale microlensing flickers. The MACHO/EROS/OGLE surveys of the 1990s–2000s watched millions of stars: far too few events. Halos are not made of dim star-stuff.
  3. Any form of atoms at all? Two completely independent meters agree on the total baryon budget of the universe: the yields of Big-Bang nucleosynthesis (deuterium especially) and the relative heights of the CMB acoustic peaks. Both cap atomic matter at about 5% of the cosmic total — a third of the matter that gravity demands. Most matter is not made of atoms.
  4. So: a new particle — or new gravity. The particle option (WIMPs, axions, and a broad family besides) has motivated forty years of underground detectors and collider searches; as of the mid-2020s, no confirmed detection. The gravity option is the mathnote below.
Where it stands: the gravitational evidence for dark matter is overwhelming and mutually corroborating across every scale from single galaxies to the whole observable universe; its identity remains entirely unknown. Rubin herself was at peace with either resolution — what she defended was the data.

The dissenting verdict: MOND

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (Milgrom, 1983) takes the other fork: below a critical acceleration $a_0 \approx 1.2\times10^{-10}$ m/s² — and galaxy outskirts are exactly that regime — gravity is postulated to weaken more slowly than $1/r^2$. Tuned with that single constant, MOND reproduces flat rotation curves automatically, and even predicts the tight observed relation between a galaxy's luminosity and its flat velocity (Tully–Fisher) more naturally than halos do. Why isn't it the consensus? It struggles where dark matter shines: galaxy clusters still need unseen mass even under MOND, the Bullet Cluster shows mass displaced from matter (hard to fake with modified gravity), and the CMB acoustic structure fits a universe with real collisionless dark matter. Most cosmologists read MOND as an intriguing regularity any final theory must explain — and a minority defend it vigorously. The fork Rubin exposed is, formally, still open.

"Fame is fleeting. My numbers mean more to me than my name. If astronomers are still using my data years from now, that's my greatest compliment."

— Vera Rubin. They are: her 1970 Andromeda velocities remain in use, and an observatory surveying the dark universe now carries her name.

The story of Vera Rubin's galaxy-rotation measurements and the dark-matter conclusion they forced, told as a story arc with the working tools built along the way: circular orbits and the enclosed-mass scales, Doppler velocimetry, tilted-disk projection geometry, the moving observer's velocity budget, and differential rotation. Every plot computes the real physics live. A sibling to the spectroscopy companion (how spectra are read — including the trigonometry supplement this document leans on).

Key papers retraced: Rubin & Ford 1970, ApJ 159, 379 (Andromeda, 67 HII regions) · Rubin, Ford & Thonnard 1978, ApJ 225, L107 and 1980, ApJ 238, 471 (21 Sc galaxies, all flat) · Zwicky 1933, Helv. Phys. Acta 6, 110 (Coma cluster, "dunkle Materie") · Ostriker & Peebles 1973 (disk stability) · Bosma 1978 & van Albada et al. 1985 (21 cm curves beyond the starlight).

Reference values used throughout: $G = 4.301\times10^{-6}$ kpc (km/s)² M☉⁻¹; Hα rest wavelength 656.28 nm; Earth orbital speed 29.79 km/s, equatorial spin 0.465 km/s; the Sun at $R_0 \approx 8.2$ kpc orbiting at $v_0 \approx 220$ km/s (period ≈ 230 Myr); Andromeda systemic velocity ≈ −300 km/s. Some illustrations are deliberately schematic — accurate in scaling and spirit, simplified in detail.
