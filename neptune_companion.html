On the night of 23–24 September 1846, Johann Galle pointed the Berlin Observatory's telescope at a patch of sky Urbain Le Verrier had calculated from his desk — and found an 8th-magnitude "star" less than 1° from the predicted spot. Neptune was discovered by mathematics before it was ever seen. This companion rebuilds the logic of that prediction step by step, with a real 3-body integration running live in your browser. The twist: the predicted orbit was badly wrong, and a geometric accident is the reason it worked anyway.

1766 · the expectation

The Titius–Bode "law": why everyone expected ~38 AU

An empirical rule that fit six worlds — and set a trap for the seventh

In 1766 Johann Titius noticed, and Johann Bode popularized, that the planets' distances from the Sun follow a simple geometric pattern. Write a sequence

$$a_n = 0.4 + 0.3 \times 2^{\,n}, \qquad n = -\infty,\,0,\,1,\,2,\,3,\,\dots$$

with $a$ in astronomical units (AU). The rule was a sensation: it nailed the known planets out to Saturn, predicted the "missing" planet at $n=3$ (2.8 AU, vindicated by Ceres in 1801), and when Uranus turned up in 1781 at 19.2 AU it landed almost exactly on the $n=6$ slot (19.6 AU). So when Uranus misbehaved, it was natural to assume the next planet sat at the next slot, $n=7 \Rightarrow$ ~38.8 AU. Adams and Le Verrier both started there.

1.1The pattern is pure doubling — and it has a give-away signature

The constant 0.4 is just a baseline; all the structure lives in 0.3 × 2ⁿ, where each step doubles the one before. A geometric sequence has a tell: take its logarithm and it becomes a straight line. Since $a_n - 0.4 = 0.3\cdot 2^{\,n}$,

$$\log_{10}(a_n - 0.4) = \log_{10}(0.3) + n\,\log_{10} 2,$$

a line of slope $\log_{10}2 \approx 0.301$. So the real test of the "law" is: subtract the 0.4 baseline from each planet's actual distance and plot it on a log axis. If the pattern is real, the planets land on a straight line.

The seed of the whole story

There was never a physical reason for the rule. From Venus to Uranus the fitted slope comes out near $0.30 \approx \log_{10}2$ — the pattern is genuinely there for six worlds, which is exactly why 18th-century astronomers bet a telescope on it. Then Neptune drops below the line: Bode predicts 38.8 AU for a planet that actually sits at 30.07 AU, a +29% error. Everyone expected Neptune up on the line, so that 38-AU error is where the predictions began — and it drives the punchline in Section 5.

From a numerology that worked to a force that had to be there

Bode's law set the expectation. The actual evidence was hiding in Uranus's own motion.

1820s–1840s · the evidence

Uranus would not stay on its predicted orbit

A real 3-body integration of Sun + Uranus + Neptune, running in your browser

Uranus (discovered 1781) should trace a fixed Keplerian ellipse. But by the 1820s–40s its observed positions drifted by a couple of arcminutes away from the best-fit orbit — first running ahead of schedule, then behind. The boldest explanation: an unseen outer planet tugging on it. Let's not hand-wave — let's integrate the gravity. We build a genuine planar 3-body model and integrate Newton's equations. Working units keep it clean:

$$\text{length}=\text{AU},\quad \text{time}=\text{yr},\quad \text{mass}=M_\odot \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; G M_\odot = 4\pi^2.$$

Then we run a second integration with Neptune deleted (Sun + Uranus only, a fixed ellipse). The difference between the two Uranus orbits is exactly "the perturbation an astronomer of 1840 could not explain."

the fingerprint

The O−C residual: rise, peak, reverse

Why this one curve shape "screams outer planet"

The quantity an 1840s astronomer plotted was O−C: Observed longitude (reality, with Neptune) minus Computed longitude (the Neptune-free ellipse they had fit). The O−C plot in the lab above is the actual shape of the clue. Here is how to read it:

Why the curve rises, peaks, then reverses sign

  1. Before conjunction — Neptune is ahead of Uranus on the track, so its gravity pulls Uranus forward. Uranus arrives early → O−C is positive.
  2. At conjunction (~1822) — the pull is purely radial (toward/away from the Sun), not along-track, so the longitude residual swings through its steepest change.
  3. After conjunction — Neptune is now behind, dragging Uranus back. Uranus runs late → O−C goes negative.
The payoff: this rise–peak–reverse signature is what no error in Uranus's own orbit could fake. It demands a body farther out, and the sign-flip even says roughly where along its orbit the perturber sits. That is the information Adams and Le Verrier inverted: from the residual, solve for the unseen planet's mass and position.

What "inverting the residual" means

Forward, the problem is: given Neptune's mass and orbit, predict Uranus's wobble — that's the integration in the lab above. Adams and Le Verrier ran it backwards: given the measured wobble, search the space of possible perturbers for the mass and orbital elements that reproduce it. It was months of hand computation. The output that mattered was a single number an observer could use: a heliocentric longitude — a direction in the sky to point the telescope.

The subtle, beautiful part: the right answer from the wrong orbit

The predictors never knew Neptune's distance. They assumed 38 AU from Bode's law. The real planet is at 30. Here is why the telescope still landed on it.

1846 · the lucky geometry

The perihelion accident

Why a 38-AU prediction still pointed at a 30-AU planet

The residual constrains mainly the perturber's direction and the timing of its tug — not cleanly its distance. To fit the same 1840s wobble with a planet placed too far out (38 AU), the math had to compensate: it pulled the hypothetical planet inward at that epoch by putting it near perihelion (closest approach) and giving the orbit extra eccentricity. The upshot:

The coincidence, stated plainly

In the 1840s, the predicted 38-AU planet near its perihelion and the real 30-AU planet sat at almost the same heliocentric longitude — the same direction in the sky. Le Verrier's predicted longitude was within ~1° of truth, even though his orbit was wrong. A telescope reads direction, not distance — so getting the longitude right to ~1° is all you need to see the planet.

The 1846 sightline

Both planets are placed at Neptune's real Sep-1846 direction (ecliptic longitude ≈ 327°). The real planet is near-circular at 30 AU. Push the predicted orbit's semi-major axis out and its eccentricity up — and watch how the perihelion distance $a_\text{pred}(1-e_\text{pred})$ has to drop back toward 30 AU to keep the predicted planet sitting on the same line of sight as the real one.

So how much was genius and how much was luck?

  1. Genuine triumph. Inverting the gravitational residual to recover the perturber's direction was a real, hard, correct calculation — Newtonian gravity run backwards.
  2. Lucky break. The bad 38-AU Bode distance forced a perihelion placement that happened to drop the predicted planet's current distance to ~30 AU and onto the real planet's sightline. A telescope only needs the direction.
Aftermath: within months astronomers tracked Neptune's real motion and found $a\approx 30$ AU, nearly circular. Bode's law was quietly abandoned as the numerology it always was. But the prediction stood as the great 19th-century vindication of Newtonian gravity — a planet found because the equations said it had to be there.

§Practice problems

1. The Titius–Bode slot for Neptune is $n=7$. Compute the predicted distance $a_7 = 0.4 + 0.3\times 2^{7}$ and its percentage error against Neptune's true 30.07 AU.

Show solution
  1. Power of two. $2^{7}=128$.
  2. Apply the rule. $a_7 = 0.4 + 0.3\times128 = 0.4 + 38.4 = 38.8$ AU.
  3. Error. $100\times(38.8-30.07)/30.07 = +29.0\%$.
Answer: 38.8 AU, a +29% overshoot — the single outlier that started the prediction off at the wrong distance.

2. Using Kepler's third law in solar units ($P=a^{3/2}$ yr), find the orbital periods of Uranus ($a=19.19$) and Neptune ($a=30.07$), then their synodic period $S$ from $\tfrac1S=\tfrac1{P_U}-\tfrac1{P_N}$.

Show solution
  1. Periods. $P_U=19.19^{1.5}\approx84.1$ yr, $P_N=30.07^{1.5}\approx164.9$ yr.
  2. Rates. $\tfrac1{84.1}-\tfrac1{164.9}=0.01189-0.00606=0.00583$ per yr.
  3. Invert. $S=1/0.00583\approx 171$ yr.
Answer: $S\approx171$ yr — so only one conjunction (~1822) falls in the 1740–1860 window, which is why the O−C curve shows a single rise-and-reverse.

3. A predicted orbit has $a_\text{pred}=37$ AU and $e_\text{pred}=0.18$. What is its perihelion distance, and why does that explain how a 37-AU prediction pointed at a 30-AU planet?

Show solution
  1. Perihelion formula. $r_\text{peri}=a(1-e)=37\times(1-0.18)=37\times0.82$.
  2. Evaluate. $r_\text{peri}=30.3$ AU.
Answer: 30.3 AU — essentially Neptune's real 30.07 AU. With perihelion aimed at the 1846 direction, the predicted planet sat at nearly the real distance and the real longitude, so the sightline matched.
