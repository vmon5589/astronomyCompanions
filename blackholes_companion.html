1814 – 1925 · What are stars made of?

Reading a star's temperature in its colour

Fraunhofer · Kirchhoff & Bunsen · Planck & Wien · Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin

What was known

In 1814 Fraunhofer saw dark lines crossing the Sun's spectrum; by 1859 Kirchhoff & Bunsen showed each line is a fingerprint of a chemical element. And a glowing body's colour betrays its temperature: Planck's 1900 law and Wien's law say a hotter object peaks at a bluer wavelength.

The idea

If the continuous glow gives the temperature and the dark lines give the elements, a stellar spectrum should reveal both what a star is made of and how hot it is — without ever leaving Earth.

The test

In her 1925 Harvard thesis, Cecilia Payne applied Saha's ionisation equation to thousands of spectra. The strength of a line depends on temperature, not just abundance — so she had to disentangle the two.

What she found

Stars are overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium — a million times more hydrogen than the Earth-like mix everyone expected. Pressured by Henry Norris Russell, she hedged and called it "almost certainly spurious." She was right; he later came round. It is now called the single most brilliant PhD thesis in astronomy.

Practice problems

1. A hot B-type star has a surface temperature of $T=10{,}000$ K. Using Wien's law $\lambda_{\text{peak}}=\dfrac{2.898\times10^{-3}}{T}$ m, at what wavelength does it shine brightest, and in which band is that?

Show solution
  1. Apply Wien's law. $\lambda_{\text{peak}}=\dfrac{2.898\times10^{-3}}{10{,}000}=2.898\times10^{-7}$ m.
  2. Convert to nanometres. $2.898\times10^{-7}\text{ m}=290$ nm.
Answer: $\lambda_{\text{peak}}\approx290$ nm — in the ultraviolet, just shortward of visible, which is why hot stars look blue-white to the eye.

2. A blackbody's energy output per unit area follows Stefan–Boltzmann, flux $\propto T^4$. A blue O-star surface sits at $30{,}000$ K, the Sun's at $5778$ K. How many times more power does each square metre of the O-star radiate?

Show solution
  1. Take the temperature ratio. $\dfrac{T_O}{T_\odot}=\dfrac{30{,}000}{5778}=5.19$.
  2. Raise to the fourth power. $\left(5.19\right)^4=727$.
Answer: about $730\times$ — each patch of the O-star's surface blazes hundreds of times brighter, the steep $T^4$ scaling behind the brilliance of massive stars.

3. A star's spectrum peaks at $\lambda_{\text{peak}}=290$ nm. Use Wien's law backwards to find its surface temperature, then name the spectral class (O>30000, B>10000, A>7500 K).

Show solution
  1. Rearrange Wien's law for $T$. $T=\dfrac{2.898\times10^{-3}}{\lambda_{\text{peak}}}$.
  2. Substitute (in metres). $T=\dfrac{2.898\times10^{-3}}{290\times10^{-9}}=9990$ K.
Answer: $T\approx9990$ K, a class-B star — reading temperature straight off the colour of light is exactly the trick Payne's spectra relied on.
Light beyond the rainbow

The whole electromagnetic spectrum

Herschel · Maxwell · and every telescope since

What was known

Visible light is a sliver. In 1800 William Herschel found "invisible" heat — infrared — beyond the red, and within a century Maxwell showed light is an electromagnetic wave. Radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray and gamma are all the same thing at different wavelengths.

Why it matters here

Black holes are invisible, but the gas around them screams across the spectrum. We image Sgr A* in radio, watch accretion disks glow in X-rays, and catch infant stars in infrared. Each band is a different window.

The relationships

Shorter wavelength means higher frequency and more energetic photons: $c=\lambda f$ and $E=hf=hc/\lambda$. A gamma-ray photon carries a trillion times the energy of a radio photon.

The catch

Earth's atmosphere blocks most of it — only visible light and some radio reach the ground. To see the X-ray and gamma-ray universe, we must go to space.

Practice problems

1. An accretion disk glows in X-rays at wavelength $\lambda=1.0$ nm. Using $f=c/\lambda$ and $E=hf$, find the photon's frequency and its energy in keV ($h=6.626\times10^{-34}$ J·s, $1$ eV $=1.602\times10^{-19}$ J).

Show solution
  1. Frequency. $f=\dfrac{c}{\lambda}=\dfrac{2.998\times10^8}{1.0\times10^{-9}}=3.0\times10^{17}$ Hz.
  2. Energy in joules. $E=hf=(6.626\times10^{-34})(3.0\times10^{17})=1.99\times10^{-16}$ J.
  3. Convert to eV. $E=\dfrac{1.99\times10^{-16}}{1.602\times10^{-19}}=1240$ eV $=1.24$ keV.
Answer: $f\approx3.0\times10^{17}$ Hz, $E\approx1.24$ keV — an X-ray photon carrying thousands of times the energy of visible light, the glow of million-degree gas.

2. A gamma-ray photon from a black-hole jet carries $E=1.0$ MeV. Using $\lambda=hc/E$, find its wavelength in picometres.

Show solution
  1. Energy in joules. $E=1.0\times10^{6}\times1.602\times10^{-19}=1.602\times10^{-13}$ J.
  2. Apply $\lambda=hc/E$. $\lambda=\dfrac{(6.626\times10^{-34})(2.998\times10^8)}{1.602\times10^{-13}}=1.24\times10^{-12}$ m.
Answer: $\lambda\approx1.24$ pm — smaller than an atomic nucleus, the vanishingly short wavelength of the most violent light in the Universe.

3. Radio astronomers map hydrogen at the "21-cm line" ($\lambda=0.21$ m). Using $f=c/\lambda$, what frequency is that, and does such radio light reach the ground?

Show solution
  1. Apply the wave relation. $f=\dfrac{c}{\lambda}=\dfrac{2.998\times10^8}{0.21}$.
  2. Evaluate. $f=1.43\times10^{9}$ Hz $=1.43$ GHz.
Answer: $f\approx1.43$ GHz — radio, which passes freely through the atmosphere, so ground-based dishes (and the EHT) can observe it.
1859 · The barcode of an atom

Emission & absorption lines — starlight's barcode

Gustav Kirchhoff & Robert Bunsen

What was known

Heat a pure element and it glows at sharp, fixed wavelengths — bright emission lines, unique to each element. Kirchhoff and Bunsen turned this into chemistry-at-a-distance in 1859.

Kirchhoff's three laws

A hot dense object gives a smooth rainbow (continuum). A hot thin gas adds bright emission lines. And a cool gas in front of a continuum carves dark absorption lines — exactly the Fraunhofer lines in the Sun, where cooler outer gas absorbs specific colours.

The test

Each element absorbs and emits the same set of wavelengths. Hydrogen's Balmer series (Hα red, Hβ blue-green, …) and calcium's H & K lines became a barcode you could match against any star.

What it revealed

The pattern and strength of lines give a star's composition, temperature and motion. The same trick uncovers gas swirling around black holes — and its redshift tells us how fast it moves.

Build a stellar spectrum

Pick a spectral type and a viewing geometry. Absorption = cool gas in front of the star (dark lines on a rainbow). Emission = glowing thin gas (bright lines on black) — Kirchhoff's laws in action. Hot blue stars show weak lines; cool stars show a forest of them.

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda}=R_H\left(\frac{1}{2^2}-\frac{1}{n^2}\right)\ \text{(hydrogen Balmer series, } n=3,4,5\ldots) $$

Practice problems

1. The hydrogen Balmer series follows $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{2^2}-\dfrac{1}{n^2}\right)$ with $R_H=1.097\times10^{7}$ m⁻¹. Find the wavelength of the H$\alpha$ line ($n=3$).

Show solution
  1. Insert $n=3$. $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=1.097\times10^{7}\left(\dfrac{1}{4}-\dfrac{1}{9}\right)=1.097\times10^{7}(0.1389)$.
  2. Evaluate. $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=1.524\times10^{6}$ m⁻¹, so $\lambda=6.56\times10^{-7}$ m.
Answer: $\lambda\approx656$ nm — the deep-red H$\alpha$ line, the brightest fingerprint of hydrogen and the glow of countless nebulae.

2. Using the same Balmer formula, find the H$\beta$ line ($n=4$) and confirm it lands in the blue-green.

Show solution
  1. Insert $n=4$. $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=1.097\times10^{7}\left(\dfrac{1}{4}-\dfrac{1}{16}\right)=1.097\times10^{7}(0.1875)$.
  2. Evaluate. $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=2.057\times10^{6}$ m⁻¹, so $\lambda=4.86\times10^{-7}$ m.
Answer: $\lambda\approx486$ nm — blue-green, exactly where H$\beta$ appears in stellar spectra; the same barcode lets us identify gas around black holes.

3. Where does the Balmer series end? Take $n\to\infty$ in $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{4}-\dfrac{1}{n^2}\right)$ to find the series limit wavelength.

Show solution
  1. Let $n\to\infty$. The $1/n^2$ term vanishes: $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=R_H\cdot\dfrac{1}{4}=\dfrac{1.097\times10^{7}}{4}$.
  2. Evaluate. $\dfrac{1}{\lambda}=2.743\times10^{6}$ m⁻¹, so $\lambda=3.65\times10^{-7}$ m.
Answer: $\lambda\approx365$ nm — the Balmer limit in the near-ultraviolet, where the discrete lines crowd together and merge into a continuum.
The life of a star

From gas cloud to grave — the H–R diagram

Ejnar Hertzsprung & Henry Norris Russell

What was known

By 1910 Hertzsprung and Russell plotted stars' luminosity against their temperature. Stars didn't scatter randomly — most fell on a diagonal stripe, the main sequence, with giants and dwarfs off to the sides.

The idea

A star's whole life is a journey across this diagram. On the main sequence it fuses hydrogen to helium; its mass sets where it sits, how brightly it burns, and how long it lives.

The test

Mass is destiny. A heavy star is hot, blazing and short-lived; a red dwarf sips its fuel for trillions of years. When the core's hydrogen runs out, the star swells into a red giant or supergiant.

The conclusion

The ending depends on mass: a Sun-like star puffs off a planetary nebula and leaves a white dwarf; a massive star explodes as a supernova, leaving a neutron star or black hole.

1. A main-sequence star has $M=2\,M_\odot$. Using $L\approx M^{3.5}L_\odot$, how much brighter is it than the Sun?

Show solution
  1. Apply the mass–luminosity law. $L=M^{3.5}=2^{3.5}$ (in solar units).
  2. Evaluate. $2^{3.5}=11.3$.

2. The same $2\,M_\odot$ star: using $t_{\text{MS}}\approx10\text{ Gyr}\times M^{-2.5}$, how long does it live on the main sequence?

Show solution
  1. Apply the lifetime law. $t_{\text{MS}}=10\text{ Gyr}\times2^{-2.5}$.
  2. Evaluate. $2^{-2.5}=0.177$, so $t_{\text{MS}}=1.77$ Gyr.
Answer: about $1.8$ billion years — far shorter than the Sun's 10 Gyr; heavy stars burn bright and die young.

3. A $10\,M_\odot$ star is destined to leave a neutron star or black hole. How long does it spend on the main sequence ($t_{\text{MS}}\approx10\text{ Gyr}\times M^{-2.5}$)?

Show solution
  1. Apply the lifetime law. $t_{\text{MS}}=10\text{ Gyr}\times10^{-2.5}$.
  2. Evaluate. $10^{-2.5}=3.16\times10^{-3}$, so $t_{\text{MS}}=0.0316$ Gyr $=31.6$ Myr.
Answer: only about $32$ million years — a black-hole progenitor lives a cosmic blink before exploding.
1930 · How stars die

The mass limit that dooms a star to collapse

Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar · vs. Sir Arthur Eddington

What was known

A dead star with no fuel left should be held up by electron degeneracy pressure — a quantum refusal of electrons to share a state. Everyone assumed this could support a stellar corpse of any mass forever.

The idea

On the boat to England in 1930, 19-year-old Chandrasekhar combined degeneracy pressure with special relativity. Relativity caps how fast electrons can push back — so the support is not unlimited.

The test

He computed the maximum mass a white dwarf can carry. Above it, gravity wins. Eddington publicly ridiculed the result — "there should be a law of Nature to prevent a star from behaving in this absurd way."

What he found

The Chandrasekhar limit ≈ 1.4 M☉. A heavier core cannot stop collapsing — it becomes a neutron star, and if heavier still, a black hole. He was vindicated and shared the 1983 Nobel Prize.

1. A dying star leaves a degenerate core of $1.2\,M_\odot$. With the Chandrasekhar limit at $\approx1.4\,M_\odot$, does electron degeneracy pressure hold it up, and what kind of remnant results?

Show solution
  1. Compare to the limit. $1.2\,M_\odot < 1.4\,M_\odot$, so the core is below the Chandrasekhar mass.
  2. Interpret. Electron degeneracy pressure can still balance gravity — no collapse.
Answer: the core survives as a white dwarf; only cores above $1.4\,M_\odot$ are doomed to collapse into a neutron star or black hole.

2. A core of $3.0\,M_\odot$ exceeds even the neutron-star limit and becomes a black hole. Using $R_s=\dfrac{2GM}{c^2}$, what is its event-horizon radius?

Show solution
  1. Mass in kg. $M=3.0\times1.989\times10^{30}=5.97\times10^{30}$ kg.
  2. Apply the formula. $R_s=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(5.97\times10^{30})}{(2.998\times10^8)^2}$.
  3. Evaluate. $R_s=8.86\times10^{3}$ m $=8.86$ km.
Answer: $R_s\approx8.9$ km — the whole horizon is the size of a small city, the hallmark of a stellar-mass black hole.

3. A white dwarf packs $1.0\,M_\odot$ into roughly an Earth-sized sphere ($R=6.371\times10^6$ m). Estimate its mean density ($\rho=M/\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3$).

Show solution
  1. Volume. $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi(6.371\times10^6)^3=1.08\times10^{21}$ m³.
  2. Density. $\rho=\dfrac{1.989\times10^{30}}{1.08\times10^{21}}=1.84\times10^{9}$ kg/m³.
Answer: $\rho\approx1.8\times10^{9}$ kg/m³ — over a tonne per cubic centimetre, the crush that degeneracy pressure barely holds back.
A field guide

The stellar zoo — stars and their corpses

Every type of star and remnant, to scale in spirit

The family

Stars come in a sweep of masses, from feeble red dwarfs to monstrous blue supergiants — sorted by the spectral sequence O B A F G K M, hottest to coolest. How they die, and what they leave behind, is set almost entirely by mass.

The corpses

The leftovers are the strangest objects in the sky: an Earth-sized white dwarf, a city-sized neutron star, a beaming pulsar, an ultra-magnetic magnetar, and — at the end of the heaviest stars — a black hole.

1967 · Little green men?

Neutron stars, pulsars & magnetars

Jocelyn Bell Burnell · Antony Hewish

What was known

Theory said a collapsing core too heavy for a white dwarf would crush its protons and electrons into neutrons — a neutron star: about 1.4 Suns squeezed into a 24 km ball, so dense a teaspoon weighs a billion tonnes.

The discovery

In 1967 Jocelyn Bell Burnell found a radio signal pulsing every 1.337 seconds — so regular it was half-jokingly tagged "LGM-1" for little green men. It was a spinning neutron star sweeping a beam past Earth: a pulsar, a cosmic lighthouse.

The physics

Collapse conserves angular momentum, so the tiny star spins up to hundreds of times a second, its surface racing at a sizeable fraction of light-speed. Its magnetic field, compressed, becomes colossal — and in a magnetar, the strongest in the Universe.

Why they matter

Pulsars are nature's most precise clocks, and the heaviest of them sit right at the doorway to becoming black holes. Merging neutron stars even forge gold — and ring spacetime with gravitational waves.

Practice problems

1. Jocelyn Bell's first pulsar spins once every $P=1.337$ s. For a $R=12$ km neutron star, find its equatorial speed $v_{\text{eq}}=\dfrac{2\pi R}{P}$.

Show solution
  1. Circumference. $2\pi R=2\pi(1.2\times10^4)=7.54\times10^{4}$ m.
  2. Divide by the period. $v_{\text{eq}}=\dfrac{7.54\times10^{4}}{1.337}=5.64\times10^{4}$ m/s.
Answer: $v_{\text{eq}}\approx5.6\times10^{4}$ m/s $\approx0.02\%$ of light-speed — even this "slow" pulsar's surface races at tens of km per second.

2. A millisecond pulsar spins every $P=1.4$ ms. With $R=12$ km, what fraction of the speed of light does its equator reach ($v_{\text{eq}}=2\pi R/P$)?

Show solution
  1. Equatorial speed. $v_{\text{eq}}=\dfrac{2\pi(1.2\times10^4)}{1.4\times10^{-3}}=5.39\times10^{7}$ m/s.
  2. As a fraction of $c$. $\dfrac{5.39\times10^{7}}{2.998\times10^8}=0.18$.
Answer: about $0.18\,c$ — the surface whips around at nearly a fifth of light-speed, the limit at which a neutron star would fly apart.

3. A neutron star holds $1.4\,M_\odot$ in a $R=12$ km sphere. Compute its mean density $\rho=M/\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3$ and compare to water ($10^3$ kg/m³).

Show solution
  1. Mass and volume. $M=1.4\times1.989\times10^{30}=2.78\times10^{30}$ kg; $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi(1.2\times10^4)^3=7.24\times10^{12}$ m³.
  2. Density. $\rho=\dfrac{2.78\times10^{30}}{7.24\times10^{12}}=3.85\times10^{17}$ kg/m³.
Answer: $\rho\approx4\times10^{17}$ kg/m³ — about nuclear density, so a sugar-cube's worth would weigh roughly a billion tonnes.
1783 · The first black hole idea

A star so heavy that light cannot escape

Rev. John Michell · (and, independently, Pierre-Simon Laplace, 1796)

What was known

Newton's gravity gives every body an escape velocity: throw something faster than $v_{esc}=\sqrt{2GM/r}$ and it never falls back. Light, people then thought, was a stream of corpuscles — particles that should feel gravity too.

The idea

Michell asked: what if a star were so massive and compact that its escape velocity reached the speed of light? Then even light could not leave. He called them "dark stars."

The test

Set $v_{esc}=c$ and solve for the size. For the Sun's density, you would need a star several hundred times the Sun's diameter — a thought experiment with real numbers, presented to the Royal Society in 1783.

What he found

Such an object would be invisible, yet its gravity would still tug on companion stars — exactly how we hunt black holes today. The idea slept for over a century until relativity revived it.

The test: push the escape velocity up to light-speed

Pick an object's mass, then shrink its radius. As it gets denser the escape velocity climbs. When the bar reaches $c$, you've built a dark star — and the radius you crossed is the event horizon.

Practice problems

1. What is Earth's escape velocity? Use $v_{esc}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2GM}{r}}$ with $M=5.972\times10^{24}$ kg and $r=6.371\times10^{6}$ m.

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. $\dfrac{2GM}{r}=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(5.972\times10^{24})}{6.371\times10^{6}}=1.251\times10^{8}$ m²/s².
  2. Take the square root. $v_{esc}=\sqrt{1.251\times10^{8}}=1.12\times10^{4}$ m/s.
Answer: $v_{esc}\approx11.2$ km/s — the familiar launch speed to leave Earth; Michell asked what mass would push this all the way up to $c$.

2. Set $v_{esc}=c$ for a $1\,M_\odot$ dark star. Solving $\sqrt{2GM/r}=c$ gives $r=2GM/c^2$. How small must the Sun be crushed?

Show solution
  1. Rearrange. $r=\dfrac{2GM}{c^2}$.
  2. Substitute. $r=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(1.989\times10^{30})}{(2.998\times10^8)^2}$.
  3. Evaluate. $r=2.95\times10^{3}$ m $=2.95$ km.
Answer: just $2.95$ km — squeeze the Sun inside a 3 km ball and even light cannot escape; this radius is the Schwarzschild radius.

3. What is the escape velocity at the Sun's actual surface ($M=1.989\times10^{30}$ kg, $R_\odot=6.96\times10^{8}$ m)? Compare to $c$.

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. $\dfrac{2GM}{R_\odot}=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(1.989\times10^{30})}{6.96\times10^{8}}=3.81\times10^{11}$ m²/s².
  2. Square root. $v_{esc}=\sqrt{3.81\times10^{11}}=6.18\times10^{5}$ m/s.
Answer: $v_{esc}\approx618$ km/s — only $0.2\%$ of $c$, so the real Sun is nowhere near a dark star; you must shrink it 235,000-fold first.
1915 – 1916 · Geometry, not force

The exact size of a hole in spacetime

Karl Schwarzschild, solving Einstein's equations — from the WWI front line

What was known

Einstein's 1915 general relativity recast gravity as curved spacetime, but his field equations are fearsomely nonlinear. Einstein doubted anyone would find an exact solution soon.

The idea

Schwarzschild, serving in the German army, solved them for a single spherical mass within weeks — writing the maths in the trenches. His solution has a special radius where time and space swap roles.

The test

At $r_s=2GM/c^2$ the maths blows up. Was it a real edge or a mathematical artefact? It is real in the sense that matters: cross it and no signal can ever climb back out. It is the event horizon.

What he found

Every mass has one. Squeeze the Sun inside 3 km, or the Earth inside 9 mm, and it becomes a black hole. Schwarzschild died months later; the radius bears his name.

The data: how big is the horizon?

Practice problems

1. Sgr A* weighs $M=4.1\times10^{6}\,M_\odot$. Find its Schwarzschild radius $R_s=\dfrac{2GM}{c^2}$ and express it in AU ($1$ AU $=1.496\times10^{11}$ m).

Show solution
  1. Mass in kg. $M=4.1\times10^{6}\times1.989\times10^{30}=8.15\times10^{36}$ kg.
  2. Apply the formula. $R_s=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(8.15\times10^{36})}{(2.998\times10^8)^2}=1.21\times10^{10}$ m.
  3. Convert to AU. $\dfrac{1.21\times10^{10}}{1.496\times10^{11}}=0.081$ AU.
Answer: $R_s\approx1.2\times10^{7}$ km $\approx0.08$ AU — about a fifth of Mercury's orbit, the size of our galaxy's central horizon.

2. How small would Earth ($M=5.972\times10^{24}$ kg) have to be crushed to become a black hole? Find $R_s=2GM/c^2$ in millimetres.

Show solution
  1. Apply the formula. $R_s=\dfrac{2(6.674\times10^{-11})(5.972\times10^{24})}{(2.998\times10^8)^2}$.
  2. Evaluate. $R_s=8.87\times10^{-3}$ m $=8.87$ mm.
Answer: $R_s\approx8.9$ mm — squeeze the entire Earth to the size of a marble and it becomes a black hole.

3. M87* holds $M=6.5\times10^{9}\,M_\odot$. Find its Schwarzschild radius in AU. (Since $R_s\propto M$, you can also scale from the $2.95$ km per $M_\odot$ rule.)

Show solution
  1. Use the linear scaling. $R_s=2.95\text{ km}\times6.5\times10^{9}=1.92\times10^{10}$ km $=1.92\times10^{13}$ m.
  2. Convert to AU. $\dfrac{1.92\times10^{13}}{1.496\times10^{11}}=128$ AU.
Answer: $R_s\approx128$ AU — larger than the entire orbit of Pluto, a single horizon swallowing our whole planetary system.
Falling in · Tidal forces

Why a small black hole is deadlier than a giant one

Newtonian tides · the counter-intuitive twist Becky loves

What was known

Gravity is stronger nearer the source, so your feet feel more pull than your head as you fall in. That difference — the tidal force — stretches you head-to-toe and squeezes you sideways: spaghettification.

The idea

Tidal stretching grows as $1/r^3$, far faster than gravity's own $1/r^2$. So the danger is set by how close the horizon sits — which depends on mass.

The test

Evaluate the stretch at the horizon. The algebra gives a surprise: tidal force at $r_s$ scales as $1/M^2$. Bigger black hole, gentler crossing.

The conclusion

At a stellar-mass black hole you are pulled apart long before reaching the horizon. At a supermassive one you could sail across the horizon barely noticing — the violence comes later.

Relativity · The ultimate time machine

Time runs slow near a black hole

Einstein · tested by Pound & Rebka · dramatised by Interstellar

What was known

General relativity says clocks tick slower the deeper they sit in a gravity well. It’s tiny on Earth — but real: GPS satellites must correct for it, and the 1959 Pound–Rebka experiment measured it in a lift shaft.

The idea

Near a black hole the effect runs wild. Hover just outside the event horizon and your clock crawls compared to a distant observer’s — approach the horizon and time almost stops, seen from afar.

The maths

The slow-down factor is $\sqrt{1-r_s/r}$. In Interstellar, an hour on Miller’s planet near Gargantua passed as seven years back home — a factor of ~61,000, only possible skimming a rapidly spinning black hole.

The conclusion

Time is not universal; gravity bends it. A black hole is a one-way time machine into the future — and the light that climbs out of the well is stretched and reddened on the way.

Practice problems

1. You hover at $r=2r_s$ above a black hole. Using $\dfrac{d\tau}{dt}=\sqrt{1-\dfrac{r_s}{r}}$, how fast does your clock run compared with a distant observer's?

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. $\dfrac{r_s}{r}=\dfrac{r_s}{2r_s}=0.5$.
  2. Apply the formula. $\dfrac{d\tau}{dt}=\sqrt{1-0.5}=\sqrt{0.5}=0.707$.
Answer: your clock ticks at $0.71\times$ the distant rate — already a 29% slowdown just two horizon-radii out.

2. You hover very close, at $r=1.01\,r_s$. For every $1$ hour you experience, how much time passes far away? (Distant time $=\dfrac{\text{your time}}{\sqrt{1-r_s/r}}$.)

Show solution
  1. Compute the factor. $\dfrac{r_s}{r}=\dfrac{1}{1.01}=0.9901$, so $1-0.9901=9.9\times10^{-3}$.
  2. Take the root and invert. $\sqrt{9.9\times10^{-3}}=0.0995$, so the dilation $=\dfrac{1}{0.0995}=10.0$.
  3. Scale the hour. Distant time $=10.0\times1$ h.
Answer: about $10$ hours pass outside for each hour you feel — skim still closer and the gap explodes, the Interstellar effect.

3. Light emitted by gas hovering at $r=4\,r_s$ climbs out to a distant observer. Find its gravitational redshift $1+z=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-r_s/r}}$.

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. $\dfrac{r_s}{r}=\dfrac{1}{4}=0.25$.
  2. Apply the formula. $1+z=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.25}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.75}}=\dfrac{1}{0.866}=1.15$.
Answer: $1+z\approx1.15$, a 15% redshift — light loses energy climbing out of the well, reddening as it escapes.
1992 – 2020 · The monster next door

Weighing the black hole at the Milky Way's heart

Andrea Ghez & Reinhard Genzel · Nobel Prize 2020 (with Roger Penrose)

What was known

Something invisible and compact lurked at the galactic centre, blazing faintly in radio as Sagittarius A*. But was it really a black hole — or just a dense star cluster?

The idea

Track individual stars whipping around the centre. Their orbits obey Kepler's third law, and the orbit fixes the mass inside — no light from the object required.

The test

For two decades, two teams imaged the star S2 with adaptive optics, watching it swing through a 16-year orbit and round its closest approach in 2018. Period and orbit size in, mass out.

What they found

≈ 4 million solar masses packed inside S2's tiny orbit — far too dense to be anything but a black hole. Nobel Prize, 2020.

1963 · The brightest things in the Universe

How a black hole shines — accretion & quasars

Maarten Schmidt · and the Eddington limit

What was known

A black hole emits nothing — yet in 1963 Maarten Schmidt found "quasars": starlike radio sources, billions of light-years away, outshining whole galaxies. What could be so small yet so luminous?

The idea

Gas spiralling toward a black hole can’t fall straight in; it forms an accretion disk. Friction heats it to millions of degrees, and it blazes in ultraviolet and X-rays — converting up to ~10% of the infalling mass to pure energy, far beating nuclear fusion.

The limit

There’s a ceiling. As the disk brightens, its own radiation pressure pushes outward; past the Eddington luminosity it blows the fuel away. That caps how fast a black hole can feed — and grow.

What it explained

Quasars are supermassive black holes feeding near their Eddington limit in the young Universe — devouring a few Suns a year. Accretion is both how black holes betray themselves and how they grew to millions of solar masses.

1974 · Black holes are not quite black

The glow that makes black holes evaporate

Stephen Hawking

What was known

Bekenstein argued a black hole must have entropy, proportional to its horizon area. But anything with entropy and a temperature should radiate — and a black hole was supposed to swallow everything.

The idea

Hawking applied quantum field theory near the horizon. The vacuum fizzes with particle pairs; near a horizon one can fall in while its partner escapes, carrying away energy.

The test

He derived the temperature of that leakage. It is inversely proportional to mass: small black holes are hot, giant ones are colder than deep space.

The conclusion

Black holes slowly evaporate. A stellar black hole would take $10^{67}$ years; but a mountain-mass primordial one would be detonating right about now. It tied gravity, quantum theory and thermodynamics in one knot we still can't fully untie.

14 September 2015 · Hearing two black holes collide

The chirp that proved black holes merge

LIGO · event GW150914 · Nobel Prize 2017

What was known

Einstein predicted that accelerating masses ripple spacetime, but the waves are absurdly faint — he doubted they'd ever be measured. Two orbiting black holes should spiral together, radiating these ripples.

The idea

As the pair spirals in, the orbit speeds up, so the wave's frequency and amplitude rise together into a "chirp" — a precise, predictable swoop ending in a merger.

The test

LIGO's two 4-km laser arms watch for a length change of $10^{-19}$ m — a thousandth the width of a proton. On its first run it caught a textbook chirp.

What they found

Two black holes of 36 and 29 M☉, 1.3 billion light-years away, merged into one of 62 — radiating 3 M☉ of pure energy as gravitational waves in a fifth of a second. Nobel Prize, 2017.

10 April 2019 · Seeing the unseeable

The first photograph of a shadow

The Event Horizon Telescope · M87*, and in 2022, our own Sgr A*

What was known

General relativity predicted a black hole would cast a dark "shadow" about 2.6 times the horizon size, ringed by light bent around it. No telescope on Earth was sharp enough to see it.

The idea

Turn the whole planet into one telescope. Link radio dishes from Hawaii to the South Pole so they act as a single Earth-sized lens — enough resolution to read a newspaper in New York from a café in Paris.

The test

In April 2017 the array stared at M87*, a 6.5-billion-solar-mass giant 55 million light-years away. Petabytes of data flew home on hard drives; two years of computation assembled the image.

What they found

A glowing ring around a dark heart — exactly the size and shape Einstein's maths demanded, a century on. The shadow is real.
