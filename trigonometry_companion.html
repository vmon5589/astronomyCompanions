Part I — Trigonometry: angles into distances

Five ideas — right-triangle ratios, the unit circle, the radian, the small-angle rule, and the ellipse — carry you from a schoolroom triangle to the orbit of a star skimming the Milky Way's central black hole.

Trigonometry · 1

Three ratios hiding in a right triangle

SOH-CAH-TOA — and how the same ratios fix the bending of a light ray and the size of a black hole's shadow on the sky

We keep one rhythm in every chapter: what it is, how it works, and how it's applied — the math first, the astrophysics as the payoff at the end.

1The definitions

What it is

Take a right triangle — one corner is a square 90°. Fix attention on one of the other angles, $\theta$. The three trigonometry functions are just ratios of its sides: the sine is opposite over hypotenuse, the cosine is adjacent over hypotenuse, and the tangent is opposite over adjacent. The schoolroom tag is SOH-CAH-TOA. Each merely asks "which two sides am I comparing?"

How it works

The quiet miracle is that these ratios depend only on the angle, never on the triangle's size. Scale the triangle up and every side grows in lockstep, so each ratio is frozen. One angle maps to one fixed value of $\sin$, $\cos$ and $\tan$ — a lookup table from an angle to a ratio and, read backwards through the inverse functions $\arcsin,\arccos,\arctan$, from a ratio to an angle. And $\tan\theta=\sin\theta/\cos\theta$ blows up toward 90°, where the adjacent side shrinks to nothing.

How it's applied

Whenever a ray of light changes direction, a sine keeps the books. Snell's law of refraction, $\sin\theta_1=n\,\sin\theta_2$, bends starlight entering a lens; the same ratios, pushed to the extreme by gravity, set the angle a light ray is deflected as it grazes a black hole — the geometry behind the photon sphere and the bright ring around the shadow.

2Where the three ratios come from

∴ Step by step: why one angle fixes all three ratios

Build it once from similar triangles and sine, cosine and tangent — plus the identity linking them — all follow.

  1. Two right triangles with the same acute angle are similar. Each already has a 90° corner; give them a second equal angle θ and the third must match too (angles sum to 180°). By the AA test they are similar — the same shape at different scales.
  2. Similar triangles have proportional sides. If the big triangle is $k$ times the small one, every side is $k$ times longer, so any ratio of two sides is the same in both — the $k$ cancels.
  3. Name the three ratios. With θ fixed, define $\sin\theta=\dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}$, $\cos\theta=\dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}$, $\tan\theta=\dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$. Step 2 guarantees each depends on θ alone, not on the triangle's size.
  4. They interlock. Dividing, $\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}=\dfrac{\text{opp}/\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}/\text{hyp}}=\dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}=\tan\theta$.
  5. Pythagoras ties them together. Since $\text{opp}^2+\text{adj}^2=\text{hyp}^2$, dividing through by $\text{hyp}^2$ gives $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$.
Conclusion: one angle ⇒ one fixed value of each ratio. Rearranged — $\text{opp}=\text{adj}\cdot\tan\theta$ — that is exactly how a measured angle on the sky becomes a physical size.

The pattern beneath it · one force, two jobs: the block on a slope

Before the astrophysics payoff below, one pattern is worth building slowly, because you will meet it in every branch of physics — and the black-hole story underneath uses it move for move. A force almost never points along the direction you care about. Set a block on a hill tilted at angle $\theta$: gravity pulls it straight down with force $mg$, but the block can only slide along the slope. The four panels below rebuild the whole argument one move at a time — resolve the force into two perpendicular arrows, name the pieces by SOH-CAH-TOA, and watch everything except $mg\sin\theta$ cancel away.

$$ mg \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{mg\cos\theta}_{\text{presses in: cancelled}} \;+\; \underbrace{mg\sin\theta}_{\text{slides: the whole story}} $$

Once you see the move, it is everywhere. The two smaller panels below the sequence run it in different costumes: the magnetic force on a charge is all sine — always across the motion, pure steering — and a wrench turns a bolt only through the across-the-handle piece, $F\sin\theta$. The rule of thumb: across goes with sine, along goes with cosine (in vector language: cross products carry the sine, dot products the cosine).

From the slope to the black hole

The astrophysics payoff below repeats the slope argument beat for beat, with one substitution: the block becomes a photon flying past a black hole, and "along the slope" becomes "along the photon's flight". Gravity pulls the photon straight toward the mass — diagonally to its flight, in general. Resolve that pull into the same two pieces. The piece along the flight cancels out over the flyby, just as the cosine piece was cancelled by the hill: it hurries the photon on the way in and holds it back by the same amount on the way out. The piece across the flight — the pull times $\sin\varphi$, where $\varphi$ is the angle between the flight direction and the line to the mass — has nothing to cancel it. That lone surviving sine piece is what bends the ray.

One more character to meet before the payoff: as the photon flies, its distance to the mass $r$ and its angle $\varphi$ both change constantly, but the product $b=r\sin\varphi$ stays frozen at the value set when the ray was aimed — it is simply the miss distance (how close the straight-line aim would come to the mass), called the impact parameter. (If you have met conservation of angular momentum, this is it in disguise: $L=mvr\sin\varphi=mvb$, and with $m$ and $v$ fixed, constant $L$ means constant $b$. If you haven't, no matter — the payoff derives the constancy from a right triangle alone.) The name comes from Rutherford's alpha-particle scattering experiments; the same aim-distance geometry steers comet flybys and spacecraft gravity assists.

Astrophysics payoff · the bending of light past a black hole

One number sets up this whole story, so meet it first: the impact parameter $b$. Imagine gravity switched off. The light ray would fly dead straight, and $b$ is simply how close that straight line would pass to the centre of the mass — the miss distance at closest approach. Aim straight at the centre and $b=0$; aim well wide and $b$ is large. It is fixed the instant the ray is launched, and nothing that happens later changes it. That is all it is: a measure of the aim.

Now switch gravity back on. A ray aimed with impact parameter $b$ is deflected by an angle that Einstein's general relativity gives as $\alpha = \dfrac{4GM}{c^2 b}$ — the closer the aim, the harder the bend. The formula looks like pure gravity, but a sine is doing the bookkeeping at every step: the fixed aim distance $b$ can be read off the flying photon as a sine ($b=r\sin\varphi$, as the diagram shows), and gravity bends the ray only through the sine of the angle to the mass.

The diagram and derivation below unpack exactly where each $\sin$ enters. Push $b$ inward to $1.5$ Schwarzschild radii and the summed bending closes into a full loop: that critical circle is the photon sphere, and it is what paints the glowing ring in the Event Horizon Telescope image of M87*. Every photon in that ring is a right triangle resolved by gravity.

Ride along the ray — slide P and watch the two sines work

Slide the photon's position $P$ along its path and watch three things, one at a time.

The aim never moves. As $P$ flies, its distance to the mass $r$ and its angle to the mass $\varphi$ both change constantly — but in lock-step: $r$ grows by exactly the factor that $\sin\varphi$ shrinks. So their product $b=r\sin\varphi$ is pinned at one value for the whole flight. Nothing mysterious is being conserved; the right triangle built at each $P$ has a different hypotenuse $r$ and a different angle $\varphi$, but always the identical short leg $b$ — the miss distance you fixed when you aimed the ray.

Only part of the pull can steer. The amber arrow is gravity's full pull on the photon, pointing straight at the mass — diagonally to the flight, in general. Resolve it into two perpendicular pieces: the grey piece lies along the flight direction (it can speed the photon up or slow it, but never turn it), and the rose piece points across the flight — that sideways piece, equal to pull $\times\sin\varphi$, is the only thing that bends the ray.

The bend is an area. The strip below the diagram plots that rose sideways tug at every point of the path, and the shaded region is its running sum. By the time $P$ has flown past the mass, the area has grown into the full deflection $\alpha$.

∴ Step by step: where the sine hides in $\alpha = 4GM/c^2b$

Two different sines do the work — one fixes the aim, the other selects the bending; both are the plain opposite-over-hypotenuse ratio from the start of this chapter. The only physics input is the pull of a single body, $GM/r^2$. The five panels below run the whole derivation.

$$ b=r\sin\varphi,\qquad \text{sideways tug}=\frac{GM}{r^2}\sin\varphi,\qquad \int_0^{\pi}\sin\varphi\,d\varphi=2 \;\Rightarrow\; \alpha_{\text{Newton}}=\frac{2GM}{c^2b},\qquad \alpha_{\text{Einstein}}=\frac{4GM}{c^2b} $$
Conclusion: the deflection is nothing but a heap of sideways tugs, each one the pull times the sine of the angle to the mass. Pile them up to the limit and the heap becomes the bright ring the Event Horizon Telescope photographed around M87*.

3Practice problems

1. Starlight strikes the front of a telescope lens (refractive index $n=1.50$) at $40°$ from the normal. Snell's law of refraction is $\sin\theta_1=n\sin\theta_2$. Find the angle $\theta_2$ inside the glass.

Show solution
  1. Solve Snell's law for the inside angle. $\sin\theta_2=\dfrac{\sin\theta_1}{n}$.
  2. Substitute. $\sin\theta_2=\dfrac{\sin 40°}{1.50}=\dfrac{0.643}{1.50}=0.4285$.
  3. Invert the sine. $\theta_2=\arcsin(0.4285)=25.4°$.
Answer: $\theta_2\approx25.4°$ — the ray bends toward the normal on entering the denser glass, the same sine ratio that lets a refractor focus starlight.

2. A galaxy's radio jet is $3.0$ kpc long and lies $50$ Mpc away, seen side-on. Using the tangent (small-angle) relation $\theta\approx L/d$, what angle does it subtend on the sky, in arcseconds?

Show solution
  1. Put both lengths in the same unit. $L=3.0\text{ kpc}=3.0\times10^3$ pc; $d=50\text{ Mpc}=5.0\times10^7$ pc.
  2. Take the ratio (radians). $\theta=\dfrac{L}{d}=\dfrac{3.0\times10^3}{5.0\times10^7}=6.0\times10^{-5}$ rad.
  3. Convert to arcseconds. $\theta=6.0\times10^{-5}\times206{,}265=12.4''$.
Answer: about $12.4''$ — a tangent ratio of two wildly different lengths, the everyday way an on-sky angle becomes a true size.

3. Light just grazing the Sun's edge is deflected by $\alpha=\dfrac{4GM}{c^2 b}$. With $M=1.99\times10^{30}$ kg and impact parameter $b=R_\odot=6.96\times10^8$ m, compute $\alpha$ (Eddington's 1919 eclipse test).

Show solution
  1. Numerator. $4GM=4(6.674\times10^{-11})(1.99\times10^{30})=5.31\times10^{20}$.
  2. Denominator. $c^2 b=(2.998\times10^8)^2(6.96\times10^8)=6.26\times10^{25}$.
  3. Divide, then convert to arcseconds. $\alpha=\dfrac{5.31\times10^{20}}{6.26\times10^{25}}=8.49\times10^{-6}$ rad $\times206{,}265=1.75''$.
Answer: $\alpha\approx1.75''$ — the bending Eddington measured to confirm general relativity. The glowing photon ring around a black hole is this same deflection pushed to its limit.

The unit circle: where sine becomes a wave

Spin a radius and its shadow is a sine wave — the shape of light, of a pulsar's beam, and of a gravitational wave

1From triangle to circle to wave

What it is

Make a right triangle inside a circle of radius 1: the tilted radius is the hypotenuse (length 1, one end pinned at the centre), the flat run along the x-axis is one leg ($\cos\theta$), and the vertical rise up to the circle is the other leg ($\sin\theta$). So the corner on the circle sits at coordinates $(\cos\theta,\ \sin\theta)$: cosine is how far across, sine is how far up. This unit circle is the honest definition of the functions — it extends $\sin$ and $\cos$ to any angle, well past the 0–90° a triangle can hold.

How it works

Let the radius spin at a steady rate and its height traces a smooth up-and-down curve: that curve is the sine wave, and cosine is the same wave a quarter-turn ahead. A wave is written $y=A\cos(2\pi f t+\phi)$, where $A$ is the amplitude, $f$ the frequency (turns per second), and $\phi$ the phase. A sine wave is, quite literally, the shadow of circular motion.

How it's applied

Light itself is this curve: the electromagnetic wave is a sine in space and time, its wavelength one full turn of the circle. A spinning neutron star sweeps its beam past us once per rotation, giving the metronomic sine-like pulse of a pulsar. And a passing gravitational wave stretches space as a cosine — the raw signal LIGO listens for.

2From a spinning radius to A·cos(2πft)

∴ Step by step: building the wave equation

A wave is not a new object — it is the vertical shadow of a point circling at a steady rate. Each symbol in $y=A\cos(2\pi f t+\phi)$ falls out of that picture.

  1. Put the point on the unit circle. At angle θ its coordinates are $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$, and Pythagoras on the radius gives $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ for every θ.
  2. Spin at a steady rate. Let the angle grow with time $t$, as $\theta=\omega t$, where ω is the angular speed in radians per second and $t$ is the elapsed time in seconds. The height is then $\sin(\omega t)$.
  3. Count turns — introduce frequency. One full turn is $2\pi$ radians, so $f$ turns per second means $\omega=2\pi f$. Substitute that into $\sin(\omega t)$ and the height becomes $\sin(2\pi f t)$.
  4. Scale and shift. A real oscillation has an amplitude $A$ and may start at any point in its cycle — the phase φ: $y=A\sin(2\pi f t+\phi)$. Physics conventionally starts the clock at the highest point. But with $\sin$, at the start ($t=0$, $\phi=0$) the argument is $0$, and $\sin(0) = 0$ — zero, not the peak; to begin at the peak you need a quarter-turn head-start, $\sin(2\pi f t+90°)$. And $\sin(\theta+90°)=\cos\theta$, so that quarter-turn is exactly what turns $\sin$ into $\cos$. Folding it in, physics writes $y=A\cos(2\pi f t+\phi)$, where $\phi=0$ now starts at the peak, since $\cos(0) = 1$.
Conclusion: every periodic signal here — a light wave, a pulsar's pulse, a gravitational wave's strain — is this one construction, differing only in $A$, $f$ and φ.

Astrophysics payoff · the metronome and the strain

The Pythagorean identity $\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1$ is just the unit circle written algebraically, and it is the reason a steadily spinning source produces a perfectly periodic signal. A pulsar can keep time to better than a microsecond a year because its beam is the shadow of uniform rotation. A gravitational wave arrives as a strain $h(t)=A\cos(2\pi f t)$ — a literal sine in the fabric of space — and the LIGO chapter's "chirp" is simply this wave with $A$ and $f$ slowly growing, which the chain-rule chapter will make precise.

Radians and the angular size of a shadow

Why astronomers measure angles as arc length — and how that turns a 40-microarcsecond smudge into the photo of a black hole

1The natural unit of angle

What it is

A radian is the angle that cuts an arc equal to the radius: $\theta = s/r$, with $s$ the arc length. A full turn wraps the whole circumference $2\pi r$, so it is $2\pi$ radians = 360°, and $1$ rad $\approx 57.3°$. Astronomers then chop the degree into arcminutes (1/60) and arcseconds (1/3600), and for the very smallest things, microarcseconds.

How it works

Because the radian is defined by arc length, the angular size of an object is just its true size divided by its distance, $\theta \approx D/d$, with no clumsy conversion factor — provided $\theta$ is in radians and small (the next chapter proves why). Double the distance and the angular size halves; that simple inverse law is the whole of resolving power.

How it's applied

The event horizon of a black hole has a real diameter, and its distance sets how big it looks. The supermassive hole in M87 is enormous but 55 million light-years away; Sgr A* at the Milky Way's centre is smaller but far closer. Both subtend only tens of microarcseconds — which is exactly why imaging them needed a telescope the size of the Earth.

2Why the radian makes angular size trivial

∴ Step by step: from arc length to D ⁄ d

Choosing the right unit for an angle turns an awkward triangle into a one-line division.

  1. Define the angle as arc length. Sweep an angle at the centre of a circle of radius $r$; it cuts an arc $s$. The radian is $\theta=s/r$, so $s=r\theta$.
  2. Calibrate against a full turn. A whole circle has arc $2\pi r$, so one turn is $2\pi r/r=2\pi$ rad $=360°$. Hence $1$ rad $=180/\pi=57.3°$, and splitting into seconds, $1$ rad $=206{,}265''$.
  3. A far object spans a tiny arc. An object of true size $D$ at distance $d$ subtends arc $D$ on a circle of radius $d$, so its angular size is simply $\theta=D/d$ in radians — no trig function needed.
  4. The shadow's size. A black hole's shadow has diameter $\approx5.2\,R_s$ (a relativistic factor times the Schwarzschild radius); dividing by distance gives the angle a telescope must resolve.
Conclusion: because the radian is arc length, "how big does it look?" is just size ÷ distance — the equation the lab below evaluates.

Small angles, and how parallax measures a distance you can't travel

From blinking at your thumb to the distance of a star — building the parallax equation one step at a time, and seeing in plain numbers why the astronomer's shortcut is allowed

The problem. A star is unreachable — you can't lay a ruler to it. But you can measure an angle, and you already know the width of Earth's orbit. This section turns that one angle into a distance, derives the equation step by step, and shows in plain numbers why the shortcut every astronomer uses barely costs anything.

1The idea: nearby things shift more

What it is

Hold a thumb at arm's length and blink between your left and right eye: the thumb jumps against the far wall. That jump is parallax. Pull the thumb closer and it jumps further; push it away and the jump shrinks. The size of the jump is a coded message about distance.

How it works

Your two eyes are a fixed baseline; the thumb and your two lines of sight make a long thin triangle. The closer the thumb, the wider the angle at its tip. Astronomers trade eyes for Earth's place in January versus July (a baseline across the orbit) and trade the thumb for a star.

How it's applied

Measure the star's shift, halve it to get the tip angle $p$, and the triangle hands back the distance. It is rung zero of the cosmic distance ladder — and the way the distance to the Galactic-Centre black hole is ultimately calibrated.

The pattern beneath it · tangent is the surveyor's tool

Before deriving the parallax equation, name the general trick it belongs to. Tangent has exactly one job: it trades directly between the two perpendicular legs of a right triangle, and the hypotenuse never enters. From $\tan\theta=\text{opp}/\text{adj}$ come two readings of the same fact: know the near leg and the angle, and $\text{opp}=\text{adj}\times\tan\theta$ hands you the far leg; know the far leg and the angle, and $\text{adj}=\text{opp}/\tan\theta$ hands you the near one. Every triangulation ever performed is one of these two lines.

The trick is centuries older than astrophysics — it is how surveyors measured mountain heights and river widths they could not cross, and astronomy simply inherited it. Run forward (known baseline + measured angle $\to$ unknown distance) it is stellar parallax, $d=B/\tan p$, with Earth's orbit as the baseline. Run backward (known distance + measured angle $\to$ unknown size) it is angular size, the physical length of a radio jet, the true diameter of an expanding nova shell, and the tiny orbit a star is dragged around by an unseen planet. Even friction makes a cameo: a block starts to slide when the slope steepens past $\tan\theta=\mu$.

$$ \tan\theta=\frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{d=\frac{B}{\tan p}}_{\text{angle}\ \to\ \text{distance (parallax)}} \qquad \underbrace{L = d\,\tan\theta}_{\text{distance}\ \to\ \text{size (angular size)}} $$

And because astronomical angles are almost always minuscule, $\tan\theta$, $\sin\theta$, and $\theta$ itself become interchangeable — the small-angle shortcut this chapter derives — so both readings collapse to the workhorse pair $d=B/p$ and $L=d\,\theta$.

2First in a field, where a tape measure can check it

Forget stars for a moment. You want the distance $d$ to a tree on the far bank of a river you can't cross. You have only a tape measure and a way to read angles. Here is the entire method — and where the equation comes from.

∴ Step by step: turning two sightings into a distance

The goal is "find $d$." The tools are a baseline $B$ and one angle. Watch the equation assemble itself.

  1. Build a right triangle. Stand at A directly facing the tree, so the line A→tree is square (90°) to the bank. The distance you want, $d$, is that square line.
  2. Walk a known baseline. Pace a measured $B=30$ m along the bank to point B and sight the tree again — it is no longer straight ahead but off to one side.
  3. Name the angle at the tree. The two sightlines (from A and from B) meet at the tree, making a small angle $p$ there. In this right triangle the baseline $B$ is the side opposite $p$, and the distance $d$ is the side adjacent. By the definition of tangent, $\tan p=\dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}=\dfrac{B}{d}$.
  4. Solve for the distance. Rearranging, $d=\dfrac{B}{\tan p}$. That is already a complete, exact answer.
  5. Put numbers in. Measure $p=11.3°$: then $\tan(11.3°)=0.200$, so $d=\dfrac{30}{0.200}=150$ m. A second, farther tree gives a smaller swing, say $p=5.71°$: $\tan(5.71°)=0.100$, so $d=\dfrac{30}{0.100}=300$ m. Half the angle, twice the distance — angle and distance are inversely linked.
Conclusion: $d=\dfrac{B}{\tan p}$. A known baseline and one measured angle pin a distance you never crossed — the exact trick we now aim at a star.

3For a star the angle is tiny — which lets us drop the "tan"

What it is

A star is so far away that its angle $p$ is a minuscule fraction of a degree. For an angle that small, $\tan p$ and $p$ itself (in radians) are the same number to more decimal places than any telescope can measure. So $d=B/\tan p$ simplifies to $d=B/p$. That swap is the small-angle approximation.

How it works

Why should a trig function equal a bare angle? In the wedge below, three different routes climb from the flat floor to the slanted roof: the vertical drop at the tip ($\sin p$, green), the curved rim ($p$ in radians, cyan), and the straight wall past the rim ($\tan p$, rose). They are always stacked in the order $\sin p < p < \tan p$. But squeeze the wedge shut and the roof presses toward the floor — there is simply no room left between the three routes, and they become one length.

How it's applied

This is the most-used shortcut in astronomy, because every angle on the sky is tiny. With the right units — baseline in AU, angle in arcseconds — the parsec is defined so cleanly that the whole calculation collapses to $d=1/p$.

Because $p$ is small, $p^3$ is fantastically smaller ($0.1^3=0.001$), so the correction barely registers. "The error is one part in ten thousand" simply means: for a $1°$ angle, that dropped $p^3$ piece is about $1/10{,}000$ of the answer.

With those units the conversion factors cancel and $d\,[\text{pc}] = 1 / p\,['']$.

Trigonometry · 5

Ellipses, conic sections, and Kepler's law

The shape of every orbit — and the scale that turns a star's path into the mass of the monster it circles

1The conic in polar form

What it is

An ellipse is one of the conic sections — the curves you get slicing a cone. In polar coordinates pinned at one focus it reads $r(\theta)=\dfrac{a(1-e^2)}{1+e\cos\theta}$, where $a$ is the semi-major axis (the orbit's overall size) and $e$ the eccentricity (how squashed: $e=0$ a circle, $e\to1$ a thin cigar). The cosine in the denominator is what makes the curve close.

How it works

Kepler found that a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times, and that the period $T$ and size $a$ obey Kepler's third law, $T^2\propto a^3$. With Newton's gravity the constant is filled in: $T^2 = \dfrac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G\,M}$. Measure the orbit's size and period and you have solved for the central mass $M$ — the body you are orbiting, even if it is invisible.

How it's applied

The star S2 loops the centre of the Galaxy on a 16-year ellipse, swinging to within 120 AU of an unseen point. Plug its $a$ and $T$ into Kepler's third law and the focus weighs about four million Suns packed inside that tiny perihelion — the cleanest proof that Sgr A* is a supermassive black hole.

2Deriving Kepler's third law

∴ Step by step: T² = 4π²a³ ⁄ GM from a force balance

For a circular orbit the law drops out of one equation — gravity supplying the centripetal pull — and carries over to ellipses with the semi-major axis $a$ in place of $r$.

  1. Balance gravity against the centripetal need. A body of mass $m$ on a circle of radius $r$ needs an inward force $mv^2/r$; gravity supplies $GMm/r^2$. Equate them: $\dfrac{GMm}{r^2}=\dfrac{mv^2}{r}$.
  2. Solve for the speed. The $m$ cancels and one $r$ divides out: $v^2=\dfrac{GM}{r}$.
  3. Write speed as distance over time. One lap is $2\pi r$ in period $T$, so $v=2\pi r/T$ and $v^2=4\pi^2 r^2/T^2$.
  4. Combine and tidy. Setting the two expressions for $v^2$ equal: $\dfrac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2}=\dfrac{GM}{r}\Rightarrow T^2=\dfrac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}$. For an ellipse the same holds with $r\to a$.
  5. Invert for the hidden mass. $M=\dfrac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G\,T^2}$ — measure the orbit's size and period, read off the central mass, invisible or not.
Conclusion: a star's orbit weighs whatever it circles. In solar units it shrinks to $M[M_\odot]=a^3[\text{AU}]/T^2[\text{yr}]$.

Calculus · 6

The derivative: slope, rate, and the top of a curve

A limit of slopes — and the trick that finds the colour a star glows brightest, and the speed nothing can exceed

The problem. A straight line's slope is easy — rise over run. But a curve's steepness changes at every point. How do you pin its slope at a single spot, and then use that to find the exact top of a hill? That is the derivative, and here is where it comes from — including the shortcut you'll actually compute with.

1Average slope, then the exact slope

What it is

Between two points on a curve you can always draw a straight line — a secant — whose slope is rise over run, $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$. That is the average steepness across the gap $h$.

How it works

Now slide the second point toward the first, shrinking $h$ toward nothing. The secant pivots until it just grazes the curve — the tangent — and its slope is the derivative $f'(x)$, the steepness at that one point. The value $h$ is approaching is a limit.

How it's applied

A derivative is the rate of change of anything: position over time is velocity, velocity over time is acceleration. And at the very top of a curve the tangent is flat, so $f'(x)=0$ — the equation that locates every peak, including a star's brightest colour.

2Deriving a slope from scratch

∴ Step by step: the slope of $f(x)=x^2$

No shortcut yet — just the definition, worked all the way through, so the shortcut afterward makes sense.

  1. Write the average slope. Between $x$ and $x+h$: $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}=\dfrac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}$.
  2. Expand the top. $(x+h)^2=x^2+2xh+h^2$, so the numerator becomes $x^2+2xh+h^2-x^2=2xh+h^2$.
  3. Divide by $h$. $\dfrac{2xh+h^2}{h}=2x+h$ (we can cancel $h$ because it isn't zero yet — just small).
  4. Take the limit. Let $h\to0$; the leftover $h$ vanishes, leaving $f'(x)=2x$. So at $x=1$ the slope is $2$, at $x=3$ it is $6$.
Conclusion: the slope of $x^2$ is $2x$ — derived purely from "rise over run, then shrink the run to zero." Do the same for $x^3$ and you get $3x^2$. A pattern is forming…

3The shortcut you'll actually use: the power rule

What it is

Those results — $x^2\to2x$, $x^3\to3x^2$ — are no accident. The power rule: to differentiate $x^n$, bring the exponent down in front and subtract one from it, giving $\dfrac{d}{dx}x^n=n\,x^{n-1}$. Once you trust it, no limits are needed.

How it works

Check it against what we just derived: $x^2\to2x^{1}=2x$ ✓, $x^3\to3x^{2}$ ✓, $x^1\to1x^{0}=1$ (a line has constant slope) ✓, a constant $x^0\to0$ (flat) ✓. Two more rules finish the kit: the slope of a sum is the sum of the slopes, and a constant multiplier rides along, so $\dfrac{d}{dx}(5x^4)=5\cdot4x^3=20x^3$.

How it's applied

With the power rule you can differentiate any polynomial in seconds — the working language of physics. (Its mirror image, used in the integration chapter, runs backwards: to integrate $x^n$ you add one to the exponent and divide by the new one.)

The power rule on three quick examples

① $\dfrac{d}{dx}x^5=5x^4$ — down comes the 5, the exponent drops to 4.   ② $\dfrac{d}{dx}(3x^2-7x+4)=6x-7$ — each term on its own; the constant 4 differentiates to 0.   ③ Free fall: position $s(t)=\tfrac12 g t^2$ gives velocity $s'(t)=g t$ and acceleration $s''(t)=g$ — one rule applied twice.

4Finding a peak: set the slope to zero

At the very top of a curve the tangent is momentarily flat, so the peak is wherever $f'(x)=0$. Quick example: a rectangle of fixed perimeter 20 has area $A(x)=x(10-x)=10x-x^2$; then $A'(x)=10-2x=0$ gives $x=5$ — a square, the largest area. Now the identical move, aimed at starlight.

Calculus · 7

The derivative of a field: tides and spaghettification

Gravity falls off as $1/r^2$; its change across your body falls off as $1/r^3$ — and that derivative is what stretches you into noodle soup

1From force to the gradient of force

What it is

Newton's gravity gives an acceleration $g(r)=GM/r^2$. What you feel as a stretching tidal force is not $g$ itself but how much it differs between your head and your feet — the derivative of the field, $\dfrac{dg}{dr}=-\dfrac{2GM}{r^3}$.

How it works

That extra power of $r$ matters enormously. The pull weakens as $1/r^2$, but the difference in pull across a fixed body weakens faster, as $1/r^3$. So the tidal stretch grows explosively as you approach, even while approaching a place where — surprisingly — a bigger black hole is gentler, because its horizon sits at larger $r$.

How it's applied

Fall toward a stellar-mass black hole and the $1/r^3$ tide between your head and feet reaches bone-snapping strength before you cross the horizon: spaghettification. Fall toward a supermassive one and you cross the horizon unharmed, because at that much larger radius the derivative of $g$ is tiny. The danger lives in the gradient, not the force.

2Tides as the derivative of gravity

∴ Step by step: why the stretch goes as 1 ⁄ r³

What hurts is not gravity itself but its difference across your body — and a difference over a small distance is a derivative.

  1. The field. At distance $r$ the gravitational acceleration is $g(r)=GM/r^2$.
  2. Compare two ends. Feet (at $r-\tfrac{\Delta r}{2}$) feel more than head (at $r+\tfrac{\Delta r}{2}$); the stretch is the difference $g(r-\tfrac{\Delta r}{2})-g(r+\tfrac{\Delta r}{2})$.
  3. A small difference is a derivative. For small $\Delta r$ that difference is $-\dfrac{dg}{dr}\,\Delta r$. Differentiating $g=GM\,r^{-2}$ gives $\dfrac{dg}{dr}=-2GM\,r^{-3}$.
  4. The tidal stretch. So the head-to-feet tide is $\dfrac{2GM}{r^3}\,\Delta r$ — falling as $1/r^3$, one power steeper than gravity's $1/r^2$.
  5. At the horizon. There $r=R_s=2GM/c^2$, so the tide $\propto\dfrac{2GM}{R_s^3}\propto\dfrac{1}{M^2}$ — bigger holes are gentler.
Conclusion: the danger lives in the gradient. That extra power of $r$ from differentiating is the whole reason spaghettification is lethal for small holes and survivable for giants.

Integration: adding up to energy and the horizon

The reverse of the derivative sums force over distance into energy — and out drops the escape velocity, and the size of a black hole

1Area under a curve

What it is

Integration is the limit of a sum of thin slices: $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is the area under $f$ between $a$ and $b$. The fundamental theorem of calculus says it undoes the derivative — integrating a rate gives back the total.

How it works

Work is force summed over distance, $W=\int F\,dr$. The energy to lift a mass out of a gravity well from radius $R$ to infinity is $\int_R^\infty \dfrac{GMm}{r^2}\,dr = \dfrac{GMm}{R}$. Set that equal to the kinetic energy $\tfrac12 m v^2$ a body would need, and the mass $m$ cancels: the escape velocity $v_{\text{esc}}=\sqrt{2GM/R}$ — square root and all — is an integral in disguise.

How it's applied

Now demand that not even light escapes, $v_{\text{esc}}=c$. Solving $\sqrt{2GM/R}=c$ for $R$ gives the Schwarzschild radius $R_s=2GM/c^2$ — the radius of the event horizon. Michell's Newtonian guess and Schwarzschild's exact relativistic solution land on the very same formula.

2Integrating gravity into an escape speed

∴ Step by step: from area under a curve to the horizon

Integration adds force over distance into energy; energy conservation then hands back the escape velocity, and demanding $v=c$ hands back the event horizon.

  1. Energy is force summed over distance. Work is $W=\int F\,dr$ — the area under the force curve, the limit of a sum of thin rectangles.
  2. Sum gravity from $R$ to infinity. $\displaystyle\int_R^\infty\frac{GMm}{r^2}\,dr=\Big[-\frac{GMm}{r}\Big]_R^\infty=\frac{GMm}{R}$ — the energy to climb out of the well.
  3. Match it to kinetic energy. A body just barely escaping turns all its launch kinetic energy into that climb: $\tfrac12 m v_{\text{esc}}^2=\dfrac{GMm}{R}$.
  4. Solve for the speed. The $m$ cancels: $v_{\text{esc}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2GM}{R}}$.
  5. Demand that light cannot escape. Set $v_{\text{esc}}=c$ and solve for the radius: $R_s=\dfrac{2GM}{c^2}$ — the event horizon.
Conclusion: the horizon is a definite integral solved for the radius at which escape needs light-speed — Michell's 1783 guess and Schwarzschild's 1916 exact result agreeing to the letter.

The chain rule and the chirp of merging holes

When one thing depends on another that depends on time, rates multiply — and a shrinking orbit becomes a rising tone

1Rates that ride on rates

What it is

The chain rule differentiates a function of a function: if $y=f(u)$ and $u=g(t)$, then $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\dfrac{dy}{du}\dfrac{du}{dt}$. Rates multiply along the chain. Its everyday face is related rates — how fast one quantity moves given how fast another does.

How it works

Two orbiting masses radiate gravitational waves, bleeding away energy. As the orbit shrinks, the orbital frequency climbs; the gravitational-wave frequency is twice the orbital one, and through the chain rule the rate it climbs depends on how fast the separation shrinks. The result is a signal whose frequency and amplitude both sweep upward — a chirp.

How it's applied

On 14 September 2015, LIGO recorded exactly this: a tone rising from 35 Hz to 250 Hz in a fifth of a second as two ~30-solar-mass black holes spiralled together. The rate of the rise encodes a single number, the chirp mass, $\mathcal{M}=\dfrac{(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}}{(m_1+m_2)^{1/5}}$ — read straight off the chain rule applied to the inspiral.

2The chain rule and the rising tone

∴ Step by step: why the frequency sweeps as (t_c − t)^(−3 ⁄ 8)

A merger's chirp is a chain of dependencies — frequency rides on orbital size, which rides on time as energy leaks away — and the chain rule multiplies the rates.

  1. The chain rule. If $y=f(u)$ and $u=g(t)$ then $\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\dfrac{dy}{du}\dfrac{du}{dt}$. Example: $\dfrac{d}{dt}\sin(t^2)=\cos(t^2)\cdot2t$.
  2. Frequency rides on orbital size. From Kepler, the wave frequency rises as the separation shrinks: $f\propto r^{-3/2}$.
  3. Size rides on time. Gravitational waves carry off energy ($E\propto-1/r$) at a rate set by the radiated power, giving $\dfrac{dr}{dt}\propto-r^{-3}$ — the orbit shrinks ever faster.
  4. Chain them. $\dfrac{df}{dt}=\dfrac{df}{dr}\dfrac{dr}{dt}\propto\big(r^{-5/2}\big)\big(r^{-3}\big)$, which re-expressed in $f$ becomes $\dfrac{df}{dt}\propto f^{11/3}$.
  5. Integrate. Solving that gives $f(t)\propto(t_c-t)^{-3/8}$: the frequency runs to infinity at the merger time $t_c$ — the chirp.
Conclusion: the rate of the rise encodes one number, the chirp mass $\mathcal{M}=(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}/(m_1+m_2)^{1/5}$ — so the masses are read off a slope, no distance required.

Taylor series and the slowing of time

Any smooth function is a polynomial in disguise — the tool that tames the square root in gravitational time dilation

1A curve as an infinite polynomial

What it is

A Taylor series rebuilds any smooth function near a point from its derivatives: $f(x)=f(0)+f'(0)x+\tfrac{1}{2}f''(0)x^2+\dots$ Truncate after a term or two and you have a simple polynomial that hugs the curve where it matters.

How it works

It is why $\sin\theta\approx\theta$ (first term) and why $\sqrt{1-x}\approx 1-\tfrac{x}{2}$ for small $x$. Awkward functions — square roots, exponentials, the relativistic factor — become transparent the moment you expand them, and the dropped terms tell you exactly how big the error is.

How it's applied

Near a black hole a clock runs slow by the factor $\sqrt{1-R_s/r}$ — a time dilation that goes to zero at the horizon, where time appears to freeze. Far away, expanding the square root gives $\approx 1-\tfrac{GM}{rc^2}$: the weak-field gravitational redshift that GPS satellites must correct for, recovered as the first Taylor term of the exact law.

2Building a Taylor series, then time dilation

∴ Step by step: turning a square root into a polynomial

A Taylor series matches a function's value and all its slopes at one point, so a couple of terms reproduce the curve nearby. Apply it to the relativistic clock factor and both regimes appear.

  1. The recipe. Near $x=0$, $f(x)=f(0)+f'(0)\,x+\tfrac12 f''(0)\,x^2+\dots$ — each term fixes the next derivative.
  2. Expand the square root. For $f(x)=(1-x)^{1/2}$: $f(0)=1$, $f'(0)=-\tfrac12$, $f''(0)=-\tfrac14$, so $\sqrt{1-x}\approx1-\dfrac{x}{2}-\dfrac{x^2}{8}-\dots$
  3. Insert the gravity term. A clock at radius $r$ ticks at $\dfrac{d\tau}{dt}=\sqrt{1-\dfrac{R_s}{r}}$; put $x=R_s/r$.
  4. Read the weak field. Keep one term: $\dfrac{d\tau}{dt}\approx1-\dfrac{R_s}{2r}=1-\dfrac{GM}{rc^2}$ — the tiny slowdown deep in any gravity well.
  5. Read the strong field. As $r\to R_s$, $x\to1$ and the series fails; only the exact root survives, $\sqrt{1-1}=0$ — time appears to freeze at the horizon.
Conclusion: one expansion carries you from parts-per-billion clock drift on a satellite to the frozen, infinitely redshifted surface of a black hole.

Exponentials, logarithms, and a black hole's faint glow

The function that is its own derivative governs thermal light, the brightness scale, and how a black hole's temperature hides in its mass

1Growth that feeds on itself

What it is

The exponential $e^x$ is the unique function equal to its own derivative, $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$. Its inverse, the logarithm, turns multiplication into addition and powers into straight lines — which is why astronomers plot so much on log scales, where vast ranges of size and brightness fit on one axis.

How it works

Thermal physics runs on the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E/kT}$: the chance of finding energy $E$ at temperature $T$. It shapes the Planck curve, sets how many atoms sit in each energy level (the strength of spectral lines), and on a log–log plot any power law $y\propto x^n$ becomes a straight line of slope $n$ — the astronomer's first diagnostic.

How it's applied

Hawking showed a black hole is not truly black: it glows with a temperature $T_H \propto 1/M$ — smaller holes are hotter. Its evaporation lifetime then scales as $t \propto M^3$, a power law that on a log plot is a ruler-straight line. A solar-mass hole is colder than deep space and would take $10^{67}$ years to evaporate; the numbers only become sayable in logarithms.

2Exponentials, logs, and the M³ lifetime

∴ Step by step: from T ∝ 1 ⁄ M to t ∝ M³

The exponential rules thermal light; the logarithm straightens power laws so a black hole's absurd lifetime becomes one slope.

  1. The self-reproducing function. $e^x$ is defined by $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$; from it comes the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E/kT}$ that shapes thermal spectra.
  2. Logs straighten powers. Take logs of $y=Ax^n$: $\log y=\log A+n\log x$ — a straight line of slope $n$. That is why power laws are plotted log–log.
  3. Hawking's temperature. Quantum theory at the horizon gives $T_H=\dfrac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi GM k_B}\propto\dfrac{1}{M}$: smaller holes are hotter.
  4. Its luminosity. A body radiates $L\propto A\,T^4$. The horizon area is $A\propto R_s^2\propto M^2$ and $T^4\propto M^{-4}$, so $L\propto M^2 M^{-4}=M^{-2}$.
  5. Hence the lifetime. Mass leaves at $\dfrac{dM}{dt}=-L/c^2\propto-M^{-2}$; integrating gives $t\propto M^3$, i.e. $\log t=3\log M+\text{const}$ — a line of slope 3.
Conclusion: the exponential sets the glow, the logarithm makes a $10^{67}$-year lifespan a number you can plot and pronounce.

Differential equations: the law of how things change

An equation for a rate, solved into a history — and the runaway that makes a black hole vanish in a final flash

1From a rule for change to a curve in time

What it is

A differential equation relates a quantity to its own rate of change, like $\dfrac{dN}{dt}=-\lambda N$. Solving it means finding the function that obeys the rule — here exponential decay $N(t)=N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$, the same math behind radioactive half-lives and cooling.

How it works

The solution carries the rule's fingerprint. A rate proportional to the amount gives smooth exponential decay; a rate proportional to a negative power gives something fiercer — a finite-time blow-up, where the quantity reaches zero at a definite moment rather than fading forever.

How it's applied

A black hole loses mass by Hawking radiation at a rate $\dfrac{dM}{dt}\propto -\dfrac{1}{M^2}$. Separating variables and integrating gives $M(t)\propto (t_{\text{end}}-t)^{1/3}$ — the mass hits zero at a finite time, and because the temperature is $\propto 1/M$, the final instant is a runaway burst of ever-hotter radiation: the black hole's death flash.

2Solving the evaporation equation

∴ Step by step: a rate law that ends in finite time

A differential equation states a rule for change; solving it by separating variables turns the rule into the whole life story — and this one ends with a bang, not a fade.

  1. Warm-up: ordinary decay. $\dfrac{dN}{dt}=-\lambda N$ separates to $\dfrac{dN}{N}=-\lambda\,dt$; integrating gives $N=N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$ — decay that never quite reaches zero.
  2. The black-hole rule. From the previous chapter, $\dfrac{dM}{dt}=-\dfrac{\alpha}{M^2}$: lighter (hotter) means faster loss.
  3. Separate the variables. $M^2\,dM=-\alpha\,dt$.
  4. Integrate both sides. $\dfrac{M^3}{3}=-\alpha t+C$; at $t=0$, $M=M_0$, so $C=M_0^3/3$.
  5. Solve and find the end. $M(t)=\big(M_0^3-3\alpha t\big)^{1/3}$, which hits zero at the finite time $t_{\text{end}}=\dfrac{M_0^3}{3\alpha}$ — and since $T\propto1/M$, the last instant is a runaway flash.
Conclusion: the negative-power rate gives a finite-time end, not an endless fade — a black hole's death is a scheduled event.

Answer: $M_0\approx1.7\times10^{11}$ kg — the mass of a small mountain, squeezed Big-Bang-tight, detonating somewhere in the sky right now.

2. That $M_0=1.73\times10^{11}$ kg converts entirely to energy in its final flash. Find $E=M_0 c^2$.
