Chapter 1 · Reading light · The fingerprint of an atom

Each element signs its name in missing colours

Absorption spectra · the barcode every atom imprints · why a distant galaxy's barcode arrives shifted whole

Light carries composition

When starlight reaches Earth and its astronomers, we can read what the star is made of. Spread the light into a spectrum and a continuous rainbow appears — but crossed by sharp dark gaps. Long ago physicists catalogued these gaps for every basic chemical element; each has its own absorption signature, a unique pattern of missing wavelengths.

The lines are a fixed barcode

A cool atom in the star's outer layers absorbs precisely the wavelengths it would itself emit — so hydrogen always removes the same colours, oxygen its own set, sodium its own. The pattern is a barcode fixed by atomic physics: in the laboratory those wavelengths never move. That fixity is what makes them useful as a ruler.

The whole barcode slides

Photograph the spectrum of a distant galaxy and the barcode is unmistakably the same pattern — same spacings, same ratios — but bodily displaced toward the red end. Nothing about the atoms changed; the entire signature has been shifted in wavelength between emission and detection.

A shift you can measure

Because the rest wavelengths are known to many digits, the displacement is a number you can read off: the redshift $z$. Chapters 2–7 are about what that one number means — a velocity, a distance, and finally a verdict on whether space itself is expanding.

∴ What the shift is, as a number

One definition, and the small-shift velocity that follows from it.

  1. Define the redshift. If a line emitted at rest wavelength $\lambda_{\text{rest}}$ is observed at $\lambda_{\text{obs}}$, the redshift is the fractional stretch $z=\dfrac{\lambda_{\text{obs}}-\lambda_{\text{rest}}}{\lambda_{\text{rest}}}$. So $\lambda_{\text{obs}}=(1+z)\,\lambda_{\text{rest}}$ — every line scales by the same factor, which is why the pattern never scrambles.
  2. Read the sign. $z>0$ is a redshift (receding); $z<0$ is a blueshift (approaching). The shift of one well-known line fixes $z$ for the whole spectrum.
  3. Turn it into a speed. For modest shifts the classical Doppler relation gives $v\approx cz$, with $c=299{,}792\ \text{km/s}$. A galaxy at $z=0.01$ recedes at roughly $3000\ \text{km/s}$.
The yield: a single measured displacement of the barcode becomes a recession velocity. Chapter 2 asks what that velocity is really telling us — and uncovers the surprise that the galaxies are barely moving at all.

Why the lines, not the colour, carry the information

A star's overall colour shifts with temperature and with reddening by dust — sloppy, model-dependent clues. The absorption lines are different: their rest wavelengths are atomic constants. Measuring the displacement of a known line rather than the hue of the continuum is what makes redshift a precise, falsifiable measurement instead of an impression.

Practice problems

1. The Hα line of hydrogen has rest wavelength $\lambda_{\text{rest}}=656.3$ nm. In a galaxy's spectrum it is found at $\lambda_{\text{obs}}=663.0$ nm. Find the redshift $z$.

Show solution
  1. Apply the definition. $z=\dfrac{663.0-656.3}{656.3}=\dfrac{6.7}{656.3}$.
  2. Evaluate. $z=0.01021$.
Answer: $z\approx0.0102$ — a one-percent stretch, placing the galaxy at a recession speed of about $cz\approx3060$ km/s.

2. Using $v\approx cz$ with $c=299{,}792$ km/s, what recession speed does $z=0.0102$ imply?

Show solution
  1. Multiply. $v=cz=299{,}792\times0.0102$.
  2. Evaluate. $v=3058$ km/s.
Answer: $v\approx3060$ km/s — valid because $z\ll1$; for large $z$ the relativistic formula of Chapter 7 is required.

3. A calcium line at rest is $393.4$ nm. In a quasar it is observed at $786.8$ nm. Show this is a redshift of $z=1.0$, and explain why $v=cz$ would be wrong here.

Show solution
  1. Compute $z$. $z=\dfrac{786.8-393.4}{393.4}=\dfrac{393.4}{393.4}=1.00$.
  2. Test the naïve speed. $cz=299{,}792$ km/s $=c$ — a speed equal to light, which is impossible for a massive galaxy.
Answer: $z=1.0$ doubles every wavelength. The linear $v=cz$ breaks down because it ignores relativity (and, cosmologically, the stretching of space); the proper treatment never reaches or exceeds $c$.
Chapter 2 · The interpretation · Velocity, or stretching space?

Mostly red, a few blue — and a balloon that explains both

Andromeda approaches at 300 km/s · almost everything else recedes · why the galaxies barely move while the distances grow

Almost everything is redshifting

Survey the sky and the overwhelming majority of galaxies show redshifts — their barcodes slid toward red. The natural first reading is recession: the objects are moving away. And the farther away they are, the larger the shift.

But a hundred are blue

About a hundred nearby galaxies blueshift instead. The Andromeda Galaxy (M31) is the famous case: a blueshift of roughly $300$ km/s ($186$ mi/s). It is falling toward us and will merge with the Milky Way in about four billion years. So redshift is not a universal law of light — it is a velocity readout, and velocities point both ways.

The galaxies aren't really travelling

Here is the twist. The distant galaxies are not so much flying through space as being carried apart by space itself. The expanding universe stretches the wavelength of a photon in flight; the redshift is cosmological, written into the journey, not into a local engine.

The balloon analogy

Ink dots on an inflating balloon all recede from one another, each seeing the rest fly away, none at the centre — because there is no centre on the surface. Nearby motions (Andromeda's infall) are local wrinkles riding on top of the global stretch, exactly as a dot can be nudged across the rubber while the rubber itself grows.

∴ Andromeda's blueshift, measured from a sodium line

The keynote's worked case — the same arithmetic that first revealed M31 was approaching.

  1. The data. Comparing a sodium (Na D) line in a nearby Milky-Way star with the same line in light from M31's core, the galaxy's line sits $\Delta\lambda=-6.3$ Å shortward of rest $\lambda=5890$ Å — a shift toward the blue.
  2. The Doppler formula. For small shifts $\dfrac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}=\dfrac{v}{c}$, so $v=\dfrac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}\,c$.
  3. Substitute. $v=\dfrac{-6.3}{5890}\times(3.00\times10^{5}\ \text{km/s})=-321\ \text{km/s}$.
The result: $v\approx-321$ km/s — the minus sign meaning approach. Andromeda is the proof that "redshift" is a directionless measurement of motion, not a one-way property of ageing light.

Hubble's law, and what its slope means

Uniform expansion makes recession speed proportional to distance: $v=H_0\,d$. The slope $H_0\approx70\ \text{km/s/Mpc}$ is the Hubble constant; its inverse $1/H_0$ is a rough age of the universe. Crucially, the law looks the same from every galaxy — there is no privileged centre, the balloon's defining feature. Measuring $v$ from a redshift (Chapter 1) and $d$ from a standard candle (Chapter 3) is how the slope is pinned down.

Practice problems

1. Reproduce the keynote's M31 result. A Na line at $\lambda=5890$ Å is shifted by $\Delta\lambda=-6.3$ Å. Using $v=(\Delta\lambda/\lambda)c$ with $c=3.00\times10^{5}$ km/s, find $v$.

Show solution
  1. Fractional shift. $\dfrac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}=\dfrac{-6.3}{5890}=-1.07\times10^{-3}$.
  2. Multiply by $c$. $v=-1.07\times10^{-3}\times3.00\times10^{5}=-321$ km/s.
Answer: $v=-321$ km/s — Andromeda approaching, the textbook blueshift.

2. A galaxy recedes at $v=7000$ km/s. With $H_0=70$ km/s/Mpc, how far away is it?

Show solution
  1. Invert Hubble's law. $d=v/H_0=7000/70$.
  2. Evaluate. $d=100$ Mpc.
Answer: $d=100$ Mpc $\approx326$ million light-years — distance read straight off a velocity, the engine of the cosmic distance ladder's far rungs.

3. Estimate a "Hubble age" of the universe as $1/H_0$. Convert $H_0=70$ km/s/Mpc to per-second using $1\ \text{Mpc}=3.086\times10^{19}$ km, then take the reciprocal.

Show solution
  1. Convert. $H_0=\dfrac{70}{3.086\times10^{19}}=2.27\times10^{-18}\ \text{s}^{-1}$.
  2. Reciprocal. $1/H_0=4.41\times10^{17}$ s.
  3. To years. $\div(3.156\times10^{7}\ \text{s/yr})=1.40\times10^{10}$ yr.
Answer: about $14$ billion years — strikingly close to the true age, because uniform expansion ties the slope of Hubble's law to a timescale.
Chapter 3 · The first yardstick · 1908 – 1929 · Leavitt, Cepheids & Hubble's plot

A pulsing star becomes a ruler to the galaxies

Henrietta Leavitt's period–luminosity law · Hubble's Cepheids in Andromeda · the 1929 velocity–distance plot · and why the candle had to be upgraded

The island-universe question

In the early 1900s nobody knew how big the universe was. Were the faint spiral nebulae just gas clouds inside our own Milky Way, or separate "island universes" — whole galaxies — impossibly far away? Hubble's working hypothesis was the bold one: they are distant galaxies. To prove it he needed a distance to a faint smudge, and there was no obvious way to measure one.

Leavitt's pulsing yardstick

The key had been forged in 1908–1912 by Henrietta Leavitt. Studying Cepheid variables — stars that rhythmically brighten and dim — in the Magellanic Clouds, she found a clean period–luminosity relation: the slower a Cepheid pulses, the more luminous it truly is. Time the blinking and you know the star's real brightness — a standard candle whose distance follows from how faint it looks.

Hubble points the 100-inch

Hubble found Cepheids in the Andromeda nebula, timed their periods, applied Leavitt's law, and got a distance far beyond the Milky Way — settling the debate: the spirals are galaxies. Then he paired such distances with Vesto Slipher's measured recession velocities (read from redshifts, Chapter 1) and plotted one against the other.

Velocity rises with distance

The 1929 plot was a straight line: the farther a galaxy, the faster it recedes — $v\approx H_0 d$, the same law Chapter 2 drew from the balloon, now seen in real data. (Hubble's slope came out near $500$ km/s/Mpc — badly miscalibrated — but the linear trend was unmistakable, and the expanding universe had its first observational law.)

Watch a Cepheid breathe — and read its true brightness

A Cepheid physically swells and shrinks, brightening and dimming on a clockwork period. Slide the period: the star pulses (slower for longer-period stars), the light curve traces its characteristic fast-rise/slow-decline shape, and Leavitt's law turns the period into an intrinsic luminosity. Longer period ⇒ a genuinely brighter star — that is the whole trick.

Why a Cepheid breathes — the helium valve

A Cepheid pulsates because a buried layer of partially ionized helium acts as a heat valve — the κ-mechanism, or "Eddington valve". Watch one cycle: when the star compresses, that ionization zone turns opaque and traps the radiation streaming up from the core; the dammed heat builds pressure and pushes the layers back out; expanded and cooled, the helium recombines, the valve turns clear, the heat escapes, and gravity pulls everything back down to begin again. This self-driving cycle only runs for stars crossing the instability strip.

∴ The κ-mechanism, one cycle

Why the pulsation drives itself instead of dying away, in six beats.

  1. Ordinary layers damp. Squeeze a normal gas and it heats and becomes more transparent, so trapped radiation leaks straight out and the push dies. That is why most stars don't throb.
  2. The helium valve reverses it. In the He⁺ → He⁺⁺ ionization zone, compression spends its energy ionizing helium rather than heating it — so the opacity $\kappa$ rises with compression instead of falling.
  3. Heat is dammed. The now-opaque layer blocks the radiation flowing up from the core, and pressure builds beneath it.
  4. The push-out. That excess pressure overpowers gravity and drives the outer layers outward — the star swells.
  5. Release. Expanded and cooled, the helium recombines (He⁺⁺ → He⁺), the opacity drops, and the dammed heat escapes.
  6. Fall-back. With the pressure spent, gravity pulls the layers back inward — and the cycle repeats.
Two consequences: the valve only sits at the right depth within a narrow temperature band — the instability strip — so only stars crossing it pulsate; and the period is a sound-crossing time set by mean density, $P \propto 1/\sqrt{\rho}$, so a puffier, more luminous Cepheid rings slower. That density–period link is the physical root of Leavitt's law.

∴ From blink rate to distance — the bottom rung of the ladder

Four steps turn a flickering star into a distance in megaparsecs.

  1. Time the pulse. Watch the light curve and read off the period $P$ (days) — pure timing, no model.
  2. Leavitt's law gives the true brightness. A longer period means a more luminous star: $M_V \approx -1.43 - 2.81\,\log_{10}P$, the absolute magnitude.
  3. Measure how faint it looks. The apparent magnitude $m_V$ is read straight from the image.
  4. The gap is the distance. $m_V - M_V = 5\log_{10}\!\big(d/10\,\text{pc}\big) \Rightarrow d$ — the same distance modulus used throughout the next chapter.
The yield: a model-free distance to any galaxy near enough to resolve its Cepheids. This is the bottom rung of the cosmic distance ladder; every longer-range method above it is calibrated against this one.

Why Cepheids hit a wall — and Type Ia take over

Cepheids are luminous, but not luminous enough: even the brightest fade below detection beyond roughly $40$ Mpc, so they cannot reach the deep universe where the expansion history — and its acceleration — reveal themselves. They must also be resolved as individual stars, so crowding and dust extinction bias them, and the period–luminosity zero-point drifts with a star's metallicity. A Type Ia supernova sidesteps all of this: for a few weeks it can outshine its entire host galaxy — about a hundred thousand times brighter than a Cepheid — so it is visible across billions of light-years. The two are not rivals but a ladder: Cepheids in nearby galaxies calibrate the Type Ia peak luminosity, and the supernovae then carry that calibration into the cosmological distance. The next chapter takes up that brighter candle.

Practice problems

1. A Cepheid pulses with period $P=10$ days. Using Leavitt's law $M_V=-1.43-2.81\log_{10}P$, find its absolute magnitude.

Show solution
  1. Log of the period. $\log_{10}10=1$.
  2. Apply the law. $M_V=-1.43-2.81(1)=-4.24$.
Answer: $M_V\approx-4.24$ — a star roughly four thousand times the Sun's luminosity, set entirely by its blink rate.

2. That same Cepheid is observed at apparent magnitude $m_V=24.0$ in a distant galaxy. Use $m_V-M_V=5\log_{10}(d/10\,\text{pc})$ to find the distance.

Show solution
  1. Distance modulus. $m_V-M_V=24.0-(-4.24)=28.24$.
  2. Solve. $\log_{10}(d/10\,\text{pc})=28.24/5=5.648$, so $d=10\times10^{5.648}$ pc.
  3. Evaluate. $d=10^{6.648}$ pc $=4.45\times10^{6}$ pc $=4.45$ Mpc.
Answer: $d\approx4.5$ Mpc — a model-free galaxy distance, the very measurement that proved the spirals lie far outside the Milky Way.

3. Hubble's 1929 slope was about $H_0=500$ km/s/Mpc. Estimate the implied "Hubble age" $1/H_0$ (use $1\,\text{Mpc}=3.086\times10^{19}$ km, $1\,\text{yr}=3.156\times10^{7}$ s) and say why it troubled astronomers.

Show solution
  1. Convert. $H_0=500/3.086\times10^{19}=1.62\times10^{-17}\ \text{s}^{-1}$.
  2. Reciprocal. $1/H_0=6.17\times10^{16}$ s $=1.96\times10^{9}$ yr.
Answer: about $2$ billion years — younger than the known age of Earth's rocks. The paradox was the clue that Hubble's distances were several times too small; fixing the calibration lowered $H_0$ toward today's $\sim70$ and the age toward $\sim14$ Gyr.
Chapter 4 · The distance ladder · A bulb of known wattage

If you know the true brightness, the dimness tells the distance

Type Ia supernovae as standard candles · white dwarfs at a universal trigger mass · the inverse-square law turned into a ruler

A velocity isn't a distance

Hubble's law needs both $v$ and $d$. Redshift hands us $v$; distance is the hard half. The trick is to find objects whose true luminosity we already know — standard candles — and let their apparent faintness do the ranging.

The light-bulb argument

Know a bulb's wattage, and its apparent brightness alone tells you how far off it is: brightness falls as the square of distance. Measure the flux you receive, compare to the known output, and invert the inverse-square law. The whole game is finding a cosmic bulb of known wattage.

Type Ia supernovae

A Type Ia supernova is the explosion of a white dwarf in a binary — the dead core of a star not massive enough to become a neutron star or black hole. It detonates near a universal mass threshold, so these explosions reach nearly the same peak luminosity every time: a standard candle bright enough to see across the cosmos.

A light curve you can recognise

Type Ia events also share a characteristic light curve — a distinctive rise and fall of brightness over weeks. That recognisable shape both confirms the candle's identity and, in Chapter 5, carries the single most decisive clue about expansion.

Push the supernova away — watch the flux fall as 1/d²

The explosion has a fixed true luminosity. Slide it outward and the light you collect drops as the square of distance. A candle of known $L$ turns a measured flux straight into a distance.

The modern many-supernova surveys

Edwin Hubble's 1929 diagram used just a few dozen galaxies and Cepheid distances. Type Ia supernovae took over as the long-range candle, and in 1998 two teams used them to discover the accelerating expansion. Today the Pantheon+ compilation fits about $1{,}550$ Type Ia supernovae spanning $0.001James Webb Space Telescope is sharpening their host-galaxy distances and pushing the sample to higher redshift. The single-candle physics of this chapter is real — but the cosmology is always read from the ensemble.

∴ From flux to distance, and the magnitude shorthand

The inverse-square law, then the logarithmic units astronomers actually use.

  1. Spread the light over a sphere. A source of luminosity $L$ seen from distance $d$ delivers a flux $F=\dfrac{L}{4\pi d^2}$ — the energy is diluted across the sphere's area.
  2. Invert for distance. If $L$ is known (the candle is standard), then $d=\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{4\pi F}}$ — a measured flux becomes a distance.
  3. Use magnitudes. Astronomers package the same ratio logarithmically as the distance modulus $m-M=5\log_{10}\!\big(d/10\,\text{pc}\big)$, where $M$ (absolute) is the candle's known output and $m$ (apparent) is what we measure.
The yield: a distance to pair with the redshift velocity — the two numbers Hubble's law needs. With both in hand, the next chapter finds a feature of the light curves that no static-universe model can fake.

Why a white dwarf makes such a good candle

A white dwarf accreting from a companion approaches the Chandrasekhar mass ($\approx1.4\,M_\odot$), a sharp physical threshold set by quantum pressure. Detonation at a nearly universal mass means a nearly universal energy release — hence a repeatable peak luminosity. Small variations correlate with light-curve width and are calibrated out, tightening the candle to a few-percent distance precision.

Practice problems

1. Two identical supernovae sit at $50$ Mpc and $150$ Mpc. By what factor is the nearer one brighter?

Show solution
  1. Inverse-square ratio. $\dfrac{F_{50}}{F_{150}}=\left(\dfrac{150}{50}\right)^2$.
  2. Evaluate. $=3^2=9$.
Answer: $9\times$ brighter — three times closer means nine times the flux, the steep leverage that makes faint candles into precise distances.

2. A Type Ia has absolute magnitude $M=-19.3$. It is observed at apparent magnitude $m=16.7$. Find its distance from $m-M=5\log_{10}(d/10\,\text{pc})$.

Show solution
  1. Distance modulus. $m-M=16.7-(-19.3)=36.0$.
  2. Solve. $\log_{10}(d/10\,\text{pc})=36.0/5=7.2$, so $d=10\times10^{7.2}$ pc.
  3. Evaluate. $d=10^{8.2}$ pc $=1.585\times10^{8}$ pc $=158$ Mpc.
Answer: $d\approx158$ Mpc — a distance pulled from brightness alone, ready to pair with the object's redshift.

3. The same candle is moved from $100$ Mpc to $400$ Mpc. By how many magnitudes does it dim? (Use $\Delta m=5\log_{10}(d_2/d_1)$.)

Show solution
  1. Distance ratio. $d_2/d_1=400/100=4$.
  2. Magnitude change. $\Delta m=5\log_{10}4=5\times0.602=3.01$.
Answer: about $3$ magnitudes fainter — a factor $\approx16$ in flux, the same $1/d^2$ dimming the lab makes visible.
Chapter 5 · The verdict · Expansion's unforgeable signature

Distant explosions fade in slow motion

Light curves stretched by (1+z) · cosmic time dilation · the observation a tired-light universe cannot reproduce

A clock you can watch explode

Every Type Ia supernova rises and fades on the same intrinsic schedule — a built-in clock. So if anything stretches the arrival times of a distant explosion's light, we can detect it by simply timing how long the event appears to last.

Expansion should slow the clock

If space is expanding, later photons travel across a slightly larger universe than earlier ones — so the whole event arrives spread out in time. The prediction is sharp: a supernova at redshift $z$ should appear to evolve slower by exactly the factor $(1+z)$. This is cosmic time dilation.

The measurement

Astronomers timed the light curves of supernovae across a range of redshifts. The distant ones genuinely last longer — their light curves are stretched, and stretched by precisely $(1+z)$. The same factor that reddens the light also slows its clock.

A discriminating result

This is the crux of the keynote's comparison. A redshift could, in principle, come from many causes — but only an expanding universe stretches time along with wavelength. A photon that merely lost energy en route would redden without ever slowing the explosion's clock. The data show the slowing. The clock decides.

Stretch a supernova's light curve — and pick the model

The template is a nearby ($z=0$) Type Ia light curve. Raise the redshift, then choose what nature does to it: an expanding universe widens the curve by $(1+z)$ in time, while a tired-light photon-decay model reddens it but leaves its duration untouched. Only one matches the observations.

One factor, two faces

The relation $\Delta t_{\text{obs}}=(1+z)\,\Delta t_{\text{emit}}$ is not an extra assumption bolted on — it is the time-domain twin of $\lambda_{\text{obs}}=(1+z)\,\lambda_{\text{rest}}$. A wave's wavelength and its period are stretched by the same expansion, because both are just "distance between successive crests" measured in space and in time. Confirming the time stretch independently confirms that the wavelength stretch is genuine expansion, not energy loss.

"Type Ia supernovae show cosmic time dilation: distant ones appear to evolve and fade slower, their light curves stretched by a factor of $(1+z)$ — confirming the expansion of the universe and Einstein's relativity."

Practice problems

1. A nearby Type Ia takes $20$ days to fade by a set amount. At $z=0.5$, how long should the same fade appear to take in an expanding universe?

Show solution
  1. Stretch factor. $1+z=1.5$.
  2. Apply it. $\Delta t_{\text{obs}}=1.5\times20=30$ days.
Answer: $30$ days — fully half again as long, a stretch large enough to measure cleanly, and exactly what is seen.

2. A distant supernova's light curve is observed to last $1.8\times$ as long as a local one. What redshift does the time-dilation factor imply?

Show solution
  1. Set the factor. $1+z=1.8$.
  2. Solve. $z=0.8$.
Answer: $z=0.8$ — and the wavelength redshift measured from the same spectrum agrees, the consistency that nails expansion.

3. A tired-light model says photons lose energy over distance but arrival times are unaffected. What apparent light-curve duration does it predict for the $z=0.5$ supernova of problem 1, and how does that test the model?

Show solution
  1. Tired-light prediction. No time stretch: $\Delta t_{\text{obs}}=20$ days, unchanged from the local value.
  2. Compare with data. Observation gives $\approx30$ days, matching $(1+z)$ expansion, not the flat tired-light value.
Answer: tired light predicts $20$ days; the sky shows $30$. The measured slowing falsifies energy-loss redshift and confirms expansion.
Chapter 6 · The alternative · A tempting idea that fails the tests

What if the light just grew tired on the way?

Photons losing energy to plasma, gravity, the microwave background · why every clean test still points to expansion

Space is not empty

A photon's journey can span billions of light-years through space that is not a pure vacuum: it is threaded with plasma (gas of charged particles), bathed in the cosmic microwave background, and woven with gravitational fields. It is reasonable to ask whether the light is altered along the way.

The tired-light hypothesis

The tired light idea proposes exactly that: photons gradually lose energy over distance — to gravity, to interactions with the plasma — so more distant light arrives redder. It would reproduce Hubble's law without any expansion, a static universe in which only the photons age.

Confront it with data

A good alternative must match every observation, not just the one it was built for. Tired light explains the redshift–distance trend by construction — so the decisive tests are the things it didn't set out to explain: the timing of supernovae, the brightness of distant galaxies, the spectrum of the microwave background.

Where it breaks

It fails the clock. Energy loss reddens a photon but cannot stretch the duration of an event — yet distant supernovae demonstrably fade in slow motion by $(1+z)$ (Chapter 5). Add the surface-brightness and microwave-background tests below and the static picture cannot stand. Expansion survives every test at once.

The Tolman surface-brightness test

The cleanest discriminator after time dilation. In an expanding universe the surface brightness of a galaxy falls as $(1+z)^{-4}$ — one factor of $(1+z)$ each from time dilation, photon-energy redshift, and two from angular-area dimming. A static tired-light universe predicts only $(1+z)^{-1}$. The steep observed dimming matches expansion, not energy loss. Together with the perfect blackbody form of the cosmic microwave background — which scattering-based tired light would smear — the evidence converges hard.

Practice problems

1. At $z=1$, by what factor does surface brightness dim under expansion $(1+z)^{-4}$ versus tired light $(1+z)^{-1}$?

Show solution
  1. Expansion. $(1+1)^{-4}=2^{-4}=1/16$.
  2. Tired light. $(1+1)^{-1}=1/2$.
Answer: expansion dims by $16\times$, tired light by only $2\times$ — an eightfold difference the Tolman test resolves decisively in favour of expansion.

2. A tired-light model tuned to fit Hubble's law predicts no light-curve stretching. Supernovae at $z=0.3,0.6,0.9$ are observed to last $1.3,1.6,1.9\times$ a local event. Which model do the numbers support?

Show solution
  1. Expansion prediction. $1+z=1.3,1.6,1.9$ — identical to the data.
  2. Tired-light prediction. $1.0$ at every redshift — flatly contradicted.
Answer: the stretch tracks $(1+z)$ exactly, supporting expansion; tired light's "no stretch" is ruled out at every redshift.

3. Tired light needs photons to lose energy to intervening matter. Qualitatively, why would such scattering also blur distant galaxies — and what does the sharpness of real images imply?

Show solution
  1. Scattering deflects. Any interaction that drains a photon's energy will, in general, also change its direction, smearing point sources into fuzz.
  2. Observation. Distant galaxies are imaged sharply, with no redshift-dependent blurring.
Answer: energy-loss-by-collision should blur images; the crispness of high-$z$ galaxies argues against it, while cosmological redshift (pure wavelength stretch) introduces no blur.
Chapter 7 · High speeds · When v ≈ cz is no longer allowed

The relativistic Doppler effect

Why the naïve $v=cz$ breaks · folding time dilation into the shift · the factor $\sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}$

The classical shift, recalled

For everyday speeds the Doppler shift is linear: $z\approx\beta$, where $\beta=v/c$. It works for Andromeda and for nearby galaxies. But push $\beta$ toward $1$ and it predicts $z$ growing without limit and velocities exceeding $c$ — nonsense.

The moving source also runs slow

Relativity adds a second effect the classical formula omits: a source rushing away is itself time-dilated, its emission clock ticking slow by the Lorentz factor $\gamma$. The observed shift is the classical wave-stretching multiplied by that slowdown.

The combined formula

The two effects merge into the relativistic Doppler effect: $1+z=\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}}$. At small $\beta$ it reduces to $z\approx\beta$; as $\beta\to1$, $z\to\infty$ smoothly — without the velocity ever reaching $c$.

Why it matters here

Quasars and the fastest jets reach $z$ of several. Interpreting those shifts demands the relativistic formula — and understanding why it has a $\gamma$ in it sends us, in Chapter 8, to the Lorentz transformation and a single bouncing photon.

Classical vs relativistic — watch them part company

Raise the speed $\beta=v/c$. At low speed the linear $z=\beta$ and the relativistic $z=\sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}-1$ agree. As $\beta$ climbs they diverge sharply: relativity bends upward toward infinite redshift while velocity stays below $c$.

∴ Building the relativistic Doppler factor

Classical wave-stretching, times the source's own time dilation.

  1. Classical recession stretch. In the time between two crests the source moves away, lengthening the wavelength by $(1+\beta)$ in the observer's frame.
  2. The source clock runs slow. The emitter is moving, so its period is dilated by $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$ — fewer crests per second leave it than its rest frame would say.
  3. Combine. $1+z=\gamma\,(1+\beta)=\dfrac{1+\beta}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}=\dfrac{1+\beta}{\sqrt{(1-\beta)(1+\beta)}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}}$.
The result: $1+z=\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}}$. The whole correction over the classical formula is that factor of $\gamma$ — and the next chapter derives $\gamma$ itself from nothing but a light clock and Pythagoras.

When do you actually need relativity?

Relativistic Doppler effects matter only when speeds approach $c$ (or, cosmologically, when distances are vast). One of Einstein's postulates is that light travels at the same speed for every observer; holding that fixed forces moving clocks to run slow, which is the $\gamma$ above. For everyday speeds far below $c$, $\gamma\to1$ and the humble Galilean formula $z\approx\beta$ is all you need.

Practice problems

1. A jet recedes at $\beta=0.6$. Find the Lorentz factor $\gamma$ and the relativistic redshift $z$.

Show solution
  1. Lorentz factor. $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.6^2}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.64}}=1.25$.
  2. Redshift. $1+z=\sqrt{\dfrac{1.6}{0.4}}=\sqrt{4}=2$, so $z=1.0$.
Answer: $\gamma=1.25$, $z=1.0$ — whereas the naïve $z=\beta$ would have said $0.6$, a $40\%$ error at this speed.

2. A quasar shows $z=2$. Treating it as a pure relativistic Doppler shift, what recession $\beta$ does $1+z=\sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}$ give?

Show solution
  1. Square it. $(1+z)^2=9=\dfrac{1+\beta}{1-\beta}$.
  2. Solve. $9(1-\beta)=1+\beta\Rightarrow 8=10\beta\Rightarrow\beta=0.8$.
Answer: $\beta=0.8$ — comfortably below $1$, unlike the naïve $cz=2c$ the linear law would demand.

3. Show the relativistic formula reduces to the classical one for small $\beta$. Expand $\sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}$ to first order in $\beta$.

Show solution
  1. First-order expansions. $(1+\beta)^{1/2}\approx1+\tfrac{\beta}{2}$ and $(1-\beta)^{-1/2}\approx1+\tfrac{\beta}{2}$.
  2. Multiply. $\left(1+\tfrac{\beta}{2}\right)\left(1+\tfrac{\beta}{2}\right)\approx1+\beta$, so $z\approx\beta$.
Answer: $z\approx\beta$ — the classical Doppler law is the low-speed limit, which is why $v=cz$ is safe for nearby galaxies.
Chapter 8 · The foundation · Where γ comes from

One bouncing photon, Pythagoras, and the Lorentz factor

The light clock · constant light speed forces time to bend · deriving $\gamma$ and the time-dilation that powers the relativistic Doppler shift

Changing perspective

In a spacetime diagram you are always at the origin; to describe someone moving relative to you, you must transform their coordinates onto yours. The crude "just slide everything over" recipe — a shear transformation — has a fatal flaw: it changes every velocity, including light's.

Light's speed is sacred

Because light travels at the same speed from a moving source as from a still one, any valid change of perspective must leave the speed of light unchanged in all directions. The transformation that does this — leaving one speed invariant while adjusting all others — is the Lorentz transformation.

A clock made of light

To see the consequence, build a light clock: a photon bouncing between two mirrors, one tick per bounce off the lower mirror. Stationary, the photon goes straight up and down. Set the clock moving, and from our frame the photon must travel a longer, diagonal path — yet at the same speed $c$.

So the moving clock runs slow

Same speed, longer path, therefore more time per tick: the moving clock ticks slower. Pythagoras turns that picture into the exact factor $\gamma$ — the very $\gamma$ that sits inside the relativistic Doppler formula of Chapter 7.

A cat gliding past traces a tilted line — and the tilt is its velocity, distance over time. From the cat's own point of view it sits still and you are the tilted line, inverted. To compare the two perspectives fairly we must re-draw the grid so one of them stands upright — but how we re-draw it is the whole story.

∴ Why a shear fails and a Lorentz boost works

The cat-and-person argument, in four steps.

  1. A velocity is an angle. On the $X$–$T$ diagram, a worldline's tilt from vertical is exactly distance-over-time — its speed. So changing someone's speed means changing the angle of their line.
  2. The naïve fix shears. To put the cat upright you could just slide every row sideways until the cat's line stands vertical (a shear transformation). It works for the cat — but because it tips all lines by an amount that grows with time, it changes every velocity, the $45°$ light line included.
  3. But light's angle is sacred. Light travels at the same speed from a moving source as a still one, so its line must stay at $45°$ in every frame. A shear breaks that — its measured light speed becomes $(1-u)\,c$ — so a shear cannot be the right way to change perspective.
  4. Lorentz rotates about the light line. The correct move scissors the space and time axes symmetrically toward the $45°$ line, leaving that one speed fixed while every other speed changes. The light line is unchanged in all directions — and that single requirement is the Lorentz transformation.
The payoff: holding light's angle fixed forces time and space themselves to skew between observers. That skew is time dilation and length contraction — and the light clock below turns it into the exact factor $\gamma$.

∴ The light-clock derivation of time dilation

Exactly the keynote's chain: one right triangle, solved for the period.

  1. Stationary clock. The photon crosses a gap $L_0$ at speed $c$, so a half-tick is $T_0/2=L_0/c$, i.e. the rest period is $T_0=2L_0/c$.
  2. Moving clock, the triangle. In our frame the clock moves a distance $vT/2$ sideways while the photon climbs $L_0$, so the photon's diagonal is the hypotenuse: $\left(\dfrac{cT}{2}\right)^2=L_0^2+\left(\dfrac{vT}{2}\right)^2$.
  3. Solve for $T$. $\dfrac{c^2T^2}{4}-\dfrac{v^2T^2}{4}=L_0^2\Rightarrow \dfrac{T^2}{4}(c^2-v^2)=L_0^2\Rightarrow T=\dfrac{2L_0}{\sqrt{c^2-v^2}}$.
  4. Factor out $c$. $T=\dfrac{2L_0/c}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}=\dfrac{T_0}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}=\gamma\,T_0$.
The result: $T=\gamma T_0$ with $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta^2}}$ — the moving clock's tick is stretched by $\gamma$. Drop this $\gamma$ into Chapter 7 and the relativistic Doppler factor $\sqrt{(1+\beta)/(1-\beta)}$ is fully explained, from a single photon up.

Length contraction, and when to bother

Run the same light clock lying along the direction of motion and the geometry instead delivers length contraction: moving rulers shrink by $1/\gamma$. Both effects are two readings of one Lorentz transformation. And both vanish for everyday speeds — when $\beta\ll1$, $\gamma\to1$ and the world is safely Galilean. Relativity is only forced on us by the stubborn constancy of $c$ at extreme speeds and cosmic distances — the same constancy that made the redshift such a clean cosmological ruler in the first place.

Practice problems

1. A light clock moves at $\beta=0.8$. By what factor $\gamma$ does its tick lengthen?

Show solution
  1. Apply the formula. $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.8^2}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}}$.
  2. Evaluate. $\gamma=\dfrac{1}{0.6}=1.667$.
Answer: $\gamma\approx1.67$ — the moving clock ticks two-thirds as fast, i.e. its tick lasts $1.67\times$ longer.

2. Use the light-clock triangle directly. With $L_0=1$ (in units where $c=1$) and $v=0.6$, solve $\left(\tfrac{T}{2}\right)^2=1+\left(\tfrac{0.6\,T}{2}\right)^2$ for the period $T$, and compare to $T_0=2$.

Show solution
  1. Expand. $\dfrac{T^2}{4}=1+\dfrac{0.36\,T^2}{4}\Rightarrow \dfrac{T^2}{4}(1-0.36)=1$.
  2. Solve. $T^2=\dfrac{4}{0.64}=6.25\Rightarrow T=2.5$.
  3. Ratio. $T/T_0=2.5/2=1.25=\gamma$.
Answer: $T=2.5$, i.e. $\gamma=1.25$ — Pythagoras alone reproduces the Lorentz factor for $\beta=0.6$.

  2. Via the closed form. $\sqrt{1.6/0.4}=\sqrt{4}=2.0$ — identical.
Answer: $z=1.0$ both ways — the light-clock $\gamma$ feeds straight into the relativistic Doppler shift, closing the loop from a bouncing photon to a quasar's redshift.
