The planet that
never was

Mercury's orbit does not quite close: its perihelion creeps forward by an amount Newton's gravity could not fully account for — a stubborn 43 arcseconds per century. The same astronomer who found Neptune from a wobble, Urbain Le Verrier, declared the culprit a hidden inner planet he named Vulcan. People claimed to see it for fifty years. It was never there. The leftover was finally explained in 1915 not by a new planet but by a new theory of gravity — Einstein's general relativity, which predicts 43″/century exactly.

A direct sequel to the Neptune companion: same astronomer, same anomaly-hunting method — opposite ending. There the missing mass was real; here the equations themselves had to change.

the anomaly

A perihelion that will not stay put

What "perihelion precession" actually looks like

A single planet around a point-mass Sun traces a closed ellipse — it returns to the same perihelion (closest point) every orbit, forever. But Mercury's perihelion slowly rotates: each orbit the long axis of the ellipse swings a tiny bit further around the Sun, so the path is not a closed ellipse but a slowly turning rosette. The rate is tiny — a fraction of an arcsecond per orbit — but it accumulates, and by the 19th century it was measured with brutal precision.

A precessing ellipse

The key idea first: a single orbit is always a normal, closed ellipse — the shape you see is exactly what Mercury's orbit looks like at any moment. There is no special "correct" perihelion angle; every value is an ordinary ellipse, just pointing a different way. Precession is that this angle slowly drifts over time, so across the centuries the orbit takes every orientation in turn. Drag the slider to step forward in time: the faint grey ellipse is the starting orbit, the bright one is the orbit later, and the angle between their long axes is the precession. (The drift is hugely sped up here — Mercury's real rate is only ~1.4″ per orbit.) The eccentricity slider sets how elongated the orbit is; Mercury's real $e\approx0.206$.

How big is the real effect?

Mercury's perihelion advances about 574″ per century as measured against the fixed stars — but that is spread over the ~415 orbits Mercury completes in a century, so it is only about 1.4″ per orbit: a rotation you would never notice in a single loop. Almost all of it is ordinary Newtonian physics. The whole drama is about the last 43″/century that ordinary physics leaves behind.

Where does 574 arcseconds come from?

Most of the precession is bookkeeping and planetary tugs. The mystery is the small remainder that refuses to balance.

1859 · the accounting

The precession budget — and the 43″ that won't balance

Adding up every known cause, and finding it isn't enough

To test gravity you account for everything first. Mercury's observed perihelion advance (relative to the fixed stars) is 574.10 ± 0.65 ″/century. The overwhelming share is the gravitational tug of the other planets — mostly Venus, Jupiter, and Earth — which Newtonian celestial mechanics predicts at 532.30 ″/century. A whisker more comes from the Sun's slight oblateness. Add the known Newtonian effects and you are still left short by about 43 ″/century. That is the anomaly Le Verrier published in 1859.

Le Verrier's number

Le Verrier's 1859 analysis put the leftover near 38″/century; refined data and the modern reduction settle it at ~43″/century. Either way it was a real, stubborn residual far larger than the measurement error — exactly the kind of "this doesn't add up" that, a decade earlier, had led him straight to Neptune.

The man who found Neptune reaches for the same answer

A missing residual worked once. Why not twice?

1859–1915 · the hunt

Vulcan: a planet hunted for fifty years

The most reasonable wrong idea in 19th-century astronomy

Le Verrier's logic was impeccable by precedent. In 1846 an unexplained residual in Uranus's motion had pointed him to a real new planet, Neptune, found within a degree of his prediction. So when Mercury came up short, he applied the same playbook: an unseen mass close to the Sun — a planet (or a ring of asteroids) he named Vulcan — whose gravity would supply the missing tug. On 2 January 1860 he announced it. Coming from the discoverer of Neptune, the claim carried enormous weight.

The hypothesis · 1859–60

Le Verrier attributes Mercury's ~38″/century residual to Vulcan, an intra-Mercurial planet orbiting between Mercury and the Sun — invisible in the solar glare except during a transit or a total eclipse.

The "confirmation" · 1859

Country physician Edmond Lescarbault reports a small black dot crossing the Sun on 26 March 1859 — a transit of Vulcan. Le Verrier visits him unannounced, inspects the homemade instrument, and (despite misgivings) endorses the sighting. Vulcan seems real.

The searches · 1860s–1900s

Eclipse expeditions hunt for Vulcan in 1883, 1887, 1889, 1900, 1901, 1905, 1908. A scatter of claimed sightings never reconciles. At the 1878 eclipse, respected observers Watson and Swift each report a ruddy object near the Sun — but their positions disagree with each other and with every known star.

The verdict · 1908

Campbell and Perrine at Lick Observatory close the case: deep eclipse photography shows nothing down to faint limits. No planet large enough to move Mercury's perihelion could have hidden from all those searches.

Why the same method failed this time

For Neptune, the residual genuinely was another mass, and inverting the gravity gave a direction that worked (see the Neptune companion for that geometry). For Mercury, no mass could fit: a body big enough to shift the perihelion by 43″/century would be bright enough — and perturb Venus and Earth enough — to have been caught. The anomaly was telling astronomers something Newtonian gravity simply could not say. The problem wasn't a missing planet. It was the law.

Not a new planet — a new gravity

In November 1915 the 43 arcseconds fell out of pure geometry.

1915 · the resolution

General relativity: the 43″ from curved spacetime

No extra mass — just the Sun bending space and time

In Einstein's general relativity the Sun's mass curves spacetime, and a planet follows the straightest path through that curvature. Close to the Sun this departs subtly from a Newtonian ellipse: the orbit no longer quite closes, and the perihelion advances. For a small eccentricity the extra advance per orbit is

$$\Delta\varphi \;=\; \frac{6\pi G M_\odot}{c^2\,a\,(1-e^2)} \;\;\text{(radians per orbit)} \;=\; \frac{24\pi^3 a^2}{c^2 T^2 (1-e^2)},$$

with $a$ the semi-major axis, $e$ the eccentricity, $T$ the orbital period, $c$ the speed of light. The key feature: the effect grows as the orbit shrinks (the $1/a$, and through $T$ another strong dependence) and as eccentricity rises ($1/(1-e^2)$). Mercury — closest in and most eccentric — feels it most. Plug Mercury's numbers in and you get 43″/century, with no free parameters. Einstein wrote that he was so excited he had heart palpitations.

Watch the orbit fail to close

This is "the orbit no longer quite closes" made visible. With zero advance the ellipse retraces itself every loop — the planet returns to the same perihelion forever (Newton). Why this counted as proof, not just a fit

  1. No tuning. The 43″ comes out of $G$, $M_\odot$, $c$ and Mercury's known orbit — quantities fixed by other measurements. There was no knob to turn to make it match.
  2. It was a postdiction and a prediction. The anomaly was already measured, so GR had to hit a number it could not miss — and it did, to the decimal. The same theory then predicted starlight deflection, confirmed at the 1919 eclipse.
  3. It killed Vulcan. Once the residual was accounted for by gravity itself, no inner planet was needed — and none was ever found.
The lesson: an anomaly can mean "there is something we haven't seen yet" (Neptune) or "our law is incomplete" (Mercury). Same symptom, two profoundly different cures — and only experiment can tell which is which.
recap

The arc of the story

The anomaly

Mercury's perihelion advances 574″/century; Newtonian planets + Sun oblateness explain ~532″, leaving a stubborn ~43″/century.

The first guess

Le Verrier — fresh from Neptune — blames a hidden inner planet, Vulcan (announced 1860). The method that found one planet is reused to invent another.

The dead end

Fifty years of transits and eclipse expeditions turn up nothing real. No mass can fit the data without breaking everything else.

The cure

General relativity (1915) produces 43″/century from spacetime curvature alone — no new mass, no free parameters. Vulcan vanishes.

§Practice problems

1. Mercury's observed perihelion advance is 574.10 ″/century; Newtonian planetary tugs give 532.30 ″/century and solar oblateness 0.03 ″/century. What residual is left, and what supplies it?

Show solution
  1. Subtract the known Newtonian terms. $574.10 - 532.30 - 0.03 = 41.77$ ″/century.
  2. Compare to GR. General relativity predicts $42.98$ ″/century; the small difference is measurement uncertainty (±0.65) plus the tiny Lense–Thirring term.
Answer: about 42 ″/century, supplied by general relativity — the anomaly Le Verrier blamed on Vulcan.

2. Using $\Delta\varphi = \dfrac{6\pi G M_\odot}{c^2 a (1-e^2)}$ per orbit, with $GM_\odot=1.327\times10^{20}$, $c=3.00\times10^{8}$, $a=5.79\times10^{10}$ m, $e=0.206$, find the advance per orbit in arcseconds.

Show solution
  1. Numerator. $6\pi\,GM_\odot = 6\pi(1.327\times10^{20}) = 2.50\times10^{21}$.
  2. Denominator. $c^2 a (1-e^2) = (9.0\times10^{16})(5.79\times10^{10})(0.958) = 4.99\times10^{27}$.
  3. Ratio. $\Delta\varphi = 5.02\times10^{-7}$ rad. Times $206265$ ″/rad $= 0.104$ ″.
Answer: ≈ 0.10 ″ per orbit. Over ~415 orbits per century that is ≈ 43 ″/century.

3. The GR advance per century scales roughly as $1/\big(a^{5/2}(1-e^2)\big)$ (combining the per-orbit $1/a$ with $\sim a^{3/2}$ orbits per century). Roughly how many times smaller is Earth's GR precession than Mercury's, ignoring eccentricity?

Show solution
  1. Distance ratio. Earth $a=1.00$ AU vs Mercury $a=0.387$ AU, so $a_\oplus/a_\text{Me}=2.58$.
  2. Apply the power. $2.58^{2.5}\approx 10.7$.
Answer: roughly 10× smaller — about 4 ″/century for Earth vs ~43 for Mercury, which is exactly why Mercury was the planet that exposed the discrepancy.
