Chapter 1 · The afterglow · A blackbody at 2.725 K

The Big Bang left the whole sky faintly glowing

Leftover radiation · why it is invisibly cold · why its light peaks in the microwave · and the static on an untuned TV

Heat left over from the Big Bang

The cosmic microwave background is leftover radiation from the Big Bang — the heat of the early universe, stretched and cooled by 13.8 billion years of expansion. It fills all of space, arriving from every direction at once. It is not light from any star or galaxy; it is the glow of the universe itself when it was young and hot.

So cold it is invisible

That glow is now just $2.725$ degrees above absolute zero — far too cold for human eyes. A body at that temperature radiates most strongly not as visible light but in the microwave part of the electromagnetic spectrum, with a wavelength of about a millimetre. To see the afterglow you need a radio dish, not a telescope's mirror.

You have already seen it

According to NASA, before cable, a television tuned to an empty channel showed a faint snow of static — and a small fraction of that hiss was the cosmic microwave background itself, the afterglow of the Big Bang picked up by the antenna. The oldest light in the universe was leaking into living rooms as noise.

A near-perfect blackbody

Spread the CMB out by wavelength and it traces a blackbody spectrum — the exact thermal curve of an object in equilibrium at one temperature. That perfect thermal shape is the fingerprint of a hot, dense origin, and it is the single hardest fact for any rival to the Big Bang to explain.

Heat the early universe — watch the blackbody peak slide

The curve is the Planck spectrum $B_\nu(T)$ — the brightness of a blackbody at temperature $T$, across frequency. Drag the temperature up from today's $2.725$ K toward the $\sim3000$ K of recombination: the whole curve brightens and its peak marches from the microwave band up through the infrared toward visible light. Today's cold CMB peaks squarely in the microwave, near $160$ GHz (about a millimetre).

∴ Wien's law, and why a colder universe glows redder-er

Two short facts fix where the afterglow peaks today and where it peaked in the past.

  1. Wien's displacement law. A blackbody's peak wavelength is inversely proportional to its temperature: $\lambda_{\max}=b/T$ with $b=2.898\times10^{-3}\ \text{m·K}$. At $T=2.725$ K this gives $\lambda_{\max}=1.06$ mm — a microwave.
  2. Equivalently, in frequency. $\nu_{\max}\approx(58.8\ \text{GHz/K})\,T=160$ GHz today — the microwave band, exactly where radio astronomers find it.
  3. The temperature–redshift link. Expansion stretches every wavelength by $(1+z)$, so the CMB was hotter in the past: $T(z)=T_0(1+z)$. Run the slider to $T=3000$ K and you are viewing redshift $z=T/T_0-1\approx1100$ — the epoch of recombination in Chapter 3.
The yield: the same number you read off the slider — temperature — doubles as a clock. A blackbody at $2.725$ K is the universe at age 13.8 Gyr; at $3000$ K it is the universe at 380,000 years, the moment its light was set free.

Why a perfect blackbody is such damning evidence

A blackbody spectrum only forms in tight thermal equilibrium — matter and radiation colliding so often they share one temperature. The early universe was exactly that: a hot, opaque plasma. The CMB measured by the COBE satellite matched a $2.725$ K blackbody to better than one part in ten thousand — the most perfect blackbody ever measured. No model in which the redshift comes from light merely "tiring" on its journey can reproduce that thermal curve; only an expanding universe that was once hot and dense can.

Practice problems

1. Use Wien's law $\lambda_{\max}=b/T$ with $b=2.898\times10^{-3}$ m·K to find the peak wavelength of the CMB at $T=2.725$ K.

Show solution
  1. Divide. $\lambda_{\max}=\dfrac{2.898\times10^{-3}}{2.725}=1.06\times10^{-3}$ m.
  2. Convert. $1.06\times10^{-3}$ m $=1.06$ mm.
Answer: $\lambda_{\max}\approx1.06$ mm — a microwave, which is why it is called the cosmic microwave background.

2. The CMB was released when the universe was about $3000$ K. Using $T(z)=T_0(1+z)$ with $T_0=2.725$ K, find the redshift of that surface.

Show solution
  1. Rearrange. $1+z=T/T_0=3000/2.725$.
  2. Evaluate. $1+z=1101\Rightarrow z\approx1100$.
Answer: $z\approx1100$ — the light has been stretched by a factor of about 1100 since it was set free, cooling from 3000 K to 2.725 K.

3. A microwave photon at the CMB peak has frequency $\nu=160$ GHz. Find its energy in electronvolts. Use $E=h\nu$, $h=4.14\times10^{-15}$ eV·s.

Show solution
  1. Multiply. $E=(4.14\times10^{-15})(1.60\times10^{11})$.
  2. Evaluate. $E=6.6\times10^{-4}$ eV.
Answer: $E\approx0.66$ meV — a thousand times feebler than visible light, which is why the CMB is invisibly faint and cold.
Chapter 2 · The discovery · 1948 – 1965 · Alpher's prediction, Penzias & Wilson's accident

Predicted on paper, then found as unexplainable noise

Ralph Alpher's 1948 prediction · the Bell Labs antenna that hissed from every direction · what a plasma is and why it matters

Predicted in 1948

The American cosmologist Ralph Alpher first predicted the cosmic microwave background in 1948. If the early universe was extremely hot and dense, he reasoned, then as it expanded and cooled, a bath of relic radiation must have been released — and it should still be detectable today, redshifted into a faint, cold glow. The prediction sat largely unnoticed for nearly two decades.

Found by accident

In 1965 two researchers at Bell Telephone Laboratories, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, were building a sensitive radio receiver and were puzzled by a persistent background hiss they could not remove. They cleaned the antenna, even evicted nesting pigeons — and the noise stayed. They soon realised it came uniformly from all over the sky.

The same in every direction

That uniformity was the decisive clue. Noise from a city, the Sun, or the galaxy would point somewhere; this pointed nowhere because it came from everywhere. An isotropic signal — identical in all directions — is the signature of something filling all of space, not a source sitting in it. Penzias and Wilson had stumbled onto Alpher's predicted afterglow.

A glimpse of the primordial plasma

Observing the sky at millimetre to sub-millimetre wavelengths lets us see the early universe's plasma just as it vanished — at the instant it cooled into neutral gas. A plasma is matter so hot that electrons are torn free of their nuclei; the Sun is a great gravitationally-bound ball of it. The CMB is the last light of the cosmic plasma, the moment before it switched off.

“We have observed an excess antenna temperature… essentially isotropic, unpolarized, and free from seasonal variations.”

— A. A. Penzias & R. W. Wilson, A Measurement of Excess Antenna Temperature at 4080 Mc/s, Astrophysical Journal, 1965. The understated sentence that announced the afterglow of the Big Bang and later won a Nobel Prize.

Point the horn antenna — the hiss never goes away

Penzias and Wilson's puzzle in one control. Swing the Bell-Labs horn antenna to any patch of sky and read its excess temperature. A local source — the galaxy, the Sun — would spike in one direction and vanish elsewhere. The CMB does not: it reads the same everywhere, the hallmark of an isotropic signal filling all of space. (Crank the precision and a tiny part-in-a-thousand variation appears — the dipole of Chapter 7 — but the zeroth-order story is sameness.)

∴ Why "the same in all directions" means "fills all of space"

The logic that turned an annoying hiss into a discovery.

  1. Sources have locations. Any object in space — a transmitter, a star, the Milky Way's disk — subtends some direction. Aim away from it and its signal drops. Direction-dependence is the fingerprint of a localized source.
  2. The hiss had none. Penzias and Wilson found the excess temperature was the same toward every patch of sky, day and night, season to season. No pointing made it stronger or weaker.
  3. Only a filled volume does that. A signal coming equally from all directions must be emitted by — or fill — the whole volume around the observer. Redshifted to microwaves and thermal in shape, that volume-filling glow is exactly Alpher's predicted relic.
The verdict: isotropy promoted the noise from "instrument problem" to "the oldest light in the universe." The faint anisotropies — Chapters 4–8 — are the deviations from this sameness, and they carry nearly all the cosmological information.

What a plasma is, and why the CMB is its farewell

A plasma is an ionised gas: electrons dissociated from nuclei, free to roam. Plasmas are hot — it is thermal energy that keeps the negative electrons from binding to the positive nuclei. Free electrons scatter light fiercely, so a plasma is opaque. The early universe was one vast plasma, and the CMB is the radiation that escaped at the very moment it cooled enough for electrons and nuclei to combine into transparent neutral gas. The next chapter watches that switch flip.

Practice problems

1. Penzias and Wilson worked at $4080$ MHz. What wavelength is that? Use $\lambda=c/\nu$, $c=3.00\times10^8$ m/s.

Show solution
  1. Divide. $\lambda=\dfrac{3.00\times10^8}{4.080\times10^9}$.
  2. Evaluate. $\lambda=0.0735$ m $=7.35$ cm.
Answer: $\lambda\approx7.4$ cm — a radio/microwave wavelength on the low-frequency tail of the CMB blackbody curve, where the antenna was sensitive.

2. Alpher reasoned the relic radiation would be redshifted by expansion. If it was released at $3000$ K and we observe $2.725$ K, by what factor have its wavelengths stretched?

Show solution
  1. Take the ratio. stretch $=T_{\text{emit}}/T_{\text{obs}}=3000/2.725$.
  2. Evaluate. $\approx1100$.
Answer: a factor of about $1100$ — the same $(1+z)$ that turns 3000 K into 2.725 K, confirming Alpher's "hot start, then cool" picture.

3. Explain, in one or two sentences, why a uniform hiss from all directions could not be coming from the Milky Way's disk.

Show solution
  1. The disk is a structure with a shape. Its emission is concentrated along a band on the sky (the Milky Way we see at night), so a receiver pointed toward the band reads more than one pointed at a galactic pole.
  2. The CMB shows no such band. Equal signal in every direction rules out any structure embedded in space.
Answer: Galactic emission is strongly direction-dependent; the CMB's perfect isotropy means it fills space rather than sitting within it.
Chapter 3 · The first 380,000 years · Recombination & the surface of last scattering

The fog clears, and the universe lets its light go

The opaque primordial plasma · electrons binding to nuclei · the universe turning transparent · and the shell of last scattering we still see

An opaque, foggy plasma

The primordial plasma was a soup of electrons, hydrogen and helium nuclei, a trace of other light nuclei, and photons — all at once. Photons could not travel far: they were constantly scattered off the free electrons, ricocheting like sunlight inside a dense fog. The universe was opaque. You could not have seen across the room, let alone across the cosmos.

Cooling lets matter neutralise

As the universe expanded it cooled, and eventually it cooled enough that electrons could bind with the protons and helium nuclei. This is recombination: the charged plasma transforms into a neutral gas of hydrogen and helium atoms. With the free electrons mopped up, there is suddenly almost nothing left to scatter the photons.

The universe turns transparent

At that moment — about 380,000 years after the Big Bang — the fog lifts. The thermal photons stop ricocheting and begin to free-stream across the universe in straight lines, hardly touching matter again. This switch from opaque to transparent is called decoupling: light and matter go their separate ways.

A shell of last scattering around us

The photons that left the right distance away, headed our way, are arriving now. They paint an image of the universe at that transition — a thin spherical shell around us, the surface of last scattering, some 46 billion light-years off in comoving distance. Mapping the CMB is looking back in time to the instant the universe became clear, and no further: it is a wall of light we cannot see behind.

∴ Why recombination waits until 3000 K, not 150,000 K

Hydrogen's binding energy is $13.6$ eV, which corresponds to a temperature of $\sim158{,}000$ K. So why does the gas stay ionised until it is fifty times cooler?

  1. Photons hugely outnumber atoms. There are roughly two billion CMB photons for every baryon. Even when the average photon is too feeble to ionise hydrogen, the rare energetic photons in the blackbody tail are still numerous enough to keep tearing atoms apart.
  2. The tail sets the schedule. Recombination only completes once even those tail photons are too few — which the Saha equation places at $T\approx3000$ K, redshift $z\approx1100$, not at the naïve $158{,}000$ K.
  3. Then it runs fast. Once it tips, the free-electron fraction $x_e$ plummets, the scattering rate crashes, and within a relatively thin shell of redshift the universe goes from opaque to transparent — giving the CMB a sharp surface of last scattering rather than a gradual haze.
The yield: the enormous photon-to-baryon ratio is why the CMB was released at a cosy 3000 K — cool enough that its redshifted light lands in the microwave today, and sharp enough that it carries a crisp snapshot of the infant universe.

A wall of light, 13.8 billion years deep

Because the universe was opaque before decoupling, no light reaches us from earlier than the surface of last scattering — it is the most distant thing any telescope can ever see in light. Everything beyond it is hidden behind the cosmic fog. To probe earlier still, cosmologists must turn to neutrinos or gravitational waves, which the plasma did not stop. The CMB is the universe's baby photograph, taken at 380,000 years, and the faint blotches on it — Chapter 4 — are the seeds that grew into every galaxy.

Practice problems

1. Recombination occurs near $z\approx1100$. If the universe is $13.8$ Gyr old and recombination was at $380{,}000$ yr, what fraction of cosmic history had passed when the CMB was released?

Show solution
  1. Form the ratio. $\dfrac{3.8\times10^5}{1.38\times10^{10}}$.
  2. Evaluate. $\approx2.8\times10^{-5}$.
Answer: about $0.0028\%$ — the CMB snapshot was taken when the universe was a newborn, less than three hundred-thousandths of its present age.

2. Hydrogen's ionisation energy is $13.6$ eV. Convert that to a temperature using $E=kT$ with $k=8.62\times10^{-5}$ eV/K.

Show solution
  1. Rearrange. $T=E/k=13.6/(8.62\times10^{-5})$.
  2. Evaluate. $T\approx1.58\times10^{5}$ K.
Answer: $\sim158{,}000$ K — yet recombination waits until $\sim3000$ K, fifty times cooler, because photons outnumber baryons two-billion-to-one.

3. The surface of last scattering lies at comoving distance $\sim46$ billion light-years, yet the light is only $13.8$ billion years old. Why is the distance larger than the light-travel time?

Show solution
  1. Space expanded during the journey. The photon set out when the universe was tiny; as it travelled, expansion carried its starting point ever farther away.
  2. Comoving distance accounts for that stretch. It measures where the emitting matter is now, not how far the light went.
Answer: the $46$ Gly comoving distance reflects 13.8 Gyr of expansion stretching the space the light crossed; light-travel time alone would undercount it.
Chapter 4 · Mapping the sky · 1989 – 2018 · COBE, WMAP & Planck

From a uniform glow to a map of the infant universe

Why the tiny fluctuations matter · COBE's first full-sky map · Planck's exquisite picture · the anomalies · and the case for dark matter and dark energy

Uniform — but not perfectly

The CMB is at a strikingly uniform temperature, the same to about one part in a hundred thousand across the whole sky. But it is not exactly uniform. With precise enough telescopes, faint fluctuations appear — patches a few microkelvin hotter or colder than average. Those anisotropies are where all the cosmology lives.

The fluctuations are the seeds

By studying these fluctuations, cosmologists learn how the early universe was built. The hot and cold patches mark where matter was very slightly denser or sparser at last scattering — the seeds that gravity later grew into galaxies and the vast cosmic web. Reading them measures the basic parameters of the Big Bang theory itself.

COBE, then ever sharper eyes

Portions of the CMB were mapped in the decades after 1965, but the first space-based full-sky map came from NASA's Cosmic Background Explorer, COBE, launched in 1989 — which first resolved the fluctuations. WMAP sharpened the picture; then in 2013 the European Space Agency's Planck telescope released the highest-precision map yet, down to a few arcminutes.

Evidence for the dark sector

Planck also confirmed puzzles WMAP had seen — unexpected large-scale asymmetries and a mysterious cold spot. And its final 2018 release sharpened the evidence that dark matter and dark energy — the unseen mass and the force driving cosmic acceleration — really do exist, by pinning down the heights of the fluctuation peaks. That weighing is the subject of the rest of this companion.

∴ The three layers of the CMB sky, by amplitude

The map is a stack of signals a thousand-fold apart in size — which is why each had to be peeled off in turn.

  1. Monopole — $2.725$ K. The average temperature over the whole sphere. It is the entire signal to one part in a thousand, and it is what Penzias and Wilson measured.
  2. Dipole — $\sim3.4$ mK ($10^{-3}$). A single hot-cold modulation from our own motion through the CMB: the sky is blueshifted (hotter) ahead of us and redshifted (colder) behind. It is a local Doppler effect, not primordial — so it is subtracted off.
  3. Anisotropies — $\sim30\ \mu$K ($10^{-5}$). What remains after removing monopole and dipole: the true primordial pattern. COBE detected it in 1992; resolving its fine structure took WMAP and Planck. These ripples, and only these, encode the contents of the universe.
The yield: the cosmologically precious signal is ten thousand times fainter than the foreground it hides under. Extracting it — and then reducing it to a single curve, the power spectrum — is what Chapters 5–8 are about.

Why a map is not the prediction

Theory cannot predict that this particular patch of sky is hot and that one cold — the pattern is a single random draw from the early universe. What theory predicts are the statistical properties of the map: how much fluctuation there is at each angular scale. So cosmologists do not compare the map pixel-by-pixel to a model; they compress it into a curve of "roughness versus scale" — the power spectrum — and compare that. The next chapter builds exactly that idea.

Practice problems

1. The CMB anisotropies are about $\Delta T/T\sim10^{-5}$. With $T=2.725$ K, what temperature fluctuation $\Delta T$ does that correspond to, in microkelvin?

Show solution
  1. Multiply. $\Delta T=10^{-5}\times2.725$ K $=2.7\times10^{-5}$ K.
  2. Convert. $=27\ \mu$K.
Answer: $\Delta T\approx27\ \mu$K — a few tens of microkelvin, the scale of the ripples Planck mapped.

2. The dipole is $\Delta T/T\approx1.23\times10^{-3}$. Show this implies a Solar-System speed of about $370$ km/s. Use $\Delta T/T=v/c$, $c=3.00\times10^5$ km/s.

  1. Solve for $v$. $v=(\Delta T/T)\,c=1.23\times10^{-3}\times3.00\times10^5$.
  2. Evaluate. $v=369$ km/s.
Answer: $v\approx370$ km/s — our motion relative to the CMB rest frame, read straight from the dipole's amplitude.

3. COBE resolved features about $7°$ across; Planck reached about $5'$ (arcminutes). By what factor did the angular resolution improve?

Show solution
  1. Put both in arcminutes. $7°=420'$; Planck $=5'$.
  2. Divide. $420/5=84$.
Answer: about $80\times$ finer — enough to resolve not just the first peak of the power spectrum but a whole series of them.
Chapter 5 · The power curve · Roughness as a function of scale

How rough is the sky? It depends how closely you look

Why theory predicts statistics, not maps · the ocean analogy · the multipole moment · and the power spectrum as a curve of roughness versus angular scale

We measure patterns, not pixels

Our theories do not predict the CMB map itself — they predict its statistical properties. The single most important one is called the power spectrum: a measure of the smoothness or roughness of the temperature map as a function of angular scale. It is the curve every CMB mission is ultimately built to measure.

"Roughness" depends on the scale

What does roughness-versus-scale mean? Picture describing the surface of the Pacific Ocean. Is it smooth or rough? It depends on the length scale. On scales far larger than a typical wave it looks smooth; on the scale of a single wave it is rough; zoom in below the wavelength of a wave and it can look smooth again. Roughness is not one number — it is a curve.

Power on the sky

The CMB power spectrum measures the spatial wavelength of temperature fluctuations across the sky. The scale-dependent amount of fluctuation we call power. Plot power on the vertical axis against angular scale on the horizontal, and the CMB's roughness reveals itself scale by scale — far from featureless, it is a series of distinct bumps.

The multipole axis

Angular scale is labelled by the multipole moment $\ell$: a higher multipole corresponds to a smaller scale. $\ell\approx2$ is the whole-sky variation; $\ell\approx200$ is patches about a degree across; $\ell\approx1000$ is arcminute speckle. Read the power spectrum left to right and you sweep from the largest structures down to the finest, reading off how much fluctuation lives at each.

Tune the sky to one scale — the ocean analogy, live

The same random CMB-like field, band-pass filtered to keep only fluctuations near a chosen multipole moment $\ell$. Slide $\ell$ up and the patches shrink: low $\ell$ is a few broad blobs across the whole sky (smooth, large-scale), high $\ell$ is fine mottling (rough, small-scale). The readout converts $\ell$ to an angular size on the sky, $\theta\approx180°/\ell$ — the wave-by-wave "roughness" the power spectrum catalogues.

∴ From multipole to angle, and the ocean made precise

One conversion turns the abstract axis into something you can point at in the sky.

  1. The multipole is an inverse angle. A pattern that wraps around the sky $\ell$ times has features about $\theta\approx180°/\ell$ across. So $\ell=2$ is $\sim90°$ (whole-sky), $\ell=200$ is $\sim0.9°$ (about a degree), $\ell=1000$ is $\sim0.18°$ (a tenth of a degree).
  2. Power is variance per scale. Decompose the map into patterns of every size; the power $C_\ell$ is the mean-square amplitude of the patterns at multipole $\ell$. Large $C_\ell$ means the sky is rough at that scale; small $C_\ell$ means smooth.
  3. The ocean, formalised. "Smooth on large scales, rough at the wave scale, smooth again below it" is just a power spectrum with little power at small $\ell$, a peak at the wave's $\ell$, and falling power at high $\ell$ — exactly the shape the CMB turns out to have, with its peak near $\ell\approx220$.
The yield: a single curve, $C_\ell$ versus $\ell$, replaces the map without losing any of its cosmological content — because the early universe's fluctuations were random, fully described by how much power sits at each scale. The job of Chapters 6–7 is to explain how a map is turned into that curve.

Why compress a map into a curve at all?

A Planck temperature map has roughly fifty million pixels. No theory predicts those fifty million numbers — they are one random realisation. But every viable theory predicts the power spectrum, a few thousand numbers $C_\ell$, with exquisite precision. Comparing the measured $C_\ell$ to the predicted $C_\ell$ is how the contents of the universe are weighed. The compression from map to curve is the act of Fourier analysis on a sphere — which is why we now need the Fourier transform, and then its spherical cousin.

Practice problems

1. The first acoustic peak sits near $\ell=220$. Using $\theta\approx180°/\ell$, what angular scale is that?

Show solution
  1. Divide. $\theta=180/220$.
  2. Evaluate. $\theta\approx0.82°$.
Answer: about $0.8°$ — a touch larger than the full Moon's $0.5°$. The dominant CMB hot/cold patches are roughly Moon-sized on the sky.

2. The full Moon spans about $0.5°$. What multipole does that angular scale correspond to?

Show solution
  1. Invert. $\ell\approx180°/\theta=180/0.5$.
  2. Evaluate. $\ell\approx360$.
Answer: $\ell\approx360$ — between the first and second acoustic peaks of the CMB power spectrum.

3. In the ocean analogy, why does the surface look "smooth again" on scales much smaller than a wave?

Show solution
  1. Most power lives at the wave scale. The water's height varies most over a wavelength; that is where the spectrum peaks.
  2. Below it, little varies. Within a patch far smaller than a wave, the surface is nearly flat, so there is little power at those small scales.
Answer: roughness is concentrated near the wave's own scale; far above and far below it, the power spectrum is low, so the surface reads as smooth — exactly the bump-shaped curve the CMB also shows.
Chapter 6 · The winding machine · Decomposing a mixed signal

Wind a signal around a circle and its frequencies fall out

The Fourier transform problem · winding a signal around a circle · the "centre of mass" trick · and why peaks appear at the component frequencies

The decomposition problem

The defining problem of the Fourier transform is to take a mixed signal — several pure tones added together — and recover the individual frequencies it is built from. The CMB sky is just such a mixture: hot and cold patches of many sizes superimposed. To read off "how much power at each scale," we first need a machine that separates a blend into its parts.

Wind it around a circle

Here is the trick. Take the signal's graph and wind it around a circle, so that a high point of the signal sits far from the origin and a low point sits close in. You control one knob: how fast the signal winds around the circle — the winding frequency. For most winding speeds the wound-up graph is a smudge balanced symmetrically about the centre.

Watch the centre of mass

Now track the centre of mass of that wound-up graph. There are two rates in play: the signal's own frequency, and the winding frequency. When the winding frequency equals a frequency present in the signal, the bumps all pile up on one side, and the centre of mass lurches far from the origin. Off-resonance, it sits near zero.

Peaks reveal the recipe

So sweep the winding frequency and plot how far the centre of mass strays from the origin. You get a flat baseline with sharp peaks — one at each frequency hiding in the signal. The Fourier transform is exactly this centre-of-mass measurement, and its peaks read out the recipe of the mixture. That is how a tangled CMB map is sorted into power at each scale.

Turn the winding knob — find the hidden frequencies

Below right is the payoff: the distance of the centre of mass from the origin, swept across all winding frequencies. Drag the knob and watch the dot swing wide exactly when you hit a frequency baked into the signal — the spikes in the right-hand plot. That measurement is the Fourier transform.

∴ The winding machine, written as an integral

The picture is literally the Fourier transform; here is the same thing in symbols.

  1. Winding is multiplication by a rotating phasor. Winding the signal $g(t)$ around the origin at winding frequency $f$ means forming $g(t)\,e^{-2\pi i f t}$ — a point in the plane that spins at rate $f$ with radius $g(t)$.
  2. Centre of mass is an average. The centre of mass of the wound graph over a duration is $\hat g(f)=\dfrac{1}{T}\displaystyle\int_0^T g(t)\,e^{-2\pi i f t}\,dt$ — exactly the (finite-window) Fourier transform.
  3. Resonance makes it large. If $g$ contains a tone $\cos(2\pi f_0 t)$, then at $f=f_0$ the rotation cancels that tone's own spin, the contributions stop averaging away, and $|\hat g(f)|$ spikes. Away from $f_0$ the phasor washes the contribution out to nearly zero.
The yield: $|\hat g(f)|$ peaks at every frequency present in $g$ — the recipe of the mixture, recovered. On the sky the analogue of "frequency" is angular scale, and the analogue of $|\hat g(f)|^2$ is the power $C_\ell$. The only change for the CMB is that the signal lives on a sphere, not a line.

From the line to the sphere

The winding machine decomposes a signal on a line into sine waves. The CMB is a signal on the surface of a sphere, so its natural building blocks are not sine waves but spherical harmonics — the sphere's version of the Fourier basis. The centre-of-mass idea carries straight over: project the map onto each spherical harmonic, and the amount of each one present plays the role of $\hat g(f)$. The multipole $\ell$ is the spherical analogue of frequency. The next chapter climbs that ladder, starting from the simplest harmonic of all.

Practice problems

1. A signal is $g(t)=\cos(2\pi\cdot2\,t)+\cos(2\pi\cdot3.5\,t)$. At which winding frequencies will the centre-of-mass plot show peaks?

Show solution
  1. Read the tones. The signal is a sum of $2$ Hz and $3.5$ Hz components.
  2. Peaks at the tones. The winding machine resonates when the winding frequency matches each.
Answer: peaks at $f=2$ Hz and $f=3.5$ Hz (plus a peak at $f=0$ from any constant offset).

2. Why does a constant offset (a "DC" term) in the signal produce a peak at winding frequency $f=0$?

Show solution
  1. At $f=0$ there is no winding. $e^{-2\pi i\cdot0\cdot t}=1$, so the wound graph is just the signal itself, all on one side if it has a nonzero average.
  2. Its centre of mass is the mean. A nonzero average puts the centre of mass off the origin even with no rotation.
Answer: a constant is a "frequency-zero" component, so it shows up as a peak at $f=0$ — on the CMB this is the monopole, the mean temperature.

3. In words, what plays the role of "frequency" when the Fourier idea is moved from a line to the sphere of the sky?

Show solution
  1. The basis changes. Sine waves become spherical harmonics.
  2. The index changes. The continuous frequency $f$ becomes the discrete multipole moment $\ell$.
Answer: the multipole moment $\ell$ is the spherical stand-in for frequency; higher $\ell$ means finer angular features, just as higher $f$ means faster oscillation.
Chapter 7 · Fourier on a sphere · Monopole, dipole, and up the ladder

The sky's building blocks: a uniform glow, a hot-cold split, then finer patterns

Spherical harmonics as the Fourier basis on a sphere · the monopole average · the dipole from our motion · and how the rungs build the power spectrum

A signal on a sphere

The CMB is a temperature defined over the whole celestial sphere — a mixed signal, like the ocean, but wrapped on a ball. To Fourier-analyse it we use spherical harmonics: the three-dimensional counterpart of the one-dimensional sine-and-cosine basis. Each harmonic is a standing pattern of hot and cold lobes on the sphere, labelled by a multipole $\ell$.

The monopole: one number for the whole sky

The simplest harmonic, $\ell=0$, is uniform over the entire sphere. The quantity averaged over the whole sky we call the monopole — and for the CMB it is just the mean temperature, $2.725$ K. It carries no pattern, only the overall level. To see structure we subtract it off.

The dipole: our own motion

Subtract the average and the next thing that remains is the dipole, $\ell=1$: one hemisphere hot, the opposite cold. It comes from our motion. Radiation in the direction we travel is blueshifted and so appears hotter; radiation behind us is redshifted and colder. The dipole is a Doppler effect of the Solar System's roughly $370$ km/s drift, not a feature of the early universe — so it too is removed.

Up the ladder to the power spectrum

Beyond the dipole come the quadrupole ($\ell=2$), octupole, and ever-finer harmonics — more and smaller hot-cold patches as $\ell$ climbs. Each multipole's total strength, averaged over its patterns, is the power $C_\ell$. Stack those numbers up across $\ell$ and you have rebuilt the power spectrum of Chapter 5 — the harmonic-by-harmonic recipe of the sky.

Climb the multipole ladder — one harmonic at a time

Each setting shows a single spherical harmonic pattern on the oval sky. $\ell=0$ is the uniform monopole (the mean temperature); $\ell=1$ is the dipole, one hot lobe and one cold; $\ell=2$ the quadrupole, and so on. As $\ell$ rises the sky breaks into more and smaller patches — roughly $\ell$ hot-cold cycles around the sphere — which is why high $\ell$ probes small angular scales. Pause on $\ell=1$ to see the motion-induced dipole called out.

∴ The dipole's size from a 370 km/s drift

The keynote's worked case — turning our motion into the millikelvin dipole the maps must remove.

  1. Doppler shift of a thermal sky. Moving at speed $v$ through blackbody radiation tilts its temperature by direction: $\dfrac{\Delta T}{T}=\dfrac{v}{c}\cos\theta$, hottest dead ahead ($\theta=0$), coldest dead behind.
  2. Insert the speed. With $v\approx370$ km/s and $c=3.00\times10^5$ km/s, the amplitude is $\dfrac{v}{c}=1.23\times10^{-3}$.
  3. Convert to temperature. $\Delta T=\dfrac{v}{c}\,T=1.23\times10^{-3}\times2.725\ \text{K}=3.4\times10^{-3}$ K $=3.4$ mK.
The result: a $3.4$ mK dipole — a hundred times larger than the primordial anisotropies, and entirely due to our own velocity. Because it is local, not cosmological, it is the $\ell=1$ term that gets subtracted before the real CMB pattern is studied. Hotter ahead, colder behind: a sky-sized speedometer.

The coefficients $a_{\ell m}$ and the spectrum $C_\ell$

Expanding the map in spherical harmonics gives a set of coefficients $a_{\ell m}$ — one centre-of-mass number for each harmonic, exactly as $\hat g(f)$ was for the line. The power spectrum is their mean square at each multipole, $C_\ell=\langle|a_{\ell m}|^2\rangle$, averaged over the $2\ell+1$ patterns at that $\ell$. That averaging is why theory can predict $C_\ell$ even though it cannot predict any single $a_{\ell m}$: the individual patterns are random, but their typical strength is fixed by physics. And the shape of $C_\ell$ — its peaks — is set by sound waves in the primordial plasma, the subject of the final chapter.

Practice problems

1. Reproduce the dipole amplitude. With $v=370$ km/s, $c=3.00\times10^5$ km/s and $T=2.725$ K, find $\Delta T$ in millikelvin.

Show solution
  1. Fractional shift. $v/c=370/3.00\times10^5=1.23\times10^{-3}$.
  2. Times $T$. $\Delta T=1.23\times10^{-3}\times2.725=3.4\times10^{-3}$ K.
Answer: $\Delta T\approx3.4$ mK — the motion-induced dipole, ~100× the primordial ripples.

2. The quadrupole is $\ell=2$. Roughly what angular scale does it correspond to, and how does that compare to the dipole?

Show solution
  1. Use $\theta\approx180°/\ell$. For $\ell=2$, $\theta\approx90°$.
  2. Compare to dipole. The dipole ($\ell=1$) is $\sim180°$, the whole sky split in two; the quadrupole halves that scale.
Answer: $\ell=2$ is $\sim90°$-scale structure — four lobes around the sky, half the angular size of the dipole's two.

3. Why can cosmologists predict $C_\ell$ but not the value of any individual coefficient $a_{\ell m}$?

Show solution
  1. Each $a_{\ell m}$ is a random draw. The early universe seeded one particular realisation we cannot predict in detail.
  2. Their variance is fixed by physics. The typical squared amplitude at each $\ell$, $C_\ell=\langle|a_{\ell m}|^2\rangle$, is set by the cosmological model.
Answer: the map is a random realisation, so individual coefficients are unpredictable, but their statistical spread — the power spectrum — is exactly what theory predicts and observation measures.
Chapter 8 · The payoff · Acoustic peaks and the case for dark matter

The peaks weigh the universe — and most of its matter is dark

Sound waves in the primordial plasma · the harmonic peaks · how peak heights separate ordinary matter from dark matter · the keynote's central claim

The plasma rang like a drum

Before recombination, the photon–baryon plasma was springy: gravity pulled matter into dense spots, photon pressure pushed back, and the competition set up acoustic oscillations — real sound waves sloshing through the early universe. When the plasma neutralised, those waves froze in place, leaving a pattern of hot and cold imprinted at special scales.

Frozen waves make peaks

Regions caught at maximum compression or maximum rarefaction at the instant of last scattering show the strongest temperature contrast. These special scales appear as a series of peaks in the power spectrum — harmonics of a fundamental, like the overtones of a struck drum. The first and tallest peak sits near $\ell\approx220$, about one degree on the sky.

The peaks are a balance sheet

The exact positions and heights of those peaks encode the contents of the universe. The first peak's position fixes the geometry (space is flat). The relative heights of the odd and even peaks weigh the ordinary baryons. And the third peak, with the overall envelope, is sensitive to something that has mass and gravity but no pressure — dark matter.

Most of the matter is invisible

Only a model with several times more dark matter than ordinary matter reproduces the measured peaks. Fitting Planck's power spectrum gives roughly five-sixths of all matter as dark — invisible stuff that neither emits nor absorbs light, felt only through gravity. The power curve is the keynote's headline evidence: the CMB doesn't just suggest dark matter, it weighs it.

∴ Why the peaks demand dark matter

Three readings of the curve, each weighing a different ingredient.

  1. First-peak position → flat geometry. The sound waves all travel the same distance (the sound horizon) before freezing, setting one physical size. Its angular size on the sky, observed at $\ell\approx220$, only works out if space is geometrically flat — fixing the total energy density to the critical value.
  2. Odd-vs-even peak heights → baryons. Baryons add inertia to the oscillating plasma, deepening the compressions (odd peaks: 1st, 3rd) relative to the rarefactions (even peaks: 2nd). The measured first-to-second peak ratio pins the baryon density at $\Omega_b h^2\approx0.022$ — only about $5\%$ of the critical density.
  3. Third peak & envelope → dark matter. Dark matter has gravity but no pressure, so it doesn't oscillate — it builds potential wells the photons fall into, boosting the third peak and steadying the whole pattern. Matching the third peak requires $\Omega_c h^2\approx0.12$, several times the baryons.
The verdict: flat geometry needs $\Omega\approx1$, but baryons supply only $\sim5\%$ and all the glowing matter even less. The peaks force a large, pressureless, invisible component — dark matter at $\sim27\%$ — with the rest, dark energy, making up the balance. The power curve is a scale that reads out the dark universe.

What "evidence for dark matter" means here

The CMB argument for dark matter is independent of galaxy rotation curves or gravitational lensing — it comes purely from the heights of the acoustic peaks at redshift $1100$, long before galaxies existed. The same fit also requires dark energy to make the geometry flat while keeping the matter density low, which is why Planck's 2018 release is quoted as evidence for both. A universe of only the atoms we can see would show the wrong peaks; the sky we actually measure is about $5\%$ ordinary matter, $27\%$ dark matter, $68\%$ dark energy. The power curve is how those numbers are weighed.

Practice problems

1. Planck finds $\Omega_b h^2\approx0.022$ for baryons and $\Omega_c h^2\approx0.12$ for dark matter. What fraction of the total matter is dark?

Show solution
  1. Total matter. $\Omega_m h^2=0.022+0.12=0.142$.
  2. Dark fraction. $0.12/0.142=0.85$.
Answer: about $85\%$ of the matter is dark — roughly five-sixths, with ordinary atoms a small minority.

2. The first acoustic peak is observed at $\ell\approx220$, corresponding to $\sim1°$. What would it mean for the geometry if the peak instead appeared at a much smaller scale (higher $\ell$)?

Show solution
  1. The physical size is fixed. The sound horizon at last scattering is a known length.
  2. Angle depends on geometry. In a negatively-curved (open) universe, a fixed length subtends a smaller angle, pushing the peak to higher $\ell$.
Answer: a peak at higher $\ell$ would signal an open, negatively-curved universe. Its observed position at $\ell\approx220$ instead indicates flat geometry, $\Omega\approx1$.

3. In one or two sentences, why does dark matter affect the peaks differently from ordinary baryonic matter?

Show solution
  1. Baryons feel photon pressure. They are coupled to the radiation, so they oscillate — loading the compressions and shaping odd-vs-even peak heights.
  2. Dark matter does not. Pressureless, it cannot oscillate; it only provides gravity, deepening wells and boosting the third and higher peaks.
Answer: baryons ring with the photons (setting peak ratios); dark matter only gravitates (setting the envelope and third peak) — so the two are separable from the curve, which is what makes the dark matter measurable.
